# Chapter 19: Her Heartbeat

The prosecutor was bought. For about a minute, I forgot how to breathe.

I'll be honest about that minute. It's the kind of thing a man doesn't admit later, when he wants to have been brave the whole way through. I wasn't.

The system had laid the last truth at my feet. The one door was rotten. The one clean man was dirty. There was no door.

I sat in the dark. Hands flat on the desk. I felt the whole beautiful structure I'd built through the night sway like a tower in the wind.

A whole night sealing the truth. Thread by thread. Slowly, carefully, the way Clara would have demanded. Every piece of evidence sealed twice so no lawyer on earth could cry forgery. And the only person allowed to carry that truth into the light had taken their money to keep it buried.

I'd built a perfect key. No lock would take it.

The Brussels apartment had the smell of those nights. Cold coffee in three cups. The dry heat of machines that have run too long. That warm smell of electronics and burnt dust that none of us ever forget.

The only light came from the screens. It's always like that, in my world. You learn to love that light. Blue, cold, faithful. The only light that judges no one.

And under the panic, lower down, in the old basement where the wild man never quite sleeps, a voice spoke. Very calm. *Then burn him too. Burn them all. You don't need a prosecutor to start a fire. You know how. You've done it before.*

I didn't answer it. You don't argue with that voice. You don't feed it, that's all. Like you don't argue with a dog that has decided to love you вҖ” you just walk into another room.

It was the voice of the old me. The one who forced the world's locks. For money, for principle, for the rush. Too often to impress a woman. The one who knew the flaws you don't sell. I'd buried him. He always surfaces at dawn, when you're too tired to hold the lid down.

So I stood up. Went to the window. Marseille was nearly a thousand kilometres to the south, somewhere beyond the curve of darkness. Clara was there. They were bleeding her dry, hour by hour. I'd spent the night building a weapon I couldn't fire. And the sun was rising, grey, over a city full of people who'd had the right to sleep.

I was so deep inside my own despair that I almost missed it.

The system changed its breathing.

That's the only way I know how to say it. The tool I'd built had a breathing. A steady background hum made of all the small things it watched for me. Six days in, I'd learned its rhythm in my body. The way you feel the engine of a familiar car.

And behind me, while I was hating the dawn, the rhythm changed.

It had found something.

Something it had been watching for, in the dark, for six days, without once telling me. Because I hadn't asked it to hope. So it hadn't.

I turned around. Slowly. The way you turn when you've heard a noise in an empty house and you're not ready yet to know what it is.

---

I have to say what I'd done, days before. What I'd half forgotten.

Early in the week. When I still had hope to spend. Before the marker games, the rotten prosecutor, the slow grinding loss of every clean idea. I'd asked the system for something that, even as I asked it, felt like a prayer dressed up as a task.

Three in the morning. The way you do things you don't admit to yourself. In the silence where there's no one left to watch a hard man go tender and broken.

I had given it everything that was *hers.*

Not her face. Not her phone. Those were gone, scrubbed, dark. A clean kill switch. Not a crumb in a dump, not an echo on a forum. The kind of disappearance only a professional can arrange. And Clara was a professional.

I'd given it the smaller things. The shape of how she moved through the world. The signature of a brilliant, meticulous woman. Her way of leaving a trace without leaving a trace. The habits she couldn't unlearn even with a gun at her back. The tells of a mind that had spent its whole life proving things.

Her particular way of being invisible. Because in our world, the way you hide is as recognizable as a face. We all have a signature. Even when we think we don't. *Especially* when we think we don't.

And I'd said to it, in plain words, alone, feeling like a fool: *If she's alive вҖ” if she's somewhere вҖ” listen for her. Not for where she is. For whether she* is. *Listen to the silence and tell me if her heart is still beating in it.*

I'd asked for proof of life. Not a location. Not a rescue. Just that. The only thing. The thing under all the other things. *Is she still breathing.*

Then I'd let it go. You can't watch something like that without going mad. I buried myself in the routes, the money, the twelve faces, the rotten door. In the forums where you whisper the names you never write in plain text. In the dark-web channels where aid convoys sell like freight and children appear as a line on an invoice.

And underneath all of it, the system kept listening. Patiently. Without hope, because it has none. Without despair, for the same reason. Listening to the silence where Clara should have been. Like a stethoscope held against a chest. Waiting.

For six days, the silence had stayed silent.

And now, at dawn, with the worst news of my life still warm at my feet, it had heard something move in the dark.

---

I crossed the room so fast I knocked the chair over.

I won't pretend I read it calmly. I fell into the seat like a man falling into water. I stared at what the system was showing me. For a second my eyes refused to make sense of it. The way you can't read a word you've waited your whole life to see.

It wasn't a map. Not a name. Not a face.

It was a line.

One line, across the dark of the screen. Small. Fragile as a thread of smoke. Rising and falling. Rising and falling. In a rhythm I would have known at the bottom of the sea.

The system had taken the faintest signal in the world. Something so thin that for six days even it hadn't known if it was real. It had pulled that signal out of the static, the noise, the lies. Out of all that white noise where we learn to fish for the one truth that counts. It had cleaned it, steadied it. And laid it in front of me.

To anyone else, it was nothing. A handful of bars rising and falling. Noise cleaned by a machine. But those three characters at the bottom вҖ” that small signature that only she knew how to leave вҖ” told me whose rhythm this was. It was her. The system didn't write "maybe." It wrote *vivant.* And it dared to believe it at ninety-two percent.

It took me a moment to understand.

It was a heartbeat.

It was *her* heartbeat.

Clara's heart. Beating. Somewhere in the cruel dark. The thing I'd built had reached across all that distance, found it, and was holding it up to me. Like a man cupping a candle flame in his hands against the wind. Saying, in the only language it had: *There. She's there. She's alive. Look. She's alive.*

I made a sound I'm not proud of and will not describe.

---

I have to say what I was carrying.

For six days I'd done all of it вҖ” the building, the hunting, the sealing of truth, the war with my worst self вҖ” over a hole. Under every clever thing I did all week there was a pit with no bottom. And the name of that pit was: *what if she's already gone.*

What if I was building a monument. What if the whole frantic machine вҖ” six sleepless nights, the case, the redemption вҖ” was something constructed over a grave. Cold coffee and screen glow while my family, scattered across the world, took turns on the channels keeping me awake.

What if Clara had been dead since the second night. What if I was just a man having a long conversation with a ghost.

I hadn't allowed myself to say it out loud. Not to Ifeoma, watching my infrastructure from Lagos, writing me at three in the morning *sleep, idiot*, knowing I wouldn't. Not to Yuri in Kyiv, who sees enemies in every shadow and is almost always right. Not even to the dark.

Because to say it was to make it possible. And I couldn't. I needed her alive the way a drowning man needs the next inch of air. So I'd refused. Stacked the refusal day after day until it became a wall. And built and built and built on top of it. Never once looking down.

And now there was a line on a screen. Rising and falling. And it meant the pit was empty. No grave. A woman, somewhere, with a heart in her chest. Doing the bravest thing a heart can do: keep going when everything around it is built to make it stop.

I pressed my hand flat against the screen. Like an idiot. Like a man who has never seen the thing he made.

I just wanted to be near her.

---

Basti called.

I'd forgotten the phone was even in my hand. Basti. My locksmith, my fixer. The smuggler who crossed to the right side and never looked back, who handles boots and doors and the parts of this world a man can actually touch. He was already in Marseille. Already in position. Already doing the patient, ugly groundwork while I made the truth heavy in Brussels.

In our family, we split the real and the invisible. Me behind the screens. Him in the back streets. Between us, an encrypted line and twenty years of debts that neither of us keeps count of anymore. He has a voice like gravel rolled in a tin can. Behind it, a heart he hides very badly.

"You sound strange," he said, before I'd opened my mouth. "Did you cry? Tell me you cried. My whole week gets better if the hacker cried."

"She's alive."

My voice came out wrong. Thin. A kid's voice.

"Basti. She's alive. I can see it. I can see her heart."

Silence. Then a long slow breath. The sound of a big man sitting down hard on something.

"You're sure." Not a question вҖ” that's how Basti asks things. "Not a guess. Not a hope. You don't get to give me hope, Delta. I have no defences against your hope. You give me hope and I drive into that building tonight whether you say go or not."

"It's not hope." I laughed. It split down the middle. "It's the opposite. Hope is what I've been fighting all week. This is the thing *under* hope. It's real. The system isn't telling me she might be alive. It's telling me she *is.* Right now. As I speak to you. Her heart is beating and I'm watching it. That's a fact, Basti. Not a feeling. A fact."

And that was the whole difference. In our line of work you learn early that everything lies. Brands, friends, governments. Yourself most of all. The only thing that doesn't lie is proof.

Clara told me that the first night, in Las Vegas. The way you hand over a family secret. *Find the genome, prove the truth, and you have the only weapon worth anything in a world of liars.* It became our religion. And there on the screen, in a line that rose and fell, I had my proof.

I heard him swallow. Basti, who cries at films with dogs and swears it's allergies. Who carried a coffin in Marseille two years ago without making a sound. Who now, on the phone, close to a thousand miles away, made one small noise and buried it in a cough.

"Okay," he said. Rough. "Okay. Then she's waiting for us. We don't have a body to find. We have a person. And a person can be brought home." A pause. "You tell Ifeoma yet?"

"You're the first."

"Good." The smile tried to climb back into his voice and failed. "So I'm the one who knew before Lagos. She'll never forgive me. Worth it." Then quieter, all the gravel gone soft: "Bring her home, brother. We've got a heartbeat. It's more than we had an hour ago. It's everything."

He hung up. My family always hangs up before you can thank them. A rule none of us agreed to and all of us keep.

---

I sat with it.

I gave myself one minute. Just to be a man watching the woman he loves be alive. Before becoming the other thing again. The thing that plans. The thing that fights. The thing that solves the small impossible problem of a rotten door and a dying clock.

I let the wall come down. The wall I'd built all week, over the pit. Slowly. Brick by brick. Because there was no pit. There was a heartbeat. And a wall can come down when there's solid ground below.

I thought about the first time I'd felt that heartbeat.

Las Vegas. Two and a half years ago. A hotel room, the air conditioning roaring, the city throwing its colours against the curtains. We'd met two days earlier, at the great gathering of people like us. That convention that empties the world's forums into one desert casino hotel once a year. Thousands of strangers under the neon. Badges with fake names. Dark rooms where you get on stage and show the flaw you found in a lock the world believed was unbreakable. Where you recognize a legend by the way everyone else lowers their voice when she walks in.

She didn't know who I was. That's what finished me.

And later, Clara asleep against my chest. Her ear over my heart. Me awake. Me, who never holds still, who never stays, who dissolves into every feeling and forgets the world вҖ” perfectly still, not to wake her. Feeling her heart through her back where it pressed against me. Thinking: *this. I could do this. I could finally hold still for this one.*

I didn't know I'd lose her. I didn't know I'd have to build a god out of code in seven days to find her again. I only knew her rhythm. The small steady miracle of it. The way it ran a little fast when she was thinking hard, even in her sleep.

The line on the screen rose and fell. Rose and fell. A little fast. She was thinking hard. Even now. Of course she was.

She was the sharpest operator in this whole story. She was inside the thing. And she was *still working.* I could feel it. Her mind running behind that brave, fast heart. Building her own case, drop by drop, in the dark. From inside the very machine they thought held her. Trusting вҖ” I knew this with a certainty that hollowed me вҖ” trusting that I'd find the genome and come.

"I found it." Out loud. To a screen. To a line. To a woman close to a thousand kilometres south who couldn't hear me. "I found your heartbeat, Clara. I found you. Hold on. I'm coming. The right way. I swear to you, the right way, even now, even like this вҖ” I'm coming, and I'm bringing the law, and we're going to *prove* them, every single one, every face вҖ” just *hold on* вҖ”"

And the heartbeat stopped.

---

I would have liked to stay calm.

I didn't. The line went flat. Just flat. Gone. The rise and fall I'd been living inside for the last quarter hour simply ceased. The way a song ceases when someone lifts the needle. And the silence that rose where her heart had been was the loudest silence of my life.

The world tipped. I was on my feet without deciding to stand. I had her name in my mouth and no air behind it.

One second.

Two.

The right-way man, the slow holy builder, the redeemed one вҖ” gone. Only the wild thing left. The thing from the basement. The one that knows the old paths and the doors that shouldn't open. On its feet. Screaming. Ready to reduce the entire earth to ash if that heart didn't start again.

Three seconds. Four.

*Start. Start. Start, Clara, start, you brave impossible woman, you do not get to do this, not now, not after six days, not while I'm watching вҖ” start вҖ”*

Five. Six. Seven.

I was begging. Out loud. To a machine. To a line. To God, in whom I do not believe, and before whom, right then, I was on my knees.

Eight.

The heartbeat stopped for nine seconds.

Then it moved вҖ” faster than any human being could run.
