# Chapter 19: Her Heartbeat

The prosecutor was bought, and for about a minute I forgot how to breathe.

I have to be precise about that minute, because it's the kind of thing a man doesn't admit later, when he's telling the story over wine to people who want him to have been brave the whole way through. I was not brave the whole way through. When the system laid that last truth at my feet — *the one door is rotten, the one clean man is dirty, there is no door* — I sat in the dark of the Brussels apartment, screen-glow on my hands, three empty coffee mugs gone cold at my elbow and the servers humming their low one-note hymn under the desk, and I felt the whole beautiful structure I'd built all night sway like a tower in a wind. A night of sealing the truth thread by thread, of doing it the slow holy way Clara would have demanded, and the only person on earth allowed to *carry* that truth into the light was a man who took their money to keep it buried.

I'd built a perfect key and there was no lock that would have it.

And under the panic, lower down, in the old basement of me where the wild man still lives and never fully sleeps — the one who used to live in the seams, in the locked forums and the back channels, the one who knew exactly how to make a man's whole clean life leak out into the dark and watch him drown in it — a voice said, very reasonably: *So burn him too. Burn them all. You don't need a prosecutor to set a fire. You used to be the fire.*

I didn't answer it. I'd learned by then that there's no arguing with that voice, only the refusal to feed it, the way there's no arguing with a dog that's decided to love a man — only the walk to a different room. So I stood up. I walked to the window. Marseille was eleven hundred kilometres south of me, somewhere past the curve of the dark, and Clara was in it, spending herself, drop by drop, and I had spent the whole night building a weapon I couldn't fire and now the sun was coming up grey over a city full of people who got to sleep through this.

I was so far inside my own despair that I almost didn't hear the system change its breathing.

That's the only way I know how to say it. The tool I'd built had a kind of *breathing*, a steady background hum of all the small things it watched for me in the dark, and over six days I'd learned its rhythm so deeply that I felt it in my body the way a man feels a familiar car's engine, the way a man who's spent his life in screen-lit rooms learns the temper of his own machines, the way a man would know in his sleep if the person beside him stopped breathing. And somewhere behind me, while I stood at the window hating the dawn, the rhythm changed.

It found something.

It found something it had been listening for, in the dark, for six days, without ever once telling me, because I hadn't asked it to hope and so it hadn't.

I turned around very slowly, the way a man turns when he's heard a sound in an empty house and isn't ready yet to know what it is.

---

Here is what I'd done, days before, and half forgotten.

Early in the week — back when I still had hope to spare, back before the marker games and the rotten prosecutor and the slow grinding loss of every clean idea — I had asked the system to do something that, even as I asked it, felt like a prayer dressed up as a task. I had given it everything that was *hers.* Not her face. Not her phone. Those were gone, scrubbed, dark — gone the way people in my world go dark when they decide the surface is too dangerous and they slip beneath it. I gave it the smaller things. The shape of how she moved through the world. The particular signature of a particular brilliant careful woman — the way she'd leave a trace without leaving a trace, the habits she couldn't unlearn even with a gun at her back, the tiny tells of a mind that had spent its whole life proving things.

And I'd said to it, in plain words, alone in the dark of the apartment with the city asleep and the machines awake, feeling like a fool: *If she's alive — if she's anywhere — listen for her. Not for where she is. For whether she* is. *Listen to the silence and tell me if her heart is still in it.*

I'd asked for proof of life. Not a location. Not a rescue. Just the one thing, the only thing, the thing under all the other things. *Is she still breathing.*

And then I'd let it go, because no man can sit and watch a thing like that without going mad, and I'd buried myself in the routes and the money and the twelve faces and the rotten door, and somewhere underneath all of it, down in the seams where the system lives and never sleeps, it had kept listening. Patiently. Without hope, because it doesn't have any. Without despair, for the same reason. Just listening to the silence where Clara was supposed to be, the way a doctor holds a stethoscope to a chest and waits, and waits, and waits.

For six days the silence had stayed silent.

And now, at dawn, with the worst news of my life still warm at my feet, it had heard something move in the dark.

---

I crossed the room so fast I knocked the chair over.

I won't pretend I read it calmly. I fell into the seat like a man falling into water and I stared at what the system was showing me and for a second I genuinely could not make my eyes resolve it, the way a man can't read a word he's been waiting his whole life to read.

It wasn't a map. It wasn't a name. It wasn't a face.

It was a line.

A single line, moving across the dark of the screen — small, faint, fragile as a thread of smoke — rising and falling, rising and falling, in a rhythm I would have known at the bottom of the sea. The system had taken the faintest signal in the world, a thing so quiet that for six days even it couldn't be sure it was real, buried under all the noise and the static and the lies that fill the dark places where the lost go, and it had pulled that signal up out of all that — cleaned it, steadied it, and laid it in front of me as a line on a screen that rose and fell.

It took me a moment to understand what I was looking at.

It was a heartbeat.

It was *her* heartbeat.

It was Clara's heart, beating, somewhere out there in the cruel dark, and the thing I'd built had reached into all that distance and found it and was holding it up to me like a man cupping a candle flame in two hands against the wind, saying, in the only language it had: *Here. She's here. She's alive. Look — she's alive.*

I made a sound I'm not proud of. It has no name and never needed one.

---

I have to be honest about what I had been carrying.

For six days I'd been doing all of it — the building, the hunting, the sealing of truth, the war with my own worst self — on top of a hole. Underneath every clever thing I did all week there had been a pit with no bottom, and the name of the pit was *what if she's already gone.* What if I was building a monument. What if the whole frantic beautiful machine, the platform I'd torn out of myself in seven sleepless days, hunched over keyboards in the screen-dark while the world slept — the case, the redemption, the slow holy right-way plan, all of it — was a thing I was constructing over a grave. What if Clara had been dead since the second night and I was just a man having a very elaborate conversation with a ghost.

I had not let myself say that out loud. Not to Ifeoma, watching my infrastructure from Lagos and watching me harder. Not to Yuri, paranoid in Kyiv and usually right. Not even to the dark at three in the morning. Because to say it was to make it possible, and I could not make it possible, I would not, I needed her alive the way a drowning man needs the next inch of air, and so I had simply refused — had stacked the refusal up day after day until it was a wall — and built and built and built on top of it and never once looked down.

And now there was a line on a screen rising and falling, rising and falling, and it meant the pit was empty. There was no grave. There was a woman, somewhere, with a heart in her chest, doing the bravest thing a heart can do, which is to keep going when everything around it is built to make it stop.

I put my hand flat against the screen like an idiot, like a man who's never seen the thing he made.

I just wanted to be near it.

---

Basti called.

I'd forgotten the phone was even in my hand. Basti — my locksmith, my fixer, the smuggler turned good who handles the boots and the doors and the parts of this world a man can touch, the man on the ground while I am the man in the dark — Basti was already in Marseille, already in position, already doing the patient ugly groundwork while I sat in Brussels and made the truth heavy. He has a voice like gravel in a tin and a heart he hides behind it badly.

"You sound strange," he said, before I'd said anything. "You been crying? Tell me you been crying. I'll feel better about my whole week if the genius cried."

"She's alive," I said. My voice came out wrong, thin, a boy's voice. "Basti. She's alive. I'm — I can see it. I can see her heart."

A silence on the line. Then a long, slow exhale, the sound of a big man sitting down on something.

"You're sure." Not a question, the way Basti asks questions. "Not a guess. Not a hope. You don't get to do hope to me, Delta, I got no defences against your hope, you do hope to me and I will drive into that building tonight whether you say go or not."

"It's not hope." I laughed, and it cracked in the middle. "That's the thing, Basti. It's not hope. Hope's the thing I've been fighting all week. This is the opposite of hope. This is — it's the thing under hope. It's *real.* The system isn't telling me she might be alive. It's telling me she *is.* Right now. As we speak. Her heart is beating and I'm watching it."

I heard him swallow. Basti, who cries at dog films and pretends it's allergies, who carried a man's coffin in Marseille two years ago and didn't make a sound, and who now, on a phone, eleven hundred kilometres away, made one small sound and covered it with a cough.

"Okay," he said roughly. "Okay. Then she's waiting for us. Then we don't have a body to find, we got a *person*, and a person can be brought home." A pause. "You tell Ifeoma yet?"

"You're the first."

"Good." I could hear the grin trying to climb back into his voice and failing. "Then I get to be the one who knew before Lagos. She'll never forgive me. Worth it." Then, lower, all the gravel gone soft: "Bring her home, brother. We got a heartbeat. That's more than we had an hour ago. That's everything."

He hung up. My family hangs up before a man can thank them. It's a rule none of us ever agreed to and all of us keep — one of a hundred unwritten ones in a world that lives in the seams, where the soft thing is never said twice and no one ever, ever says goodbye on a line that might be listening.

---

I sat with it.

I let myself sit with it, just for a minute, just to be a man watching the woman he loves be alive, before I had to become the other thing again, the thing that plans, the thing that fights, the thing that solves the small impossible problem of a rotten door and a dying clock. I let the wall I'd built all week — the wall over the pit — I let it come down, gently, brick by brick, because there was no pit under it after all, there was a heartbeat, and a wall can come down when there's solid ground beneath.

I thought about the first time I'd ever felt that heartbeat. Las Vegas, three years ago, the convention still roaring its neon somewhere below us, a city full of my own kind throwing handles and secrets across smoke-dark rooms — and up in a hotel room with the air conditioning roaring and the city throwing its colours against the curtains, Clara asleep against my chest with her ear over my heart, and me lying awake. Me, who never lies still, who never stays, who dissolves into every feeling and forgets the world — lying perfectly, perfectly still so as not to wake her, feeling her heart through her back where it pressed against me, thinking: *this. I could do this. I could finally hold still for this one.* I hadn't known yet that I'd lose her. I hadn't known yet that I'd have to build a god out of seven sleepless nights to find her again. I'd just known the rhythm of her, the small steady miracle of it, the way it ran a little fast when she was thinking hard, even in her sleep.

The line on the screen rose and fell. Rose and fell. A little fast. She was thinking hard, even now. Of course she was. She was the smartest operator in the book, and she was inside the thing, deep in the dark where she'd let herself be taken, and she was *still working*, I could feel it, I could feel her mind running behind that brave fast heart, building her own case, drop by drop, sealing the truth from inside the very machine they thought held her — trusting, I knew this with a certainty that hollowed me out, *trusting that I would find the genome and come.*

"I found it," I said, out loud, to a screen, to a line, to a woman a thousand kilometres south who couldn't hear me. "I found your heartbeat, Clara. I found you. Hold on. I'm coming. The right way. I swear to you, the right way, even now, even like this — I'm coming, and I'm bringing the law with me, and we're going to *prove* them, all of them, every face — just *hold on*—"

And the heartbeat stopped.

---

I would like to have stayed calm.

I didn't. The line went flat — just flat, just gone, the rise and fall I'd been living inside for the last quarter of an hour simply *ceased*, the way a song ceases when someone lifts the needle, and the silence that came up where her heart had been was the loudest silence I have ever heard in my life. The whole world tipped. I was on my feet without deciding to stand. I had her name in my mouth and no air to put behind it.

One second.

Two.

The right-way man, the slow holy builder, the redeemed one — he was gone. There was only the wild thing left, the thing in the basement, the old ghost from the seams, on its feet, screaming, ready to burn the whole earth down to its bones if her heart did not start again.

Three seconds. Four.

*Start. Start. Start, Clara, start, you brave impossible woman, you do not get to do this, not now, not after six days, not when I'm watching — start—*

Five. Six. Seven.

I was begging. Out loud. To a machine. To a line. To God, whom I do not believe in and was, in that moment, on my knees before anyway.

Eight.

The heartbeat stopped for nine seconds.

Then it moved — faster than any human could run.
