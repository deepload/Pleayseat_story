# Chapter 33: When It Went Wrong Before

The Surgeon had sent me a photograph of a street in Lagos, and for a long time I could not move.

It came the way everything from him came — surfacing in a channel that shouldn't have existed, on a forum buried so far under the dark web that it took three favours and a dead man's handle just to find the door. No header. No name. Just the image blooming open on a screen that lit the whole borrowed flat blue, the only light left in the room at that hour, the hour when the rest of the world is asleep and the seams of it belong to people like us.

It was Ifeoma's street. I knew it the way a man knows the smell of his own childhood — the blue gate that never quite shut, the woman who sold roasted corn on the corner from a brazier that had burned in the same place for twenty years, the broken pavement where a tree root had won an argument with the city in 1998 and never given the ground back. He had photographed all of it. Lovingly. The way you photograph something you intend to take.

There was no message under the picture. There didn't need to be. The message was the picture. *I know where your conscience lives. I can reach into the one clean thing you ever made and pull a thread, and you will feel the whole garment come apart in your hands.*

I sat in the borrowed flat above the harbour, three laptops open and breathing their small electric hum, a mug of coffee gone cold and skinned-over beside the keyboard, and I felt the old animal stand up inside me — the one I'd spent three years putting to sleep. The one that knew how to do to him exactly what he was threatening to do to me. I knew his doors. I knew the handles he'd worn out and thrown away, the dead drops he thought were buried, the rooms behind the rooms. I could have been inside his life before the corn-seller lit her brazier, walked his whole shadow the way a ghost walks the place it died, and I could have made him afraid in the specific way that only someone who has loved a thing can be made afraid for it.

My hands were already moving toward it.

And then I stopped them, the way a man stops a car on ice — not with the brake, which would spin him, but with everything at once, his whole weight against the wheel. Because I had been here before. Not this room. Not this harbour. But this *exact* place, this fork in the dark where the fast dirty road and the slow clean one split apart, and a man stands at the crossing with his hands itching over a keyboard and someone he loves at the end of one of them.

I had taken the fast road once.

It cost me everything, and I never paid for it, and that — the not-paying — was the part I had never told a soul. Not in any channel, not to any handle, not in the long encrypted nights when the family trades the things it can't say in daylight. I would say it now, here, to myself, because it was the middle of the night and the Surgeon had just put his hand on Ifeoma's gate, and I could not make the right choice without first naming the wrong one.

---

Her name was Noa. The ex who still answers. The voice that is also a place.

But there was a version of Noa from before she became the ghost on my phone, a version when she was just a woman in Tel Aviv with a laugh like a window thrown open, and I was younger and stupider and so sure of myself it's a wonder anyone could stand to be in a room with me. I had a name in the scene already by then — not my own, never your own, in our world; a handle, a reputation that walked into rooms before I did. Doors opened for it. People I'd never met owed it. We met the way I met everyone in those years — in the seam of an operation, both of us there for reasons we weren't supposed to say out loud, in a city where the people who live in the seams all eventually pass through. I was working a job. A real one. Someone bad, somewhere, with something coming, and a small careful group of people had pointed me at it and said *find the thing before the thing happens.*

I have to be honest about who I was then. I was not a man who built things. I was a man who took them. I broke into the world for money, for principle, for the cold bright thrill of being somewhere I wasn't allowed — and too often, far too often, to impress a woman watching over my shoulder while the screen scrolled. I told myself there were good takings and bad takings, that I only did the good ones, that the line between them was visible to me even when it was invisible to the law. It's a comfortable lie. A lot of people I admired believed it — the best handles in the underground, legends in a place that has no monuments, people who went dark and stayed loved. Some of them are dead now and some of them are worse than dead, and all of them, every one, would have sworn on the night I met Noa that the most important thing in the world was the *target* — the thing in front of you, the job, the clean cold focus that gets you in and gets you out and gets the bad thing stopped before it happens.

I had that focus. For about nine days.

Then Noa walked into the operation sideways — not part of it, not against it, just *near* it, the way the most dangerous people in a life always arrive near and not at — and I did the thing I do. I fell. All the way. Off the edge of the work and into her, head first, the whole of me, no hand on the door. We had four weeks I would not trade for a clean conscience, which is everything anyone needs to know about me and that I wish weren't true.

And while I fell, the target moved.

---

I will not lay the operation out, not even to myself. Partly because the rule of this whole confession was that I face the wound and not the workings, and partly because the workings don't matter and never did. What matters is small and human and fits in a sentence:

There was a window. I was supposed to be watching it. I was watching Noa instead.

That's all. That's the whole crime. A man with one job — one screen he was never to look away from, one quiet stream of the world he'd promised to keep his eyes on through the night — who let the job blur because there was something warmer than the job in the same room, and who told himself — God, the lies I could tell myself at twenty-nine — that he could do both. That love made him sharper, not softer. That she was a gift to the work and not a hole in it. I believed that the way a man believes things he needs to be true. I checked the window less. I checked it late. One night I didn't check it at all, because Noa was tracing the alphabet on my back with one finger and telling me, in a half-asleep voice, the names she'd give children she swore she never wanted, and the window — the thing I'd sworn to watch — slid open in the dark while I lay there being happy, the screen across the room still glowing, still waiting, watched by no one.

The bad thing happened. The thing I was there to stop.

I won't say what it was. Only what it cost, because the cost is the only honest currency. It cost a person their life. Not Noa. Not me. A third person, a name I'd never said to her, someone neither of us ever met, who was on the other side of that window I didn't watch, who was depending — without ever knowing my handle, my name, my face — on a man in Tel Aviv keeping his eyes open. I kept them closed. I kept them on something beautiful. And a stranger died for my happiness, in the dark, while I was tracing a different alphabet.

---

Here was the part I had never said. Not in any room. Not down any wire.

Nobody knew it was me.

The operation closed. The little careful group filed it the way they file things — *unforeseeable, regrettable, no fault assigned.* The fault was assignable. The fault had a name and the name was mine — the real one and the handle both — and I was the only living person who knew it, and I sat with that knowledge and I made a choice that I have re-made every single day since.

I didn't tell them.

I could have. I could have walked into the room and said *the window was mine and I wasn't on it, I was in a bed, and a person is dead because I loved someone more than I did my job.* I'd have lost the work. The reputation — that handle that walked into rooms before me, the strange shabby honour I had in a world that has its own honour, its own loyalties and debts and legends, its own way of deciding who is royalty and who is burned. I'd have been burned. And I'd have lost Noa, probably, because nobody stays with the man whose love killed a stranger once that's a sentence said out loud.

I told myself I stayed quiet to protect the operation. I told myself the truth would unravel things that needed to stay raveled. I told myself a hundred careful, adult, operationally-sound reasons, and every one of them was true, and not one of them was the reason. The reason was that I could not bear to be seen as the man I was. The reason was cowardice wearing the costume of discretion. I let a stranger's death go down in the books as fate so that I could keep being the brilliant lucky boy who fell in love and got away with it — the handle nobody suspected, the legend that stayed clean in a dirty world.

I paid nothing. That's the cost I never paid. The bill came, and I let someone I'd never met cover it, and I walked out of that operation with my reputation intact and Noa's hand in mine, and I did not understand for years that I'd left the most important part of myself behind in that room — the part that finishes things, the part that stays on the window even when the room turns warm.

Noa and I burned out the way I always burned out. I fell for someone else the same total way, in front of her, because the disease was the disease and I hadn't found the cure. She watched me do to her what the window had done to that stranger — be loved by a man who could not keep his eyes on one thing. She left, eventually. She still answers — a name that still lights up the phone at the wrong hours, one of the few in the whole dark web of my life I never had to firewall out. I have never been able to work out whether that's forgiveness or sentence, and I've stopped asking, because the answer is probably *both,* and *both* is more than I deserve.

But she never knew about the window. To this day. She thinks the worst thing I ever did to her was the next woman. She doesn't know the worst thing I ever did was a night she remembers as one of the happiest of her life — because while she was naming children she'd never have, a person was dying for the privilege of my attention being elsewhere.

That's the cost I never paid. That's the man the Surgeon was betting still lived inside me.

---

And here was why it surfaced tonight, with his photograph of Ifeoma's gate still glowing in the dark, throwing its blue light up the wall while three machines hummed their patient electric breath around me.

Because he was right. The man still lived inside me. I could feel him stand up the moment I saw that blue gate — the one who knows the fast road, who would burn down the Surgeon's whole life to make him too afraid to touch Lagos. The one who could be through every door he owns before dawn, leaving nothing standing, leaving him to wake to a life turned inside out. And the fast road would *work,* that was the worst of it. It always works. That's its seduction. The fast road is faster. If I went down it tonight I might genuinely make Ifeoma safer by morning.

But I'd be back on that bed in Tel Aviv. I'd have my eyes on the warm thing and off the window. Because revenge, the dirty kind, is just love with the focus knocked out of it — it feels like the most important thing in the world right up until the moment a man realises the children are still moving while he's busy being a god in someone else's nightmare. The Surgeon didn't want to scare me off Ifeoma's street. He wanted to pull my eyes off the window. He wanted me to fall — into rage this time instead of love, but it was the same fall, the same totality, the same forgotten target — so that while I was avenging Lagos, the trucks rolled through the sixth relay point and forty-seven children became a number in a different book that no one ever finds.

I understood it the way a man understands a missed stair. He had read me the way I'd read him. We were two handles cut from the same dark — he knew my flaw because it had once been his, because we'd learned the same trade in the same seams before the road forked and he kept walking the fast one. He knew the flaw. He'd named it the only way that would land: not by threatening Clara, who I'd die before I forgot, but by threatening Ifeoma, who I love the *old* way, the scattering way, the way that once made a stranger die in the dark.

So I did the hardest thing I have ever done, and I did it by doing nothing.

I took my hands off the wheel of the fast road. I left the Surgeon's doors closed — every one of them, all the ways in I'd spent years memorising, all of it left dark and untouched. I let him keep his fear of nothing, kept mine quiet, and I reached for the phone instead to do the slow clean thing — to warn Ifeoma the right way, to wake the family and put their careful eyes on her street, to build a wall in front of that blue gate out of watching and evidence and love-with-the-focus-kept-in. I would not pay the stranger's bill again. I would not let Lagos cover for my rage the way that window covered for my joy.

It cost me. Choosing the slow road always costs me. It cost me the satisfaction, the speed, the clean animal relief of making him hurt — the oldest high there is in our world, the one a man never quite stops wanting. I sat there shaking with the not-doing of it, sweat cold on my back in the screen-light, the old man inside me howling that I was leaving Ifeoma exposed, that clean was just another word for slow, that slow gets people killed.

Maybe it does. But fast had already killed someone once, on my watch, while I wasn't watching. And I'd decided — somewhere in these long weeks, somewhere between the dead drop and now — that I was finally going to be the man who stays on the window. Even when the room turns warm. Even when it turns to fire.

I reached for the phone to call Lagos.

It rang in my hand before I touched it.

---

Not Lagos. Tel Aviv.

Noa's name, lit up in the dark, at an hour even she didn't call.

Something cold walked the length of my spine. Because I had just spent an hour with her ghost — with the window, with the stranger, with the cost I never paid — and now the woman herself was calling me at the worst hour of the worst night, the way a person only calls when something has its hand around their throat.

I answered before the second ring. "Noa."

"Listen to me." Fast. Low. The voice you use when there's no time for the voice you'd choose. "Listen, I've been watching for you, you asked me to watch and I never stopped — I see what he's doing. The pictures. Lagos. I've been in the channels, I've been reading his shadow all week. It's not Lagos he wants, do you understand, it's *you,* he's trying to turn you, he's trying to make you—" A breath, ragged. "Don't go after him. Whatever he sent you, whatever it makes you want to do — *don't.* That's the trap, it's the only trap that's ever worked on you, I should know, I'm the—"

She stopped.

"Noa?"

"There's a car," she said, and her voice had changed, dropped, gone flat and small the way a voice goes when the body has just understood something the mind is still refusing. "There's a car outside that wasn't— hold on. Hold on, there's someone on the—"

The next sound was not a word.

The next sound was wood. The particular splitting roar of a door meeting a boot with intent, the frame giving all at once, the whole solid promise of *home* coming apart in a single second — and under it, swallowed by it, the front half of my name in her mouth and the back half falling off the edge of the world.

Then the line broke.

Not a click. Not a goodbye. Just the door, and her, and then the silence where Noa used to be.
