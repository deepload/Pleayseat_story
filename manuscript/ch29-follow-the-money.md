# Chapter 29: Follow The Money

The old plans showed a room under the building that the city said wasn't there, and the only floor with the lights on was the one floor that didn't exist on paper. I sat with that for an hour and I couldn't make it lie down. A whole sub-floor, carved out below the dead hospital, scrubbed from every record a clerk had ever stamped — and somebody was keeping it lit, all night, behind concrete the maps insisted was solid earth.

You don't hide a room that well unless what's in it is worth more than the whole building on top.

I knew what was in it. I'd known for days. The question that wouldn't let me sleep was the one underneath it: how do you carve a secret floor into a hospital in a French city and have nobody, anywhere, ever write it down? How do you pour that much concrete in silence? How do you bribe an inspector, lose a permit, pay a foreman to forget? You don't do it with threats. Threats leave marks. You do it with money — quiet money, patient money, money that comes dressed as something kind.

So once again, the way you keep returning to the one tooth that aches, I went back to the money.

There's a thing my old teachers used to say, back when I had teachers, back when I was the kind of young man who needed telling: *everybody lies, but the money never does.* People lie with their mouths and their faces and their kind, sad eyes. They lie to investigators and to juries and to themselves in the mirror at three in the morning. But money has no face to arrange. Money just moves — from this hand to that one, on this day, in this amount — and every move it makes is a true sentence in a language with no word for *innocent.* You can't bribe a number. You can't look a ledger in the eye and make it forget what it saw.

Clara taught me to love that. Before her I'd been a man who broke things to find the truth. She was the one who showed me you could *follow* the truth instead — patiently, without breaking anything, letting it lay itself out drop by drop until it was undeniable. *The money never lies,* she used to say, in that exact, dry, certain voice. *It's the only honest thing in the whole rotten business. Find the money and you've found the men.*

So I asked the tool to find the men.

---

I won't tell you how. I made you a promise and I'm not breaking it tonight of all nights.

But I'll tell you what it felt like, because that's mine to give.

It felt like handing a hound the smell of one drop of blood and watching it remember every place that blood had ever been.

The thing I'd built — the thing I'd made in seven sleepless days for one reason, to find one woman — it had this quality I never got used to, even after all of it. It *remembered.* Everything. It held the whole long history of where this charity's money had been the way I might hold a single afternoon. Every gift. Every transfer. Every quiet hop from a clean account into a less clean one and a less clean one after that, all the way down into the dark and out the far side as something monstrous. People had spent years arranging those moves precisely so no single human being could ever hold them all in one mind at one time — so that each piece looked innocent, and only the whole thing was a crime. They'd bet everything on the idea that no one mind was big enough to see all of it at once.

They hadn't reckoned on a mind that didn't tire. That didn't blink. That didn't, at three in the morning, decide it was too sad to keep looking.

I sat in the borrowed flat above the closed bakery — the cold-flour smell had become the smell of the whole hunt by then, I'll smell it on my deathbed — and I asked it to walk the river one last time and bring me back not the *shape* of it, which I already had, but the *people.* Not "here is a network." I was past networks. I wanted what Clara always wanted at the end. I wanted names.

And it went, the way it always went — quiet, certain, without drama, the way the patient ones do the cruellest work — and all night it laid them at my feet, one by one.

---

Let me tell you what two thousand eight hundred and forty-seven looks like when you finally see it whole.

It looks like a city at night from very high up.

Each light is one move of money — one kindness picked up in the dark and turned into freight. Two thousand eight hundred and forty-seven lights. From up there you can't see the children in them. You can't see the pensioner in Lyon who gave because her own grandson was the right age, or the man in Brussels who gave every year on a dead girl's birthday. From up there it's just a field of cold little lights, beautiful, almost, the way a thing can be beautiful right up until you understand what it is.

And then you start to see that the lights aren't scattered. They're *organized.* They run in lines, in rivers of light, and every river bends at the same places, and at each bend there's a brighter light where the rivers pool — and those bright pools, the places everything flowed toward and through, those were the men. The money told me where to look the way the lights of a city tell you where the streets are. *There. And there. And there.* Twelve bright places where the whole river bent itself around a single waiting hand.

Twelve.

I'd known the number for weeks — Clara had carried it out of the dark in the dead drop, *twelve officials,* written in her steady little hand. But knowing a number is one thing and watching the tool walk the money to each of the twelve doors and lay the proof on the threshold is another. One by one it went. The logistics man who shook hands with cabinet ministers and let convoys of the damned roll through his depots for a cut. The banker in Geneva who washed the river clean and never once looked at the water. The official — the protected one, the big one, the name Clara had nearly died to carry out — who'd built the whole machine and stood in front of cameras with his arm around an orphan, and whose particular hand the river bent around so completely that I understood, finally, that he wasn't a part of the machine. He *was* it. The other eleven were his fingers.

Twelve names. Twelve true sentences in the only honest language left in the world.

I wrote them down. By hand, on paper, the old way, because some things you need to feel come out through your own fingers. Eleven of them I already knew, or half-knew, or had felt the shape of in the dark for days. Writing them was like setting bones — it hurt, but it was the hurt of something finally being put right.

While I wrote, the others were awake too, scattered across their time zones, threaded into the night with me the way they'd been threaded into all of it. Ifeoma was up in Lagos, watching the infrastructure breathe, the way she'd watched over me for years like a sister who's decided you're not allowed to die on her shift. Yuri was somewhere in Kyiv being paranoid and being right, the way he always was, the way that had saved my life twice. And Noa — Noa had answered, hours earlier, the way she always answered, from Tel Aviv, the ghost of the man I used to be and maybe the only person who'd loved that man on purpose. *Be careful with this one,* she'd said before she hung up. *When you find the rot, it's never where you think. It's never out there. It's always at the table.* I'd laughed it off. I should have listened to her. She was always the one who knew where the bodies in me were buried.

I thought about all of them as I wrote the names — my family, the people I'd chosen, the web of fixers and ghosts and former marks I'd spent a life weaving so that I'd never again be a man with nobody. They were the floor under me. They were the reason I got to believe there was a floor at all.

And then there was the twelfth.

---

Basti came up around two with coffee, the way he did, the careful quiet of a man who's spent his own years in the dark and knows when not to fill it with words. He set the cup down. He looked at the work breathing in the dim, at me hunched over a sheet of paper writing names by hand like a monk, and he understood without being told that I was down at the bottom of it now, in the last room, with the last truth.

"You found them," he said. Not a question.

"I found them."

He was quiet. Then: "How bad."

"All the way bad." I rubbed my eyes. "Twelve men. The money walked me to every door. There's no arguing with it, Basti. No lawyer alive can stand up and say the money was confused." I almost laughed, the way you laugh at the worst moments because the alternative is worse. "She was right. She was always right. The money never lies. People lie. Money just moves, and you follow it, and at the end of it there's a face."

He nodded slowly. He'd lived that truth in his old life too, on the wrong side of it. "And you know all twelve faces?"

I looked down at the list. Eleven names in my own crabbed hand, and the twelfth still waiting, because the tool had laid it last, the way it always laid the worst thing last, patient and bloodless, *here is a thread, and here is where it goes,* and I had been staring at it for a while now without breathing.

"Eleven," I said. "I know eleven."

Basti waited. He's good at waiting. It's the locksmith in him — he knows that the lock opens when it's ready and not before, and you only break it if you shove.

"The twelfth," I said, and my voice did a thing I didn't recognize.

I want to tell you, here, what I'd been so sure of all night. I'd been so sure that the twelve would be *them.* The far ones. The men in the good suits in the bright cities, the philanthropists and the ministers, people I'd never touched and never loved, people it would cost me nothing to hate. That's how it's supposed to go, isn't it. The monster is supposed to wear the monster's face. The river is supposed to run *out there,* through their world, the clean shining world that does this to children behind its own back, and the whole point — the whole reason I'd chosen, every single time I was tempted, to do this the right way instead of the dirty way — was that *we* were not them. My family and I. The people in the seams. We broke laws, sure, half of us, in our wild years. But we never touched the children. There was a floor under us. There's a floor under everyone, I'd told myself my whole life, and ours was that.

The money doesn't know about your floor.

The money just moves, and at three in the morning it laid the twelfth name at my feet, and I read it, and the cold-flour air went out of the room.

---

I read it again. I made the tool show me the river to that door three times, four, the way you reread the one sentence in the letter you don't have the strength to believe. Each time it was the same. Each time the money walked, patient and certain, out of the charity and down into the dark and around eleven bright bends — and then, at the twelfth, it bent around a hand I knew.

A hand I'd shaken. A hand I'd needed. A hand that had pulled me out of more than one fire in the wild years, that I'd have sworn on my own life had a floor under it, the same floor I stood on, the floor that was the whole reason I got to think of myself as one of the good ones.

The money had bent around it. Two thousand eight hundred and forty-seven times the river had run, and somewhere in there, more than once, in amounts a man hopes nobody will ever add up, it had run *to* this hand and *through* it and onward, into the machine that moved the children.

I sat very still.

I thought about the thing Clara used to say, the thing I'd built my whole new self around — *the money never lies* — and for one ugly second I wanted it to. I wanted it, just this once, to be wrong. I'd have given the eleven back, every one of them, I'd have let the ministers and the bankers walk free and clean in their bright cities, if the money would only take this last name off my floor and put it somewhere out there in the dark with the rest of the monsters where I could hate it without it costing me anything.

It didn't move. The money doesn't bargain. It just sat there in the dim, twelve true sentences, and the twelfth one looked back at me with a face I loved.

Basti was watching me. He'd seen something cross me. "Brother," he said, very low. "What is it. Who's the twelfth."

I couldn't say it. I put my hand flat over the paper, over the name, as if covering it could un-write it, as if there were any version of the next hour, the next day, the rest of my whole crooked life, in which I hadn't already seen it.

Because one of the twelve names — one of the twelve men whose hands the river of stolen mercy had bent around, one of the twelve I would now have to prove, and name, and hand to the law, and watch fall —

one of them belonged to a member of my own family.
