# Chapter 11: The Rails

There is a lie so beautiful that no one wants to catch it.

The lie is a truck. A white truck, clean as a hospital, with a blue sticker on the side — two hands cupped around a small bird, or a flame, or a child, depending on which charity painted it — rolling down a road that other trucks are afraid to take. It carries rice. It carries blankets and powdered milk and the kind of cheap, sturdy shoes that get handed out in places where the school burned down. Children run after it. Grandmothers bless it. Soldiers wave it through the checkpoint and feel, for one minute, like the good guys.

Nobody stops a charity truck. That's the whole point. That's the genius of it, and I hated whoever thought of it the way you can only hate someone you understand.

I had spent the night before learning that the trail of *Phantom Mercy* was three years old — that it had been laid the same month Clara and I first fell into each other, in a desert city, a lifetime and a hand of cards ago. That had nearly broken me. So this morning, when I sat back down at the machine, I did it the way you go back to a wound: slowly, and looking somewhere else.

I asked it one question.

*Show me where the trucks go.*

---

Here is the thing I want you to feel, and I'll say it once and then never bore you with the how, because the how doesn't matter and the *what* will keep you up at night the way it kept me.

The world is full of routes nobody draws on a map.

The official routes — the ones the charity puts in its glossy report, the proud arrows from a warehouse in one country to a refugee camp in another — those are for the donors. Those are the routes you photograph. There is a smiling logistics manager in every one of those photographs, and he is always, always wearing a vest with a lot of pockets.

But under the routes you're allowed to see, there are routes you aren't. The truck doesn't go straight. It can't. A straight line is honest, and honesty is the one thing this cargo couldn't survive. So the truck wanders. It stops where it has no reason to stop. It waits in a yard behind a yard. It crosses a border at the hour the tired guard changes shift. It loses a few hours it can never quite account for, the way a drunk loses a Tuesday.

To anyone watching, that's just bad logistics. Trucks are late. Roads are bad. Wars move things around. Shrug.

The thing I built doesn't shrug.

It took every honest route and laid it over every real one — the real one being where the truck *actually* went, gathered from a thousand small breadcrumbs the world drops without meaning to — and it showed me the difference between the two. The gap. The lie in the shape of a detour.

And the gaps weren't random.

That was the moment my coffee went cold in my hand. Because random would have been innocent. Random would have been potholes and checkpoints and a driver who liked a particular roadside café. What I was looking at wasn't random. It was a *system*. The same detours, over and over, year after year, like a path worn into a carpet by a man pacing the same six steps while he waits for terrible news.

Six steps.

I counted them before I understood what I was counting. Six places where the honest route and the real route peeled apart and the truck went dark for a handful of hours and came back lighter — or heavier — than the manifest said it should be. Six stops that appeared in convoy after convoy, route after route, charity after charity, like a watermark you only see when you tilt the page to the light.

Six relay points.

I knew that number. Clara had given me that number. It had been sitting in her last message like a stone in a shoe, and I'd read past it a hundred times because I didn't yet have the map to put it on. *Six relay points.* She hadn't been guessing. She'd already walked this road in her head, alone, in the dark, with no one to call. She'd seen the worn path in the carpet and she'd counted the steps, and then she'd done the thing only Clara would do.

She'd decided to be cargo.

---

I had to get up and walk around the apartment. I want to be honest about that, because a confession that pretends the man telling it stayed calm is a coward's confession. I got up and I walked the four walls of that grey Brussels room like the truck walked its route — pacing the same six steps, waiting for terrible news — and I said her name out loud twice, which I'd been trying not to do, because saying it out loud made the room emptier.

Then I made myself sit down, because she would have. Clara had a gift I never had: she could be terrified and useful at the same time. I could only ever pick one. So I borrowed her, the way you borrow a steadier friend's voice in your head, and I sat back down, and I asked the machine the second question. The one that turns a horror into a case.

*Show me the money.*

---

Let me tell you about the money, in plain words, because the money is the cruelest part and the part the donors would least believe.

People give. That's the thing you have to hold on to. Ordinary people, good people, people who saw a photograph of a child with enormous eyes and felt something move in them and reached for their wallet — they give. They gave to *Phantom Mercy* by the hundred thousand. Small gifts, mostly. The price of a coffee. The price of a cinema ticket they decided to skip because somewhere a child needed it more. I have looked at the river of those gifts and I have wanted to lie down on the floor, because every single one of them was an act of love, and every single one of them was being used to oil the machine that was eating the children they thought they were saving.

That's the trick, you see. The convoys move the children. The donations move the *guilt* — they wash it. Dirty money goes in pretending to be a gift, gets stirred into the great warm river of real gifts from real kind people, and comes out the other side clean, blessed, untouchable, wearing the same blue sticker as the rice. Nobody audits a charity that's saving children. That would be obscene. That would be the act of a monster. So the monsters hid exactly there, behind the one door no decent person will kick down.

I sat in the dark and I watched the river of gifts and the river of lies run side by side and braid together until you couldn't tell which drop was which. And the thing I built — patient, tireless, with no heart to break, which is its own kind of mercy — pulled them apart. Drop by drop. It found the gifts that weren't gifts. The ones that came in too round, too clean, from too far, at the wrong hour, in a rhythm — there's that word again, the rhythm, the fingerprint, the man's hands he can never change. The same cold, careful hands I'd been chasing since I first called him the Surgeon.

His hands were on the money too.

Of course they were. He'd built the rails. The trucks were the body of the thing and the money was its blood, and he'd designed both to look like charity, and he'd done it with the patient, elegant cruelty of a man who treated children as weight and donors as fools and the whole grieving, generous world as a thing to be steered.

I felt the admiration try to come back. That sick professional warmth — *look what he made, look how it breathes.* And I heard Yuri in my head, the way he'd said it down the line from Kyiv with a drone going over: *Admire him from the outside. The second you climb into his head to appreciate the artistry, you stop seeing the children.*

So I didn't admire it. I counted it.

Two thousand, eight hundred and forty-seven.

That was the number of poisoned gifts the machine pulled out of the river. Two thousand, eight hundred and forty-seven separate little lies wearing the face of kindness, stretched across four countries, three years, and a dozen names too clean to be true. I didn't choose that number. I want to be clear. I didn't go looking for it to make the night feel literary. The machine handed it to me the way it handed me everything — flatly, without drama, a bird laid at my feet — and I sat and looked at it and felt the floor of the world tilt, because two thousand, eight hundred and forty-seven is not a number you survive looking at all at once. It's a number you have to look at one drop at a time or it drowns you.

Twelve of those names mattered more than the rest. Twelve hands that the river kept washing toward, again and again, like a tide returning the same drowned thing to the same shore. I didn't have their faces yet. I had the shape of where they touched the money, the way the night before I'd had the shape of where they'd been. Twelve. I wrote it down on the back of an envelope, under the six, because some numbers you don't trust to a screen. *Six relays. Twelve hands.* And the river, and the trucks, and Clara somewhere inside the body of the beast, holding her breath, keeping her own count.

---

I called Basti, because I needed to hear a human being who lived where the road ended.

Basti is in Marseille. He used to open things that weren't his, in the years when we were both younger and the rules felt optional, and then one day he started opening doors for people who'd locked themselves out instead of people who'd locked something away, and he calls that *going straight* and I call it *going sideways with better manners*, and we love each other the way only two reformed thieves can.

He answered with his mouth full. That's how I knew he was alright. The day Basti answers and isn't eating is the day I'll start praying.

"It's the middle of the night where you are," he said. "Or it's the middle of the night where you live, anyway. You don't have a where anymore, do you, you sad orphan."

"Basti." I closed my eyes. "The aid convoys. The white trucks. The charity ones, the ones nobody stops."

A pause. A swallow. When he spoke again the joking had gone out of his voice the way the tide goes out, all at once, leaving the flat hard sand. "What about them."

"They come through Marseille."

"Everything comes through Marseille," he said. "That's what a port is. It's the world's back door, and I grew up holding it open." Another pause. I could hear his city behind him — a scooter, a gull, someone laughing two floors down, the whole warm noisy life of the place. "Why are you asking me about the white trucks, Delta. Ask me something else."

"Because they're not carrying rice."

He didn't say anything for a long time. Long enough that I thought the line had dropped, the way Clara's line had dropped, the way they all dropped now in my imagination, one by one, everyone I loved going dark mid-sentence.

"Basti."

"I'm here." His voice had gone quiet and strange. "I'm here. I just — listen. There's a yard. Behind the old fish market, where they don't sell fish anymore, where nobody sells anything anymore. I've watched the white trucks go in there at hours when no charity in the world is working. I told myself it was nothing. I told myself, Basti, you have a guilty mind, you see crime everywhere because you used to be it." He took a breath I could hear. "Tell me I was wrong to leave it alone. Tell me, and I'll go and stand in that yard tonight."

"No," I said, and it came out harder than I meant, because the thought of Basti standing in that yard, in the dark, alone, while the Surgeon's cold hands moved somewhere over a map — it put a cold of its own straight through me. "No. You don't go near it. Not yet. Not until I know more. Promise me."

"You've never told me not to open a door before."

"I'm telling you now."

He laughed, but it was thin. "Now he learns caution. The man who taught me to be reckless. She really did change you, your Clara." And then, gently, because under all the food and the filthy jokes Basti is the kindest man I know: "We'll get her out, brother. The trucks always have to come back empty. And an empty truck is a confession. You taught me that."

He hung up first. They always hang up first. I sat with the dead line and the cold coffee and the envelope with the two numbers on it, and outside the Brussels morning went on buttering its bread.

---

I went back to the trucks. I had to.

Because there was one more thing the machine was still chewing on, quietly, in the corner where it kept the parts of the picture I hadn't asked to see yet. The routes had a *future*. That's the thing about a worn path in a carpet — it doesn't just tell you where a man has paced. It tells you he's going to pace it again. The convoys ran on a schedule. Cruelty, it turns out, keeps office hours. There had been one last month. One the month before that. One every few weeks, regular as a heartbeat, regular as the rhythm of those poisoned gifts, the Surgeon's cold hands keeping perfect time.

Which meant there was a next one.

I asked. I didn't want to. There are questions you ask the way you reach into the dark under a bed where you've heard something move. But Clara was inside the body of the beast, holding her breath, keeping her count, and if the beast was about to move, then she was about to move with it, and I needed to know how long I had.

The machine answered the way it always did. Flatly. A bird at my feet.

It put two things on the screen and I read them both, and then I read them again, the way I'd read the Surgeon's name was mine, the way the truth always makes me read it twice because the first time it just sits on the surface and refuses to be true.

The next convoy left in forty hours.

And the manifest counted its cargo as "47 units."
