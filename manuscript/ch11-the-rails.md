# Chapter 11: The Rails

There is a lie so beautiful that no one wants to catch it.

The lie is a truck. A white truck, clean as an operating room. A blue sticker on the side: two hands cupped around a small bird. It rolls down roads the other trucks avoid. It carries rice. Blankets, powdered milk, sturdy shoes for places where the school burned down.

Children run after it. Grandmothers bless it. At the checkpoint, the soldiers wave it through and feel, for one minute, like the good guys.

Nobody stops a charity truck. That's the whole point. And I hated whoever had thought of it. The kind of hate you can only feel for someone you understand.

I had spent the night learning one thing. The trail of PHANTOM MERCY went back two and a half years. The same month Clara and I fell into each other. A desert city, all neon and mirage. Another life. That had nearly broken me.

By the time the sky turned grey behind the blinds, I hadn't slept. Just the screen for a lamp. Its blue glow on my hands like cold water. The smell of hot machines — warm dust, electric. The only smell I have left that feels like home.

So I sat back down. The way a man goes back to a wound. Slowly, looking somewhere else.

One question.

*Show me where the trucks go.*

---

Let me say one thing. Once. And never revisit the how again. The how doesn't matter. The *what* is what keeps me awake.

The world is full of routes nobody draws on a map.

That's the first thing you learn down below, in the seams where we live. Us. The people who've disappeared from directories. The ones who talk under handles, in buried channels nobody ever finds — that you only discover once you're inside them, after days of silence, earning the right to have a door crack open.

You learn that there are always two maps. The one they show you. And the real one.

The official routes go in the glossy report. Proud little arrows: a warehouse here, a refugee camp there. Those routes are for the donors. Those are the ones that get photographed. There's always a smiling logistics manager in the photo. He always wears a vest with a lot of pockets.

But under the permitted routes, there are the ones that stay hidden. The truck doesn't go straight. It can't. A straight line is honest, and this cargo couldn't survive honesty.

So the truck wanders. It stops where it has no reason to stop. It waits in a yard behind a yard. It crosses a border at the hour the tired guard changes shift. It loses a few hours it can never quite account for. The way a drunk loses a Tuesday.

To anyone watching, that's just bad logistics. Trucks are late. Roads are rough. War moves things around. Shrug.

What I built doesn't shrug.

It took every honest route and laid it over every real one — where the truck had *actually* gone, reconstructed from a thousand small crumbs the world drops without meaning to. The traces nobody erases because nobody imagines anyone's collecting them.

And it showed me the difference. The gap. The lie in the shape of a detour.

The gaps weren't random.

That's when the coffee went cold in my hand. It always goes cold. That's the one constant of my life for twenty years. A half-drunk cup, forgotten by the keyboard while something else burns on the screen.

Random would have been innocent. Potholes, checkpoints, a driver who likes a particular roadside café. What I was looking at wasn't random. It was a *system*. The same detours, over and over, year after year. Like a path worn into a carpet by a man pacing six steps while he waits for terrible news.

Six steps.

I counted them before I understood what I was counting. Six places where the honest route and the real route split apart. Where the truck went dark for a few hours and came back lighter — or heavier — than the manifest said. Six stops that showed up convoy after convoy, route after route, charity after charity.

Six relay points.

I knew that number. Clara had given it to me. It was sitting in her last message like a stone in a shoe. I'd read past it a hundred times. I didn't yet have the map to put it on.

*Six relay points.* She hadn't been guessing. She'd already walked this road in her head. Alone. In the dark. With no one to call. She'd seen the worn path in the carpet. She'd counted the steps. And then she'd done the thing only Clara would do.

She'd decided to be the cargo.

---

I had to get up. Walk the apartment. I want to be honest about that. A confession from a man who claims he stayed calm is a coward's confession.

I got up. I paced those four grey Brussels walls the way the truck paced its route. The same six steps. Waiting for the terrible news. I said her name out loud. Twice. Something I'd been trying not to do. Because saying it made the room emptier.

Then I forced myself back into the chair. Because she would have. Clara had a gift I never had. She could be terrified and useful at the same time. I've never been able to pick more than one.

So I borrowed her courage. The way you borrow a steadier friend's voice. I sat back down. And I asked the second question. The one that turns a horror into a case.

*Show me the money.*

---

A word about the money. In plain words. Because it's the cruelest part. The part the donors would least believe.

People give. I have to hold onto that. Ordinary people. Good people. They saw a photo of a child with enormous eyes, felt something move in them, reached for their wallet. They gave.

They gave to PHANTOM MERCY by the hundred thousand. Small donations, mostly. The price of a coffee. The price of a cinema ticket they skipped because somewhere a child needed it more.

I looked at the river of those gifts and I wanted to lie down on the floor. Every one of them was an act of love. And every one of them was oiling the machine that was eating the children they thought they were saving.

That's the trick. The convoys move the children. The donations move the *guilt*. They wash it. Dirty money goes in disguised as a gift. It mixes with the warm river of real donations. It comes out the other side clean. Blessed. Untouchable. Wearing the same blue sticker as the rice.

Nobody audits a charity that saves children. That would be obscene. That would be the act of a monster. So the monsters hid exactly there. Behind the one door no decent person will kick down.

And the money doesn't even move through banks anymore. That's what the people who build these things think is so clever. Run the river through digital tokens. Currencies that belong to no one and live nowhere. An anonymous donation. A string of characters passing from one shadow to another. No name, no face, no country. They think that erases them.

They forget one thing. Nothing is truly erased down below. Not in the basement of the world where stolen lists trade hand to hand, handle to handle, on unnamed forums. Where any habit eventually betrays the man who repeats it.

Every token leaves a comet tail. You just need someone patient enough to follow it through the dark. Insomniac enough. Broken enough.

I sat in the dark. I watched the river of gifts and the river of lies run side by side. Braid together until you couldn't tell one drop from another.

And what I built pulled them apart. Drop by drop. Patient. Tireless. With no heart to break — which is its own form of mercy. It found the gifts that weren't gifts. The ones that arrived too round, too clean, from too far away, at the wrong hour. In a rhythm. That word again. Rhythm. Fingerprint. The hands of a man he can never change.

The same cold, careful hands I'd been tracking since I first called him the Surgeon.

His hands were on the money too.

Of course they were. He'd built the rails. The trucks were the body. The money was the blood. He'd designed both to look like charity. With the patient, elegant cruelty of a man who treated children as cargo weight, donors as idiots, and the entire world as something to be steered.

I felt the admiration try to come back. That sick professional warmth. *Look what he made. Look how it breathes.*

And I heard Yuri in my head. The way he'd told me from Kyiv, a drone passing overhead, his voice scraped raw through three relay hops he stacked out of habit: *Admire him from the outside. The second you get inside his head to taste the craft, you stop seeing the children.*

So I didn't admire him. I counted him.

Two thousand, eight hundred and forty-seven.

That was the number of poisoned gifts the machine pulled out of the river. Two thousand, eight hundred and forty-seven small lies, each wearing the face of kindness. Spread across four countries. Two and a half years. A dozen names too clean to be real.

I didn't choose that number. I want to be clear about that. I didn't go looking for it to make the night more literary. The machine handed it to me the way it handed me everything. Flatly. Without drama. A bird laid at my feet.

And I sat there. Looking at it. The floor of the world shifted. Two thousand, eight hundred and forty-seven is not a number you survive looking at all at once. It's a number you have to take one drop at a time. Otherwise it drowns you.

I was drowning.

Twelve of those names mattered more than the rest. Twelve hands the river kept returning to, again and again. Like a tide bringing the same body back to the same shore. I didn't have their faces yet. I had the shape of where they touched the money. The way the night before I'd had the shape of where they'd passed.

Twelve. I wrote it on the back of an envelope, under the six. Because there are numbers you don't trust to a screen. That's the first rule, the one I learned before all the others. What truly matters, you write on paper. And the paper you burn.

*Six relays. Twelve hands.* And the river, and the trucks. And Clara somewhere inside the body of the beast. Holding her breath. Keeping her own count.

---

I called Basti. I needed to hear a human being who lived where the road ended.

Basti is in Marseille. He used to open things that weren't his. Back when we were younger and the rules felt optional. Then one day he started opening doors for people locked out instead of people who'd locked something away. He calls that *going straight*. I call it *forcing the same locks with better manners*. We love each other the way only two repentant thieves can.

He answered with his mouth full. That's how I knew he was fine. The day Basti picks up without eating, I'll start praying.

"It's the middle of the night where you are," he said. "Or where you live, anyway. You don't have a where anymore, do you, you sad orphan."

"Basti." I closed my eyes. "The aid convoys. The white trucks. The charity ones. The ones nobody stops."

A pause. A swallow. When he came back, the joking was gone from his voice. Fast, like a tide pulling out. Leaving the sand flat and hard.

"What about them."

"They pass through Marseille."

"Everything passes through Marseille. That's what a port is. The world's service entrance. I grew up holding the door open." Another pause. I could hear his city behind him. A scooter. A gull. Someone laughing two floors down. All that warm, noisy life. "Why are you asking me about the white trucks, Delta. Ask me something else."

"Because they're not carrying rice."

He didn't say anything for a long time. Long enough that I thought the line had dropped. The way Clara's line had dropped. The way they all dropped now in my head, one by one. Every person I loved going dark mid-sentence.

"Basti."

"I'm here." His voice had gone low and strange. "I'm here. It's just — listen. There's a yard. Behind the old fish market, where nobody sells fish anymore. Where nobody sells anything anymore. I've watched the white trucks go in there at hours when no charity in the world is working. I told myself it was nothing. I told myself: Basti, you have a guilty mind. You see crime everywhere because you used to be crime." A breath I could hear. "Tell me I was wrong to leave it alone. Tell me, and I'll go stand in that yard tonight."

"No." It came out harder than I meant. The thought of Basti standing in that yard, in the dark, alone, while the Surgeon's cold hands moved across a map — it put a cold of its own straight through me. "No. You don't go near it. Not yet. Not until I know more. Promise me."

"You've never told me not to open a door."

"I'm telling you now."

He laughed. A thin laugh.

"Now he learns caution. The man who taught me to be reckless. She really changed you, your Clara."

Then, quietly. Because under all the food and the filthy jokes, Basti is the most tender man I know:

"We'll get her out, brother. The trucks always have to come back empty. And an empty truck is a confession. You taught me that."

He hung up first. They always hang up first. It's a discipline down below. Almost a courtesy. You don't leave an open line trailing behind you like a door you forgot to close. Not when you've spent your whole life assuming an extra ear is always listening.

I sat with the dead line. The cold coffee. The envelope with its two numbers. Outside, the Brussels morning went on buttering its bread.

---

I went back to the trucks. I had to.

The machine was still chewing on something, quietly, in the corner where it kept the parts of the picture I hadn't asked to see yet. The routes had a *future*.

That's the thing about a path worn into a carpet. It doesn't only show where a man has walked. It says he'll walk there again. The convoys ran on a schedule. Cruelty, it turns out, keeps office hours. There had been one last month. One the month before. One every few weeks, regular as a heartbeat. Regular as those poisoned donations, the Surgeon's cold hands keeping perfect time.

Which meant there was a next one.

I asked. I didn't want to. There are questions you ask the way you reach your hand into the dark under a bed where you heard something move. But Clara was inside the body of the beast. Holding her breath. Keeping her count. If the beast was about to move, she would move with it. I needed to know how much time I had.

The machine answered the way it always did. Flatly. A bird at my feet.

It put two things on the screen. I read them both. Then read them again. The way I'd read the Surgeon's name was mine. Truth always makes me read it twice. The first time it just sits on the surface and refuses to be real.

The next convoy left in forty hours.

And the manifest counted its cargo as "47 units."
