# Chapter 32: Reading The Enemy

Clara said one word through the wire. One word no one else on earth could know.

And the line she said it on was never supposed to be open.

I sat with that for a moment. The blue dark of the Marseille flat. The screen the only light. The harbour breathing through the cracked window. A mug of coffee gone cold three hours ago. And out there, somewhere, forty-seven children moving through the night across four countries.

Two true things at once. Together they said what I didn't want to hear.

The word was real. It was ours. A small private nonsense born in a Las Vegas hotel, built between us. So it was her. Really her. Alive. Close.

And if the line was open — that line I had sealed tighter than anything I have ever sealed — then someone had opened it.

Someone who could.

There is only one man. One man capable of opening a door I locked shut. I'd already met his work. I'd already given him a name, just from the clean way he cut.

And I knew. The moment Clara's word landed in my ear, I knew he had let me hear it.

That was the message. *I can reach her whenever I like. Enjoy the sound of her while it lasts.*

I didn't move.

Then I did the only thing I have ever been any good at.

I started to read him.

---

Reading a man like that is not what you think.

Not screens. Not the green lines from the movies.

You learn a man by his grain. By the way he goes quiet. By the hour he connects. By the small habit he repeats without knowing it.

I'd been doing it for days without admitting it. Every time he touched the network, the tool I built kept the trace. I had a whole gallery of him by now. The patient downstroke. The way he never rushed. The cold, impeccable housekeeping of a surgeon.

So when he opened the line, the tool didn't panic. *I* panicked — my heart went up six floors at once. But the thing I'd built just leaned in. And it recognised the hand. *That's him.*

He wasn't taunting me. Taunting is warm, human. The Surgeon had nothing warm. He was *testing* me. He'd opened a door he knew I'd feel. Now he was waiting. Watching what I'd do.

An older version of me would have torn the connection down in a rage. Would have felt brave for four seconds. Just long enough to understand he'd told the enemy everything about himself and learned nothing back.

I didn't tear anything down.

I left the door open.

---

This is the part I'm most proud of. And the hardest to say.

When you realise the other person is reading you, you don't hide. Hiding tells them you know they're there. You keep going. You start to feed them. You let them believe they have you. And while they're savoring how clever they are, you read them reading you.

That requires staying calm while a man you hate closes his fingers around the throat of the only thing you love.

I was calm.

Clara had taught me that. Not with words. By example. That woman had let herself be swallowed by a monster. Then, sitting in the monster's belly, inside a server room, she had built a chain of evidence drop by drop, with a watchmaker's patience. If she could be calm in the dark with the beast at her neck, I could be calm in a warm flat.

So I let him hear me listen. Then, very gently, I moved.

I won't say how. The how would shrink it, and that night doesn't deserve to be shrunk. What matters is the what.

He opened a door to let me hear her. I let him think it caught me off guard. Then I followed the draught. Every open door has a draught — a thin cold thread of air that tells you which way the room beyond it lies. Where his door was, his hand had been. Where his hand had been, he had been. And a man, however patient, however much of a ghost, *is somewhere*. In a chair. The chair in a room. The room in a building. The building in a city. And a city, to the thing I built, is just a large place that hasn't been narrowed yet.

He moved. I moved. He shifted, smelling it, the way I'd smelled him days before. I felt him go still. Then he made a small, beautiful, cruel adjustment. A feint so clean it left no mark — built to send me chasing a ghost three countries away while the real him slid sideways into the dark.

I didn't chase the ghost.

I'd been waiting for the feint. Of course there'd be a feint. *I* would have used a feint. He learned his trade from a man who used feints. And that man, in a life I had buried — the sleepless nights, the fake names you burn and relight somewhere else, the allegiances forged in the dark with faceless people — that man was me.

That's the thing about a dark mirror. You don't beat it by thinking faster. You beat it by remembering what *you* would do. Because it will do the same. And then doing the one thing the old you was never capable of: not taking the bait.

So when his ghost ran west, I stayed exactly where I was. Watching where he'd actually gone. The small quiet seam he'd slipped through while he thought I was looking elsewhere. And I followed. Move for move. He cut; I was already at the next seam. He doubled back; I was in the doorway. He went silent, the long predator stillness meant to make me move first —

and I went silent too.

We sat in the dark together. Two men from the same room. Neither moving. Both listening to a line that hummed with one woman's breathing somewhere beneath it all.

And for the first time I felt something change. His grain changed. The patient downstroke became *less* patient. A half-second of hesitation crept in where there had never been any.

He'd met someone who read him as fast as he read.

He didn't like it.

---

I took no pleasure in it. I need to say that clearly.

I know how it sounds. The elegant duel. Matched minds. There is a version of me — the version I used to be — who would have *loved* this. The clean drug of being finally seen by the only other person good enough to see you.

But every minute of it I could hear her.

That was the cruelty I hadn't named yet. He hadn't opened the line to threaten me. He'd opened it to *occupy* me. While I was busy matching him move for move, drunk on being read and reading back, the children were moving. The clock was running. He had handed me the most intoxicating thing in my world — a worthy opponent and the sound of my love's voice — and he'd done it deliberately. Because he knew the old me, the one who falls all the way and forgets the target, would drown in it. Would sit at this screen all night, lost in the beautiful game, while forty-seven small pairs of feet crossed a border.

He had read my flaw. The way men like us disappear into a thing until the thing is all there is. That is the real sickness of our trade. Not prison, not law enforcement. Erasure. Vanishing so completely into the work that you stop existing.

The only reason it didn't work comes down to one thing.

That capacity to disappear entirely into something — the curse that had scattered me across a dozen lives, that had broken every promise I ever made — I'd already spent it. Poured it all into one place, days ago, in Brussels. And that place had a name: Clara.

There was nothing left for the Surgeon. He'd come to seduce a man who was already entirely somewhere else. A man who was faithful, for the first time in his miserable life. So the duel that should have swallowed me only *bored* me, somewhere underneath the adrenaline.

I followed him. And I didn't drown.

I read him. I kept reading her. I kept reading the clock. All three at once — something the old me could never have done. Hold three things in one hand without dropping two. I had never been so steady in my life.

It cost me later. Everything that steadies you costs you later. But that night I could not be moved. And across the line, in his cold room, in his own pool of screen-light, the Surgeon felt it. And for the first time he made an *error*.

He let his hesitation last too long.

In that half-second too long, the thing I built laid one true thing at my feet.

A direction. Not a name. Not a room. Not yet. A *bearing*. A line through the dark that pointed, out of all the four-country night, at one quarter of one city where the man who had cut children open along their seams was sitting in a chair he thought no one could find.

I had him pointed at. After days of nothing but his grain, the trace of a hand — for one held breath, I had the Surgeon at the end of a line I had drawn. And the line was getting shorter.

I felt the smile come. The first real one in days. *I've got you*, I thought. *You opened the door to show me you could reach her. And you forgot the oldest rule of the trade — the one I taught the man who taught you. Every door you open is a door someone can walk back through.*

I leaned in to draw the line shorter.

And the Surgeon — who had felt all of it, felt me read him, refuse the bait, decline the duel, turn his own open door into a thread leading back to his chair — did the one thing I hadn't prepared for. The one move I hadn't seen coming. Because it wasn't a move on the board at all.

He stopped playing.

And he sent me, on the line that hummed with Clara's breathing, a single private message.

No words.

A photograph.

It took my mind a moment to place it. An ordinary street in ordinary evening light. A fruit stall. A yellow wall. Laundry strung between two windows above a shop. A street I had walked a hundred times in another life. A street I would know in my sleep. In my grave.

It was Ifeoma's street.

In Lagos.
