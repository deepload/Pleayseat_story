# Chapter 1: Genesis

At 23:47 on a Tuesday in February, in a Brussels apartment that smelled of cold coffee and colder rain, my phone lit up with the one name I had spent two years training myself not to wait for.

Clara.

I want to tell you I was calm. I want to tell you that the man who is supposed to be a legend in certain rooms — the man other people whisper about when they think I can't hear — looked at that screen and felt nothing but professional readiness.

The truth is I knocked over a glass. The truth is my hands stopped working for a second, the way they do when the body knows something the mind hasn't caught up to yet. The truth is I sat there in the blue glow with my heart going like a fist against a door, because for two years that name had been a wound I kept reopening on purpose, and now it was a sound my phone was making, and the two things did not fit inside the same chest.

I picked up the glass first. I don't know why. Some animal part of me wanted the room to be in order before I let the rest of my life fall apart.

Then I opened the message.

It was four words and then it was nothing.

*They're coming. I'm sorry.*

And underneath, where a sane person would put a heart or a goodbye or an explanation, she had put a string of characters. A little run of letters and numbers, the kind of thing that means nothing to anyone in the world.

Except to me. Because Clara never sent anything by accident. Clara measured her words the way a surgeon measures a cut, the way a safecracker measures a click. If she sent you nonsense, it was the most important sentence she had ever written, and your whole life was now about learning to read it.

I typed back fast. *Where are you. Talk to me. I'm here.*

The little marks under my message turned grey. Delivered. Not read.

I typed again. *Clara. I'm here. I never stopped being here.*

Grey.

I called. It rang once — one single ring, the most hopeful and most terrible sound I have ever heard — and then it didn't ring anymore. It didn't go to voicemail. It didn't tell me the number was busy or unavailable or out of range. It simply stopped, the way a candle stops, the way a heartbeat stops, the way a door stops when it is closed from the other side by someone who does not want you to follow.

She was gone.

Not gone the way she'd been gone for two years, which was a slow ache I had built a whole careful life around. Gone the way a person is gone when the floor opens under them. Gone in real time. Gone while I watched, with a glass of spilled water spreading across my desk like something I should have been able to stop.

---

Let me tell you who I was, before that night, so you understand what that night turned me into.

I was a man who broke things.

Oh, I'd have used prettier words once. I was a finder of weaknesses. A tester of locks. A person who could look at the most carefully guarded thing in the world and see, instantly, the one small place where it had lied about being safe. For money, sometimes. For the principle of it, sometimes — there are a lot of liars in this world who deserve to have their locks tested. And too often, if I'm honest, and at this hour I might as well be, I did it to make a woman look at me the way Clara once had.

That was the flaw. I'll name it now and get it over with, because she would have wanted me to. When I fell for someone, I fell all the way to the bottom. I lost the thread of my own life. The job didn't matter, the plan didn't matter, the careful clever thing I'd been building didn't matter — only the feeling mattered, the great drowning warmth of it, and I would walk away from anything for one more day inside it. I had done it again and again. I had left brilliant things half-finished because someone had smiled at me across a room. I was the man who never got to the end of anything, because the middle was always too sweet to leave.

People who loved me learned, eventually, that I was a flood. Magnificent, maybe, for a season. And then gone, and the field underwater, and nothing to show for it but the high-water mark on the wall.

I had a family, though. I want you to know that, because it matters more than anything I'm ashamed of. Not a family of blood — I'd run out of that kind a long time ago — but a family I'd made, person by person, across a whole crooked planet. A man in Lagos who keeps the lights on for me and worries like a mother. A locksmith in Marseille who once tried to rob me and is now the bravest friend I have. A spook in Kyiv who trusts nothing, which is exactly why I trust him. And one woman in Tel Aviv who still answers when I call, even now, even after everything, because she is kinder than I deserve and because she has never quite let me go, and I have never quite let her, and that is its own long sad story for another night.

That was me. A flood with a family. A genius who never finished. A man who broke things for a living and broke people for free, mostly by loving them too much and then forgetting to stay.

And then there was Clara, who was the one exception. The one I never got bored of. The one who didn't dissolve in the feeling, because she *was* the feeling — exact, and brave, and real all the way down. She used to say I was the only person she'd ever met who paid attention as hard as she did. She had no idea. She was the only thing I had ever paid attention to without it costing me my soul.

We'd met three years before, in a desert city full of liars and lights, at the kind of gathering where people like us pretend to be strangers. I'll tell you that story properly someday. For now you only need to know one thing about it, and you'll need it later, so hold it: there was a date. A specific day, in a specific August, when two careful people who trusted no one looked at each other across a loud bright room and decided, against every rule they lived by, to trust just one person each.

Nobody else knew that date. Not her handlers, not my family, not the few ghosts who knew us both. It was ours. It was the one secret we never gave away, not because it was dangerous, but because it was the only thing we owned together, and you don't spend the only thing you own.

I'm telling you this now because it's about to matter.

---

I sat in the dark for a long time after the line went dead.

I'd like to say I leapt into action. I'd like to give you the legend. But what actually happened is that I sat in a chair in a cold apartment and I shook, and somewhere in there I think I prayed, which is something I don't do, to a God I'm not on speaking terms with, that I had misread the whole thing. That she'd ringed off because her battery died. That *they're coming* meant a courier, a delivery, a friend with wine. That *I'm sorry* meant she was sorry for the silence of two years and not sorry the way you're sorry when you're saying goodbye.

I knew better. I had always known better about Clara. That was the cruelty of loving someone smarter than you: you can never lie to yourself about them for long.

She was in trouble. The real kind. The kind that erases people.

I knew, the way you know a smell from childhood, the shape of what she'd been doing these last two years — the work she'd buried herself in, the thing she cared about more than her own breathing. Children. Somewhere out there in the machinery of the world, there were children moving through the dark inside boxes labelled *mercy*, and Clara had found the seam in it, the one small place where the most carefully guarded evil had lied about being good. She'd told me once, in the only real fight we ever had, in a voice that left no room: *Someone has to protect the children. If not the people who can see, then who?*

I had told her it would get her killed.

She had looked at me with those exact grey eyes and said: *Then it will have been worth it.*

And I had loved her so much in that moment, and been so afraid, that I'd done the most cowardly brilliant thing of my whole cowardly brilliant life: I'd let her go. I'd convinced myself that loving her meant not dragging her into my flood. That she was better without a man who never finished anything. That my staying away was a gift.

It wasn't a gift. It was just the same old flood, wearing a halo. I'd left, the way I always left. I'd just told myself a nobler story about it.

And now her name was grey on my screen, and a single ring had stopped, and four words sat there glowing — *They're coming. I'm sorry* — and underneath them, that little run of nonsense she'd sent on purpose, because Clara never sent anything by accident.

I looked at it for a long time.

And somewhere in that looking, something in me changed gears so hard I felt it in my teeth.

---

Here is the thing no one ever understood about me, including me, until that night.

The flaw and the gift were the same muscle.

The thing that scattered me — the totality, the drowning, the way I lost my whole self in a feeling and couldn't think about anything else — that was a curse only because I had never once pointed it at something worth not surviving. I had spent my life falling all the way to the bottom for people who, in the end, weren't worth the swim. And so I'd learned to call it a weakness. A pattern. A reason no one should rely on me.

But it isn't weakness to love completely. It's only weakness when you love something small.

And there was nothing small about this.

I stood up. I remember that. I remember standing up in the dark like a man getting out of water, and I remember that my hands had stopped shaking, and I remember thinking, very clearly, in a voice that didn't sound like my self-pity or my wit or any of the other voices I usually argue with: *I am going to find her. I am going to find her if I have to take the whole world apart with my bare hands.*

And then a colder voice — the old voice, the one from my old life — answered, sweet and reasonable: *You know how. You've always known how. You don't need to build anything. You have favours. You have ghosts. You have a dozen dirty ways to do this by Thursday.*

It was true. I could have done it the dirty way. I could have started breaking things by midnight, the way I used to — could have reached into the world's secrets with the old skeleton keys, could have hurt people who deserved hurting, could have become, for one righteous week, exactly the man I used to be.

And I would have lost her.

I knew it the way you know a true thing in your gut before your head can prove it. If I went after Clara as the man who breaks things, I would find a body, or a frame, or a trap. The people who'd taken her were not going to be beaten by a flood. They were going to be beaten by *proof*. By something so clean, so undeniable, so patient that no power on earth could wave it away. Clara had spent two years building a case nobody could destroy, against people nobody could touch, and she'd done it not with a weapon but with the truth, assembled so carefully it became a kind of blade.

If I wanted to save her, I had to stop being the man who took things.

I had to become a man who builds.

I didn't have what I needed. So I would make it. I would make a thing that had never existed — a thing that could hold the whole rotten machine in its hands and not blink. A thing that could listen to all the noise of the world and find one silence inside it that sounded like her. I didn't have a name for it yet. I didn't have a single line of it yet. I had a cold apartment, a spilled glass, a grey name, and four words.

It was enough.

I cleared my desk with one arm — papers, the dead glass, the whole sad museum of the man I'd been hiding as for two years, all of it onto the floor. I sat down. I cracked my knuckles, which is a stupid thing to do, and I knew it, and I did it anyway, because some rituals are just for telling yourself it's begun.

And I began to build.

I won't tell you how. You wouldn't want me to; it isn't that kind of story, and she wouldn't have wanted it either. All you need to know is what it felt like, which was the opposite of the flood. For the first time in my life the totality didn't scatter me. It aimed me. Every wild brilliant useless thing I had ever learned in a life of breaking other people's locks came pouring through one narrow door, and the door had her name on it. Hours went by like minutes. The rain stopped and started and stopped. Somewhere a church bell did its rounds and I didn't hear it. I forgot to eat, forgot to be afraid, forgot to be the man who never finishes anything — because for the first time there was something I would have died before leaving unfinished.

I was building her a way home. I didn't know yet that I was building the thing that a whole hidden world would one day fight over. I didn't know it would outlive this night, this woman, this war. I only knew that I had seven days in me, maybe less, before *they're coming* finished whatever it had started, and that I was going to spend every one of those hours making the impossible thing real.

And only when the grey light was coming up over the wet rooftops did I let myself go back to the one piece I'd been circling all night and refusing to look at straight. The little run of nonsense at the bottom of her message. The thing she'd sent on purpose, because Clara never sent anything by accident.

I'd assumed it was a name. A handle, a callsign, the name of whoever was coming for her. I'd half-built the whole night around finding that name.

I was wrong.

It wasn't a name at all. It was a lock — a small, perfect, patient lock, the kind only she could make and only I could open — and when I finally, with shaking hands, asked it the one question that two careful people had agreed to never tell another living soul, it opened.

The message was not a name. It was a key — and it opened to a date only the two of us ever knew.
