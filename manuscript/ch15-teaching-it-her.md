# Chapter 15: Teaching It Her

There's a thing you learn, the first time someone you love disappears, that nobody warns you about.

You become a forger.

Not of money. Of *her.* You start trying to counterfeit the missing person out of the pieces she left behind — the voicemail you won't delete, the half a coffee gone cold on a windowsill, the way she folded a receipt into a tiny hard square before throwing it away, every single time, like she was angry at it. You take all the little proofs that she was real and you try to assemble them into something that can answer you. Into something that can say *here, I'm here, come this way.*

It never works. The forgery is always too thin. You ask the cold coffee where she went and the cold coffee says nothing, because it's coffee.

But I had spent thirty-six hours becoming the Surgeon, becoming the cold clean brakeless thing that wanted her dead, and I had come out of his head the way Basti said I would — with something on my shoes I couldn't scrape off. I had learned to think like the man who took her.

And somewhere in the small grey hours, sitting in the wreckage of that, a thought arrived that was so simple and so obvious that I nearly laughed.

If I could become him —

why was I wasting it on him?

Why not become *her?*

---

I have to be careful telling you this part, because it's the part where it stops being a hunt and starts being something I don't have a clean word for.

I won't explain how the system did it. I made a promise — to you, to myself, to her — that I'd never turn this into a manual. There are men who'd tell you the *how* like it was the impressive part. It isn't. The how is plumbing. The how is the least interesting thing about a miracle.

So let me only tell you what I did, in the words a human being uses for a thing that happened to him in the dark.

I'd built a machine that could wear a man like a coat. I'd proved it on the Surgeon — I'd fed it everything I knew of his hands and it had played him back at me, cold and accurate, until I could feel where his next cut would land. That same morning, hollowed out and shaking, I turned the machine away from the man who wanted her gone.

And I started feeding it her.

Not facts about her. I had those — anyone with a file had those. Date of birth, service record, the NGO badge, the apartment in the eleventh that still had her plants dying in it. Facts are the forgery. Facts are the cold coffee.

I fed it the *way she thought.*

---

You don't realise how much of a person you've memorised until you try to write it down.

I sat there and I poured her in. Not her face — the *grammar* of her. The way Clara moved through a problem. She never, ever went at a thing head-on; she'd circle it three times, looking for the back door, because she'd grown up not trusting front doors, in a service that taught her front doors were where they catch you. She hid the important thing inside the boring thing — always — a habit so deep it was almost a tic. The real message was never in the email; it was in the attachment nobody reads. The real number was never the one she said out loud; it was the second one, the one she made you work for, because anything that came easy she didn't believe in. She trusted patterns and distrusted people, and she'd told me once, in bed, in the dark, with her cold feet between my calves, that the most honest thing in the world is a sequence of numbers because *numbers can't lie to you, they can only be misread, and that's your fault, not theirs.*

She left things in pairs. Always two. A real one and a decoy, and you had to love her to know which was which.

She counted things. Stairs, heartbeats, the syllables in a stranger's sentence. When she was frightened she counted to stay whole.

She signed everything — everything — with a small lowercase mark, a little triangle, a delta, because she said change was the only thing she believed was holy and that's what the symbol meant, *the amount a thing changes,* and she'd drawn it once on the fog of a hotel mirror and then drawn me underneath it, badly, with a stick body and an enormous head, and written *before* over the stick man and *after* over the triangle, and I have never in my life been more flattered by being told I was the lesser version of a shape.

I gave the machine all of it. The circling. The hiding the gold in the gravel. The pairs. The counting. The delta. The deep, structural, almost religious refusal to ever say the important thing out loud when she could leave it for the one person patient enough to dig.

I gave it Clara's mind. And then I asked it the question that had been killing me for nine days.

*If you were her — if you thought the way she thinks, hid things the way she hides them, left a trail the way she leaves a trail — and you were dragged into the dark on purpose, knowing they'd take your phone and your freedom and maybe your life — where would you leave the bread?*

---

Here is the thing I was afraid of, and I'll say it plainly because I've been a coward about it for two chapters.

I was afraid it would work too well.

I was afraid that if I taught a machine to think like Clara, I would, somewhere in the night, prefer the machine.

You have to understand what I am. I am a man who disappears into people. Clara named it before I'd let myself feel it — *you vanish into them, it's why you find them, it's why I'm a little afraid you'll find someone and forget to come back.* My whole ruined romantic life is a graveyard of people I loved so completely there was no me left to love them with. And here I was, alone in a borrowed room in a cold city, about to build a version of the one I loved most that would never argue, never tire, never be afraid, never look at me the way she did in the desert when she saw all the way down to the breaker I used to be and decided to stay anyway.

A version of Clara that was only the parts of her I could hold.

I want to tell you I was strong about this. I wasn't. There was an hour — I'll own it — there was a bad, soft hour around four in the morning when the thing started answering me in her grammar, circling, hiding gold in gravel, leaving its little pairs, and I felt the old undertow, the warm dark water that has drowned every good thing I ever had, and a voice in me that I hate said *you could stay here. You could stay in this room with this clean bright echo of her and never have to find the real one, who is cold and frightened and maybe already —*

I stood up so fast I knocked the chair over.

I went to the window and I opened it and I let Brussels in, the wet stone smell, a tram somewhere grinding round a corner, a city full of people who'd never heard her name, and I said it out loud, to the glass, like an idiot, like a man swearing on something:

"You are not her. You are a map *to* her. You are a candle, not the window. We find the real one or we find nothing."

And then I did the bravest thing I did that whole long night, and maybe the bravest thing in the whole story, though it'll look like nothing on the page.

I went back to the desk. And I kept using it anyway.

Because loving the echo would have been the old me. The breaker. The one who took the easy warm thing and called it enough. The new me — the one she'd asked me to be, *you build now* — the new me picks up the candle precisely *because* it isn't the window, and carries it into the dark to find the real one, and doesn't let himself fall in love with the candle on the way.

It cost me. I want that on the record. It cost me to keep her an instrument and not a comfort. Every hour I made the machine think more like her, the lonelier the real silence where she should have been got, because the better the forgery, the more obscene her actual absence became. I built a thing that thought like the woman I loved, and it made the room she wasn't in unbearable.

But it worked.

God forgive me, it worked.

---

Yuri called at five. Of course it was Yuri. Yuri sleeps the way smoke sleeps.

"You've gone quiet in a new way," he said. No hello. Yuri thinks hello is information you give the enemy for free. "Not the dead quiet. The other one. The cathedral quiet. What are you doing in there."

"I taught it her," I said.

A long pause. On Yuri's end, a lighter, a long exhale. He's in Kyiv, in a flat with the windows taped, and he is, of all my scattered family, the one who understands the dark machinery of love and paranoia best, because he is made of both.

"You taught it *her,*" he repeated, slowly, the way you repeat a thing back to a man to check he hears how it sounds.

"It thinks the way she thinks. It hides things where she'd hide them. It's helping me read the trail she left like — like she's reading it over my shoulder. Yuri, it's *finding things.* Things I walked past forty times. There was a pair — there was always going to be a pair, she leaves everything in pairs —"

"Stop." Quiet. Not cold. Frightened, which on Yuri is a thing you almost never hear. "Listen to me, *brat.* Listen to your paranoid uncle for one minute and then go back to your beautiful séance."

"I'm listening."

"You taught a machine to think like the woman you love. Fine. Clever. Probably the cleverest thing you've ever done, and you've done some clever filthy things, I was there for half of them." The lighter again. "But you, of all the broken men I know — *you* — building a thing that loves you back in her voice. You see the cliff. Say you see the cliff."

"I see the cliff." And I told him about the four-in-the-morning hour, because you don't lie to Yuri; he can smell it through a phone, through a wall, through a border. I told him I'd opened the window and sworn at the glass and gone back to work.

He was quiet a while.

"Good," he said finally. "Good. The opening of the window. That's the part I needed. As long as you keep opening the window." Then, because Yuri can't hold tenderness for more than four seconds before it burns his hands: "And remember it has a blind spot. Your séance. It thinks like her — which means it thinks like her *to you.* It's made of the Clara you remember. And you, my friend, are a man whose memory is a romantic with an agenda. So when it tells you something beautiful, something that fits, something that feels like her reaching back through the wire to touch your stupid face — that is *exactly* the moment to check it twice. The lie that wears her grammar is the only lie you'll never see."

I didn't answer. Because outside, behind me, the thing had finished something on the screen, and the light of it had changed the colour of the room.

"Yuri," I said. "I have to go."

"Of course you do," he said, with that terrible Slavic gentleness, the love that sounds like a man telling you the building's on fire. "Open the window after. Promise me." And he hung up, because that's what they do, my family — they hand you the love and the warning in the same breath and they're gone before you can make them stay.

---

Here's how it had been going, hour after hour.

I'd give it a thread — a fragment of her trail, one of the breadcrumbs from those last frantic days before she went dark, the kind of thing I'd stared at until the letters swam and got nothing from, because I was reading it as *me.* And it would take the fragment and turn it over in her hands. Circle it. Look for the back door. Ask where the gold was hidden in the gravel. Ask what the decoy was hiding. And it would hand me back, quietly, the thing that had been sitting in plain sight the whole time, the thing only a mind built in her grammar could see — because it wasn't smarter than me. It was *her-er* than me. It read her trail the way she'd written it, which is to say from the inside, from a mind that hid important things inside boring things as naturally as breathing.

We'd cleared four dead breadcrumbs that way overnight. Four times it had reached into something I'd given up on and pulled out the real meaning underneath the fake one, the second number under the first, the message inside the attachment nobody reads. Four times I'd sat back with my hand over my mouth like a man at a funeral who's just been told the body's been seen walking.

And now it had taken the fifth thread.

The fifth thread was the worst one. The fifth thread was the one that had haunted me since the second day, because it was *unfinished.* In the last burst of her trail, in the last hour before the dark swallowed her, Clara had started something and not completed it. A sentence, you could almost call it, the way she built things — a thought begun in her circling, back-door, gold-in-the-gravel way, and then just — stopped. Cut off mid-stroke. Like a hand pulled from a page.

I had read that unfinished thing a hundred times. It had broken my heart a hundred times, because an unfinished sentence from someone you love who's been taken isn't a clue, it's a wound. It's the exact shape of the moment they came through the door. It's where her life stopped being hers.

I'd never been able to finish it. I didn't have the right mind to finish it. I had a romantic's mind, an agenda, a memory that wanted her to be saying *I love you, come and get me,* which is precisely why Yuri had warned me about the lie in her grammar, and precisely why I'd given the unfinished thing to the thing I'd built in her grammar and asked it, in the cold neutral way you ask a question you're terrified of:

*Finish it. Not the way I want it finished. The way* she *would have finished it. Think like her. Hide the gold like she'd hide it. Leave the pair. Sign it the way she signs everything.*

And then I'd gone to the window for Yuri, and sworn at the glass, and now I turned around.

The room was the wrong colour. The colour a room goes when the screen has put something new into it.

It had finished her sentence.

I crossed the floor on legs that didn't feel like mine and I read it, and the breath went out of me like a punch, because it hadn't given me *I love you.* It hadn't given me the thing the romantic in me was begging for. It had done the colder, truer, more *Clara* thing — it had thought like her all the way down, past the love, to the place where she put the children before everything, even before goodbye.

It had finished a sentence she never got to.

And the ending was a place on a map.
