# Chapter 15: Teaching It Her

There's something nobody tells you the first time someone you love disappears without warning.

You become a counterfeiter.

Not of money. Of *her.* You try to reconstruct her from the pieces she left behind. The voicemail you can't bring yourself to delete. The half-cup of cold coffee on the windowsill. The way she folded every receipt into a tight little square before throwing it away, like she was punishing it. You collect every proof that she existed. You try to build something that answers back. Something that says: *I'm here. Come find me.*

It never works. The fake is always too thin. You ask the cold coffee where she went. The cold coffee says nothing. It's coffee.

But I'd spent thirty-six hours becoming the Surgeon. Crawling into the corners of the network that no search engine ever lights — those windowless rooms where nobody arrives with a face, just a false name and a reputation built across years. Where people trade stolen secrets in the dark like holy relics. I had become the cold, brakeless thing that wanted her dead. And I had come out of it exactly the way Basti promised — with something stuck to the bottom of my shoes I couldn't scrape off. I had learned to think like the man who took her.

And then, in those grey hours before dawn, sitting in the wreckage, the screen my only sun, a thought came to me. So simple I almost laughed.

If I could become him —

why waste it on him?

Why not become *her?*

---

I have to be careful here. Even inside my own head. This is the moment it stops being a hunt and becomes something I don't have a word for.

I won't explain how the system worked. I promised myself that. Promised her. No instruction manual, ever. Some men would narrate the *how* like that was the impressive part — the same men who, at three in the morning, show off their latest trick in some closed room so someone will pat their shoulder. The how is plumbing. It's the least interesting thing about a miracle.

I'll only say what I did. In the words of a man to whom something happened in the dark.

I had built a machine that could put a man on like a coat. I'd proved it on the Surgeon. Fed it everything I knew about his hands — every signature he'd ever left in our old haunts, every alias he'd burned through over the years. It had played him back at me, cold and accurate, until I could feel where his next cut would fall. That morning, hollowed out and trembling, I turned the machine away from the man who wanted her gone.

And I started feeding it Clara.

Not facts about her. Those I already had. Anyone with a file had those. Date of birth. Service record. The NGO badge. The apartment in the eleventh arrondissement where her plants were still dying. Facts are the forgery. Facts are the cold coffee.

I fed it *the way she thought.*

---

You don't know how much of a person you've memorized until you try to write it down.

I sat there and poured her in whole. Not her face. The *grammar* of her.

The way she moved through a problem. Never head-on. She circled it three times. She looked for the back door. She'd grown up not trusting front doors, in a profession where the front door is where they catch you. She always hid the important thing inside the unimportant thing. Always. It was almost a reflex. The real message was never in the letter — it was in the attachment nobody reads. The real number was never the one she said out loud. It was the second one. The one she made you earn. Anything that came easy, she didn't trust.

She trusted patterns. She distrusted people. What they call operational paranoia in our world. For her it was a second spine. One night, in the dark, her cold feet slipped between my calves, she told me the most honest thing in the world was a sequence of numbers. *Numbers can't lie to you. They can only be misread. And that's your fault, not theirs.*

She left things in pairs. Always two. A real one and a decoy. You had to love her to know which was which.

She counted. Stairs. Heartbeats. The syllables in a stranger's sentence. When she was afraid, she counted to stay in one piece.

She signed everything. Everything. With a tiny mark. A small triangle. A δ. She said change was the only thing she held sacred, and that was the symbol's meaning: *the amount by which something changes.* It was her signature. Her seal. The mark she left where others leave a name — and where people like us leave nothing at all.

One evening, she drew it in the fog on a hotel mirror. Her δ, small and clean. And beside it, a large Δ. Same letter, uppercase. She pressed her finger to one, then the other. *You,* she said for the Δ. *Me,* for the δ. The big and the small. Then under my Δ she sketched a stick figure with an enormous head. Wrote *before* above my loud capital. Wrote *after* above her quiet lowercase. I have never in my life been more flattered than in that moment — being told I was the crude, hollow version of a shape she was the real version of.

I gave the machine all of it. The circling. The gold hidden in the gravel. The pairs. The counting. The δ. Her deep, almost religious refusal to ever say the important thing out loud when she could leave it for the one person patient enough to dig.

I gave it Clara's mind. And I asked it the question that had been killing me for six days.

*If you were her. If you thought the way she thinks, hid things the way she hides them, left a trail the way she leaves a trail. And you were dragged into the dark on purpose, knowing they'd take your phone, your freedom, maybe your life. Where would you leave the breadcrumbs?*

---

Here is what I was afraid of. I'll say it plainly, because I was a coward about it for two days.

I was afraid it would work too well.

I was afraid that if I taught a machine to think like Clara, I would, somewhere in the night, come to prefer the machine.

You have to understand what I am. I am a man who disappears into people. That's even why they say I'm the best at finding someone — I become them. I live in their margins. I breathe their hidden air. Clara named it before I let myself feel it. *You vanish into them. That's why you find them. And it's why I'll always be a little afraid you'll find someone and forget to come back.*

My whole ruined love life is a graveyard. People I loved so completely there was nothing left of me to love them with. And here I was, alone in a borrowed room in a cold city. The screen glowing like a vigil candle over a corpse. About to build a version of the woman I loved most — one that would never argue, never tire, never be afraid. One that would never look at me the way she had in the desert, when she saw all the way down to the lock-breaker I used to be and chose to stay anyway.

A version of Clara that was only the parts of her I could hold.

I would have liked to be strong about this. I wasn't. There was an hour — I'll own it — a bad, soft hour around four in the morning. That hour every person who lives nights in front of a screen eventually knows. The world asleep. Nothing left but you and the cold light and what it reflects back. The machine started answering me in her grammar. Circling. Hiding gold in the gravel. Leaving its little pairs. And I felt the old undertow. The warm black water that has drowned everything good I ever had. A voice I hate whispered: *you could stay here. Stay in this room with this clean, bright echo of her. Never have to find the real one, who is cold and frightened and maybe already —*

I stood up so fast I knocked the chair over.

I went to the window. Opened it. Let Brussels in. The smell of wet stone. A tram grinding around a corner somewhere. A city full of people who had never heard her name. And I said it out loud, to the glass, like an idiot, like a man swearing on something:

"You are not her. You are a map that leads *to* her. You are a candle, not the window. We find the real one or we find nothing."

Then I did the bravest thing of that whole night. Maybe the bravest thing in this whole story. Even if, in memory, it doesn't look like much.

I went back to the desk. And I kept using it anyway.

Because loving the echo would have been the old me. The man who forced doors. The one who took the easy warm thing and declared it enough. The new me — the one she had asked me to be, *now you build* — that one picks up the candle precisely *because* it isn't the window. He walks into the dark to find the real one. And he doesn't fall in love with the candle on the way.

It cost me. I want to keep that. It cost me to hold her as a tool and not a comfort. Every hour I made the machine think more like her, the real silence — the place where she should have been — grew lonelier. The better the forgery, the more obscene her absence became. I built a thing that thought like the woman I loved. And it made the room she wasn't in unbearable.

But it worked.

God forgive me, it worked.

---

Yuri called at five. Of course it was Yuri. He sleeps the way smoke sleeps. He calls on a line that changes its face every week — never the same one twice, burned after use like a cigarette. He lives as though everyone is already on his back. Knowing him, they probably are.

"You've gone quiet in a new way," he said. No hello. Yuri considers hello free intelligence handed to the enemy. "Not the quiet of the dead. The other one. Cathedral quiet. What are you building in there."

"I taught it Clara," I said.

A long silence. On his end, a lighter. A long exhale. He's in Kyiv — an apartment with windows sealed with tape, behind a door three locks never fully convince him is enough. Of all my scattered family, all the people I've chosen for myself across four continents and the seams of the network, he's the one who best understands the dark machinery of love and paranoia. Because he is made of both.

"You taught it *Clara,*" he repeated. Slowly. The way a man repeats something back so the speaker can hear how it sounds.

"It thinks the way she thinks. It hides things where she'd hide them. It helps me read the trail she left — like she's reading it over my shoulder. Yuri, it's *finding things.* Things I walked past forty times. There was a pair — there was always going to be a pair, she leaves everything in pairs —"

"Stop." Quietly. Not coldly. Frightened. On Yuri, you almost never hear that, and when you do, you pay attention. "Listen to me, *brat.* Give your paranoid uncle one minute. Then go back to your beautiful seance."

"I'm listening."

"You taught a machine to think like the woman you love. Fine. Clever. Probably the cleverest thing you've ever done — and you've done some clever, ugly things in corners of the network most people don't know exist. I was there for half of them." The lighter again. "But *you.* Of all the broken men I know — *you* — building a thing that loves you back in her voice. Tell me you see the cliff. Say it."

"I see the cliff." And I told him about the four a.m. hour. Because you don't lie to Yuri. He smells it through a phone, through a wall, through a border, through any layer of encryption you think you've put between yourself and him. I told him I'd opened the window, sworn at the glass, gone back to work.

He was quiet for a moment.

"Good," he said finally. "Good. The open window. That's the piece I needed. As long as you keep going." Then, because Yuri can't hold tenderness for more than four seconds before it burns his hands: "And remember — it has a blind spot. It thinks like her, which means it thinks like her *for you.* You are a man whose memory lies beautifully. So when it tells you the perfect thing, the thing that feels exactly like her reaching back through time — that is *precisely* the moment to check twice. The lie that wears her grammar is the only lie you'll never see."

I didn't answer. Because behind me, in the dark of the room, the machine had just finished something on the screen. And its light had changed the color of the walls.

"Yuri," I said. "I have to go."

"Of course you do," he said, with that terrible Slavic tenderness. The kind of love that sounds like a man telling you the building is on fire. "Open the window after. Promise me." And he hung up. The line went dead in one blink, like a blown flame. Because that's what they do, the people I've chosen for family. They hand me love and warning in the same breath. And they're gone before I can hold them.

---

Here's how it had been going, hour by hour through the night.

I'd give it a thread. A fragment of her trail. One of the breadcrumbs from those last frantic days before she went dark — the kind of thing I'd stared at until the letters swam and my eyes burned and I got nothing. Because I was reading it as *me.*

It would take the fragment and turn it over in her hands. Circle it. Look for the back door. Ask where the gold was hidden in the gravel. Ask what the decoy was concealing. And it would hand back to me, quietly, the thing that had been sitting in plain sight the whole time. The thing only a mind built in her grammar could see. Because it wasn't smarter than me. It was *more her* than me. It read her trail the way she'd written it — from the inside, from a mind that hid important things inside boring things as naturally as breathing.

We had cleared four dead breadcrumbs that way through the night. Four times it reached into something I'd written off and pulled out the real meaning underneath the false one. The second number under the first. The message in the attachment nobody reads. Four times I sat back with my hand over my mouth, like a man at a funeral who has just been told the body was seen walking.

And now it had taken the fifth thread.

The fifth thread was the worst. It had haunted me since the second day, because it was *unfinished.* In the last flare of her trail, in the last hour before the dark swallowed her — before she vanished from the network the way a plug gets yanked from a wall, a kill switch thrown on an entire life — Clara had started something and not finished it. A sentence, almost. Built the way she built things. A thought begun in her circling, back-door, gold-in-the-gravel way. And then simply stopped. Cut off mid-stroke. Like a hand pulled from a page.

I had read that unfinished thing a hundred times. It had broken something in me each time. Because an unfinished sentence from someone you love, cut off when they were taken, isn't a clue. It's a wound. It's the exact shape of the moment they came through the door. It's where her life stopped being hers.

I had never been able to finish it. I didn't have the right mind. I had a romantic's mind — a memory that wanted her to be saying *I love you, come and find me.* Which is exactly why Yuri warned me about the lie in her grammar. And exactly why I had given the unfinished thing to the machine I had built in her grammar, and asked it, in the cold flat way you ask a question that terrifies you:

*Finish it. Not the way I want it finished. The way* she *would have finished it. Think like her. Hide the gold the way she'd hide it. Leave the pair. Sign it the way she signs everything.*

Then I had gone to the window for Yuri. Sworn at the glass. And now I turned back.

The room was the wrong color. The color a room goes when the screen has put something new into it.

It had finished her sentence.

On the screen, in that machine font that doesn't know how to tremble, six lines. Clara's grammar, exact, as though she had typed it herself.

```
> achève_comme_elle()
  premier_chiffre : 14   (le leurre, dit tout haut)
  second_chiffre  : 41   (le vrai, mérité)
  paire : { vieux_port, "près de la vieille eau" }
  les_47 d'abord. toujours. même avant l'adieu.
  — δ
```

To anyone else it would have been noise. A machine talking to itself in the dark. Not to me. I saw what it meant, and what it meant dropped me to my knees. She had left her pair — always a pair, always — a false and a true. She had said her number out loud and hidden the only one that mattered right behind it. And she had signed it δ. Her little mark. Not a machine imitating. Clara, reading over my shoulder, reaching back through six days of silence, handing me the thing in the only language she ever trusted. And in the middle of it, like a slap: what she had placed before me, before goodbye, before everything else.

The children.

I crossed the floor on legs that didn't feel like mine. Read it again. The breath left me like a fist. Because she hadn't given me *I love you.* She hadn't given me what the romantic in me was begging to hear. She had done the colder thing. The truer thing. The most *Clara* thing possible. She had thought like herself all the way to the end. Past love. To the place where she put forty-seven children before anything else. Even before goodbye.

She had finished a sentence she never had time to finish.

And the ending was a place on a map.
