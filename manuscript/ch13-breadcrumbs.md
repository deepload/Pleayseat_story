# Chapter 13: Breadcrumbs

The system told me my door was next. Then it went quiet. It waited for me to do something human with that news.

So I did the most human thing I know.

I made coffee. I burned my hand on the pot. I swore in three languages at a radiator that had no opinion on the subject.

Then I stood at the window in Brussels and watched the rain fall. Somewhere on that street, a man might already be picking the right hour to come for me.

A warning like that should make a man run. It didn't. It froze me solid.

Old stillness. Cold, careful, exact. I'd worn it like a second skin in the life I buried — the one that used to come on right before I went somewhere I had no business going. Back when my name was just a handle whispered in closed rooms. Rooms without walls, where faces don't exist, where you know each other only by what you call yourself in the dark. Back when I made my living opening doors that weren't mine.

But I don't go to those places anymore. I find people. I bring them home.

Standing there with my burned hand wrapped around a cup I couldn't taste, I understood something.

If they were coming for me, the hunt was working. Nobody kicks down a man's door to make him quit unless he's getting close. The threat signed in Clara's hand. The fire the Surgeon set. And now this cold calculation: *his door is next.* That wasn't the network showing strength. That was the network showing fear.

I'd rattled something. If they wanted me gone, it meant I was finally somewhere worth being.

So I didn't run. I sat back down in front of the thing I'd built — the only light in the room, the thing a machine had helped me make in seven sleepless days for exactly this moment. And I asked it to do the one job it does better than any creature that breathes.

Pick up the trail.

---

I need to say what a trail actually is. Because it isn't what the films tell you.

In the films there's a clue. One clue. A matchbook, a lipstick print on a glass, a single perfect thread that the detective pulls, and the whole thing unravels right in his clever hands.

Real life doesn't work that way. Real life doesn't give you one clue. It gives you ten thousand. Scattered across the whole world, mixed in with ten million others that mean nothing at all. And it dares you to tell the difference.

A lunch photo someone posted online, and in the background, soft and out of focus, a van. A complaint buried in some dead forum about a delivery that arrived late to a warehouse that wasn't supposed to receive anything. A message dropped at three in the morning by someone who thought they were alone in the dark. A file nobody noticed — a leak thrown out six months ago by a furious employee, that everyone believed was dead. A charity brochure, all smiles and clean fonts, with a map on page four that was quietly, fatally wrong. A plane landing where no plane should. A boat moving when boats sleep. A name spelled one way here, another way there, by a tired clerk who will never know what he did.

None of it is hidden. That's what nobody understands. The world isn't a locked safe. The world is a beach. Everything is right there on the sand, in plain sight, in front of everyone, all the time.

The men who do terrible things don't bury their crumbs in a vault. They don't hide them deep in the dark web either, in those nameless markets where people think shadow erases everything. No. They drop them on the beach, with everyone else's. And they count on the size of the beach to keep them safe.

Who could ever sift a whole beach? Who has the patience? Who could hold ten thousand grains of sand in their head at once and feel the three that are warm?

I know who. Or rather — I know what.

I stayed there in the dark, the screen the only light, that warm hum of machines that never sleep. And I gave the thing I'd built the entire beach.

All of it. Every crumb the network had dropped without meaning to. Every careless lunch photo, every late delivery, every crooked map, every borrowed name. The whole scattered, broken, half-erased trail of something that thought it was invisible because it was hiding in plain sight.

I gave it the beach. And I asked it the only way I know how to ask — like someone's life depended on it. Because someone's did.

*Find the warm grains. Bring me the three that are hers.*

Then I did the hardest thing in the world.

I waited.

---

Waiting is the part they never show.

It's the worst part. Worse than danger. Danger at least gives you something to do with your hands. Waiting leaves you alone in a small room with everything you've ever done wrong. And at three in the morning, everything I'd ever done wrong formed an orderly line and took its turn.

Three in the morning is the hour for people like me. When the world sleeps and we stay awake in the blue glow. Cold coffee at the bottom of the cup. The hum of machines the way other people listen to the sea.

I thought about Clara. I let myself — one minute, which I almost never allow, because that door is hard to close once it's open.

I thought about her laugh. Rare, which made it worth a fortune. I thought about her hands. I thought about the last real morning, before any of this, before the word *mercy* became something with teeth. The two of us, a window, nowhere to be. And her, chin on my chest, saying:

*You know what's wrong with you. You love the way a house burns. All at once. Nothing left.*

I didn't argue. Because she was right. She was always right. And because that morning, I'd have burned to the foundations with a full heart if it kept her in the light.

That was the flaw. She'd named it before I could. My totality. The way I fall all the way down and forget who I was supposed to be. It had killed every good thing I'd ever had. A dozen times, in a dozen cities, I'd let the feeling dissolve the mission. *All at once. Nothing left.*

But here is what I was just learning, at thirty-something, with my burned hand and my heart in my throat.

A flaw is just a strength aimed the wrong way. That same totality that scattered me like ash across the world — point it at one thing, one woman, one impossible task, and it stops being a weakness. It becomes the thing nobody else has. An attention that doesn't waver. A love that doesn't get tired, doesn't get bored, never once looks away.

I was the only man alive who could not stop looking for Clara Dubois. They had built an entire network on the comfortable belief that everyone eventually looks away.

They had never met me.

The screen changed.

---

It started the way it always starts. The world got smaller.

I couldn't have explained the how, and I wouldn't have tried. The how never mattered. That night I wasn't watching the how anyway. I was watching the *what.* And the what was this: the whole impossible beach, beginning to narrow.

Like a tide going out. Like a fist closing.

At first it was everywhere. Every country, every coast. Every crumb glowing faint on the map of the dark, like coals under ash.

Then it wasn't everywhere. It was the south. The warm south. The Mediterranean south. The south where humanitarian convoys ran and smiling brochures pointed, where money entered clean and came out dirty — laundered through charity tokens passed hand to hand, and no hand got soiled.

The crumbs were clustering. The warm grains were finding each other, the way iron filings find a magnet. Three here, five there, a dozen along a coastal road. All of them leaning. All of them pointing south.

And then the south got smaller too.

I leaned forward. My breath fogged the glass. I didn't wipe it.

I'd watched this happen enough times to feel it in my body before I read it with my eyes. Like a name sitting on the tip of your tongue a half-second before you say it. The crumbs weren't pointing south anymore. They were pointing at a *place.* One specific place. A harbour place, an old place. Stone and salt and a thousand years of people arriving and vanishing. A place where a man could disappear a hundred children into the noise of a hundred thousand ordinary lives, and trust the crowd to keep his secret.

A city I knew. A city I had sworn, on one bad night a long time ago, I would never set foot in again.

Marseille.

I said it out loud to the empty room, the way you say a name you've been dreading. *Marseille.* The radiator ticked. The rain fell. And the warm grains on the screen all leaned toward the old port like sunflowers turning toward the sun.

I went very still. I felt the floor of my old life open up beneath the new one.

Of course it was Marseille. It was always going to be Marseille. The route in her last report ran through it. The man I'd buried — the Surgeon, my dark mirror, the brother I'd helped shape, one of ours before he became one of theirs — he'd cut his teeth there, same as me.

And I had family there. I had *Basti* there. The smuggler who became a locksmith. A laugh like a building coming down. The man who once carried me four blocks on two broken ribs and never once let me apologize for the weight.

I'd have to call Basti.

Which meant bringing him into the fire.

I sat with that for a while. Cold cup in my burned hand. Because this is the price of the family I'd made — the people you don't choose by blood, but by debts, by sleepless nights, by secrets kept in the dark. Behind every door I knocked on, there was someone. Someone who loved me. Someone who would say yes the instant I asked. And every yes I collected was another person I was walking toward a man who sets fires near the people you love, just to watch you break.

*One of them,* I thought. *One of them is going to get hurt because of me. And I don't know which one. And I would give everything I have not to find out.*

I buried the thought the way I bury everything — badly, with both hands. And I went back to the screen. Because the narrowing wasn't done.

---

The city wasn't the end. That was what I learned that night — its cruelty and its gift, both at once.

A city is still a million doors. You can shout her name in the middle of Marseille until your voice gives out. The city swallows it like a stone dropped in the sea.

I didn't need a city. I needed a street. A door. The one warm window in a wall of cold ones.

So I let it keep going. I let the fist close.

And it did. God help me, it did. The crumbs that had all leaned south, all leaned toward Marseille — they kept going. Kept finding each other in the dark. And the city on the screen began to do what the world had done, what the south had done.

It shrank.

A district. Then a corner of a district. Then a handful of streets near the old water — the kind of streets forgotten on purpose, where a building can sit empty for years and nobody asks why, where men who run the world's quiet horrors like to work because the world stopped looking there a long time ago.

I had said it once, casually, that the thing I'd built could narrow an entire country to a single street by morning. I said it the way you brag about something you love. But I watched it happen that night and I stopped bragging. Because it isn't something to brag about. It's something to pray over.

By the time the rain thinned and the first grey crept over the Brussels rooftops, the beach was gone. The whole world of ten thousand scattered crumbs was gone. There was only the warm south, the old port, and a forgotten knot of streets near the water. And at the centre of all of it — glowing faint, stubborn, and *warm* — the place where every crumb had leaned. The place where all the bread led. The place I would be standing before the day was out, even if I had to walk there on my hands.

One block. One building, maybe. The end of the trail.

I should have felt triumph. I felt something colder and better than triumph. I felt *close.*

I felt her. Nearly a thousand kilometres to the south, in a room I could almost picture now. Behind a wall I could almost touch. And I put my burned hand flat against the cold glass — the way I'd done once before — like an animal, like a fool, like a man who hasn't held the person he loves in far too long and has just been told which window the light is behind.

*I'm coming,* I told the warm place in the south. *I found your bread. Every crumb. Hold on. I'm coming.*

---

The thing I'd built doesn't get tired. Doesn't sleep. Doesn't let itself hope. Never falls in love.

So it did the one more thing I hadn't asked for.

It gave me a face.

Among all those crumbs — the lunch photos, the crooked maps, the moments dropped carelessly by a network that thought the size of the beach would protect it — there were images. There always are. The world is drowning in images. Everyone photographs everything. And somewhere in that flood, on some street in the south, a stranger with a phone had taken a picture of nothing in particular, posted it for nobody, and never once known what they'd caught at the edge of the frame.

The thing I'd built caught it for them. It felt the warm grain in the cold sand. It pulled one photo out of ten million. And it placed it, quietly, at the centre of my screen.

Three days old. The timestamp in the corner said so. Plain and cold. Three days.

And in it — at the edge, half-turned, caught by accident in a stranger's careless frame, on a street near the old water — was a woman.

My breath stopped. The radiator ticked. The grey rose over the rooftops, and I didn't see it.

It was her.

Clara. Three days ago. *Alive.* Not a memory I'd been nursing in the dark. Not a heartbeat I'd invented to stay standing. Clara — in the world, in the light, in a street in the south, chin up, eyes doing the thing her eyes do. Alive three days ago. Which meant, by every prayer I had left, alive right now.

I would have wept. I think I started to.

And then I saw the rest of the photo. The part behind her. The reason the thing I'd built had pulled this single frame from ten million and laid it before me — as an offering, and as a warning.

Behind her. Close. Half a step back. Watching her the way a keeper watches.

A face.

A face I knew.
