# Chapter 13: Breadcrumbs

The thing told me my own front door was next, and then it sat there in the dark, screen-glow on my face, waiting for me to do something human about it.

I did the most human thing I know. I made coffee, burned my hand on the pot, swore in three languages at a radiator that had no opinion on the matter, and stood at the window of the Brussels apartment watching the rain come down on a street where, somewhere, a man might already be deciding the best hour to come for me.

A warning like that should have made a man run. It didn't. It made me still. The same stillness as always — the old, cold, careful stillness I'd worn like a second skin in the life I'd buried, the one that used to come on right before I went somewhere I had no business going. Back when I was a handle and not a name. Back when I lived in the seams of the world with everyone else who'd gone dark, the way a man lives in a country with no flag. Except I don't go places like that anymore. I find people. I bring them home.

And I had just understood something, standing there with my scalded hand around a cup I couldn't taste.

If they were coming for me, it meant the hunt was working. Nobody kicks down a man's door to make him stop unless he's getting close. The threat signed in Clara's own hand, the fire the Surgeon had set, and now this — the cold arithmetic that said *his front door is next* — they weren't the network being strong. They were the network being *scared.* I'd rattled something. The fact that they wanted me gone meant I was finally somewhere worth being.

So I didn't run. I sat back down in front of the thing I'd built, the only light in the room, the thing a machine had helped me make in seven sleepless days for exactly this, and I asked it to do the one job it does better than any creature that breathes.

I asked it to pick up the trail.

---

Here is what a trail is, because it isn't what the films pretend.

In the films there's a clue. One clue. A matchbook from a nightclub, a lipstick on a glass, a single perfect thread that the detective tugs and the whole sweater of the case unravels in his clever hands. Real life isn't like that. Real life doesn't give one clue. Real life gives ten thousand of them, scattered across the whole wide world, mixed in with ten million things that mean nothing at all, and it dares a man to tell the difference.

A photograph somebody posted of their lunch, and in the background, out of focus, a van. A complaint on some forgotten forum — the kind of place that loads slow and smells of cigarettes even through a screen — about a delivery that came late to a warehouse that shouldn't have had deliveries. A charity's brochure, glossy and smiling, with a map on page four that was just slightly, fatally wrong. A flight that landed where no flight should. A boat that moved when boats sleep. A leak in some channel three handshakes deep in the dark — a dump of files somebody pasted to the wrong room and deleted four minutes too late. A name spelled one way here and another way there by a tired clerk who would never know what he'd done.

None of it is hidden. That's the thing nobody understands. The world isn't a locked box. The world is a beach, and everything is just lying there on the sand in plain sight, in front of everybody, all the time. The men who do terrible things don't hide their crumbs in a vault. They drop them on the beach with everybody else's, and they trust the sheer size of the beach to keep them safe. Who could ever sift a whole beach? Who has the patience? Who has the time? Who could possibly hold ten thousand grains of sand in his head at once and feel the three that are warm?

I knew who. Or rather — I knew what.

I sat there in the ticking dark, somewhere a server humming low and patient under the desk like a second heartbeat, and I gave the thing I'd built the whole beach. All of it. Every crumb the network had ever dropped without meaning to, every careless lunch photo and late delivery and crooked map, every handle they'd worn and shed, all the scattered, broken, half-erased trail of a thing that thought it was invisible because it was hiding in plain sight. I gave it the beach and I asked it, in the only way I know how to ask, which is by asking like my life depends on it because somebody's did:

*Find the warm grains. Bring me the three that are hers.*

And then I did the hardest thing in the world.

I waited.

---

Waiting is the part they never show.

It's the worst part. It's worse than the danger. Danger at least gives a man something to *do* with his hands. Waiting just leaves him alone in a small room, screen-lit and silent, with everything he's ever done wrong, and at three in the morning everything I'd ever done wrong formed an orderly queue and took turns.

I thought about Clara. I let myself, for a minute, which I almost never do, because it's a door I have trouble closing once it's open. I thought about the way she laughed, which was rare and therefore worth a fortune, a thing a man would work for. I thought about her hands. I thought about the last real morning, before any of this, before the word *mercy* turned into a thing with teeth — the two of us and a window and nowhere to be, and her saying, with her chin on my chest, *you know the trouble with you. You love the way a house burns. All at once. Nothing left.* And me not arguing, because she was right, she was always right, and because that morning I'd have happily burned to the ground if it kept her in the light.

That was the flaw. She'd named it before I had. The totality of me. The way I fall all the way down and forget where I am and who I was supposed to be. It had ended every good thing I ever had. I'd let the feeling dissolve the mission a dozen times, in a dozen cities, with a dozen people I'd promised things to. *All at once. Nothing left.*

But there was a thing about a flaw, a thing I was only just learning at thirty-something with my hand burned and my heart in my throat. A flaw is just a strength pointed the wrong way. The same totality that scattered me across the world like ash — point it at one thing, one woman, one impossible task, and it stops being a weakness. It becomes the thing nobody else has. An attention that does not waver. A love that does not get tired and does not get bored and does not, ever, look away.

I was the only man on earth who could not stop looking for Clara Dubois. They had built a whole network on the comfortable certainty that everybody eventually looks away.

They had never met me.

The screen changed.

---

It started, the way it always starts, with the world getting smaller.

The *how* of it I couldn't have said and wouldn't have if I could; the how doesn't matter and never did, and that night I wasn't watching the how anyway. I was watching the *what*, and the what was this: the whole wide world, the entire impossible beach, beginning to narrow. Like a tide going out. Like a fist closing. Like a man squinting at a far-off shape on the horizon until, slowly, it resolves into a thing with a name.

First it was everywhere. Every country, every coast, every dropped crumb glowing faintly on the map of the dark.

Then it wasn't everywhere. It was the south. The warm south, the Mediterranean south, the south where the aid convoys ran and the smiling brochures pointed and the money went in clean and came out filthy. The crumbs were clustering. The warm grains were finding each other, the way iron filings find a magnet, three here and five there and a dozen along a coast road, all of them leaning, all of them pointing, all of them *south.*

And then the south got smaller too.

I leaned in. My breath fogged the glass. I didn't wipe it.

Because by now I'd watched this happen enough times — the narrowing, the fist closing — to feel it in my body before I read it with my eyes, the way a name sits on the tip of the tongue a half-second before it's said. The crumbs weren't just pointing south. They were pointing at a *place.* A specific place. A harbour place, an old place, a place of stone and salt and a thousand years of people arriving and disappearing, a place where a man could vanish a hundred children in the noise of a hundred thousand ordinary lives and trust the size of the crowd to keep his secret.

A city I knew. A city I'd sworn, on a bad night a long time ago, that I'd never set foot in again.

Marseille.

I said it out loud to the empty room, the way a man says a name he's been dreading. *Marseille.* And the radiator ticked, and the rain came down, and the warm grains on the screen all leaned toward the old port like sunflowers leaning at a sun, and I sat very still and felt the floor of my old life open up beneath the new one.

Of course it was Marseille. It was always going to be Marseille. The route in her last paper ran through it. The man I'd buried — the Surgeon, my dark mirror, the brother I'd helped to shape back when we were two handles in a back room of the underground, learning the seams together — he'd cut his teeth there same as me. And I had family there. I had *Basti* there, the smuggler-turned-locksmith with the laugh like a landslide, the man who'd once carried me four blocks with two broken ribs and never once let me apologize for the weight.

I'd have to call Basti.

Which meant I'd have to bring him into the fire.

I sat with that for a while, the cup gone cold in my burned hand. Because here was the price of the family I'd made — the people who live in the seams the same as me, who answer on the first ring at four in the morning and never ask why: every door I knocked on had a person behind it, a person who loved me, a person who would say yes the instant I asked because that's what we are to each other, and every yes I collected was a person I was leading toward a man who set fires near the people a man loves just to watch him break.

*One of you,* I thought, *one of you is going to get hurt because of me. And I don't know which, and I'd give anything not to find out.*

I shoved the thought down the way I shove everything down, badly, with both hands, and I went back to the screen, because the narrowing wasn't done.

---

The city wasn't the end of it. That was the truth of that night, the cruelty and the gift of it both.

A city is still a million doors. A man can stand in the middle of Marseille and shout her name until his voice gives out and the city will swallow it like a stone dropped in the sea. I didn't need a city. I needed a street. I needed a door. I needed the one warm window in a wall of cold ones.

So I let it keep going. I let the fist keep closing.

And it did. God help me, it did. The crumbs that all leaned south, that all leaned at Marseille — they kept leaning, kept clustering, kept finding each other in the dark, and the city on the screen began to do the thing the world had done, the thing the south had done. It got smaller. A district. Then a quarter of a district. Then a handful of streets near the old water, the kind of streets that had been forgotten on purpose, the kind of streets where a building could stand empty for years and nobody would ask, the kind of streets where the men who run the world's quiet horrors like to do their business because the world stopped looking long ago.

I'd said it before, easily — that by morning the thing I'd built had narrowed a whole country to a single street. I'd been bragging, the way a man brags about a thing he loves. But I watched it happen that night and I stopped bragging, because it isn't a thing to brag about. It's a thing to pray over.

By the time the rain thinned and the first grey came up over the Brussels rooftops, and the coffee at my elbow had gone cold and skinned over, the beach was gone. The whole wide world of ten thousand meaningless crumbs was gone. There was just the warm south, and the old port, and a forgotten knot of streets near the water, and at the centre of all of it, glowing faint and stubborn and *warm*, the place where every crumb leaned, the place all her bread led to, the place I would be standing in within the day if I had to walk there on my hands.

One block. One building, maybe. The end of the trail.

I should have felt triumph. I felt something colder and better than triumph. I felt *close.* I felt her, four hundred miles south, in a room I could almost picture now, behind a wall I could almost touch, and I put my burned hand flat against the cold glass the way I had once before, like an animal, like a fool, like a man who has not held the person he loves in far too long and has just been told which window the light is behind.

*I'm coming,* I told the warm place in the south. *I found your bread. Every crumb. Hold on. I'm coming.*

---

And then, because the thing I'd built does not get tired and does not get hopeful and does not, ever, fall in love, it did the one more thing I hadn't asked for.

It gave me a face.

Among all those scattered crumbs — the lunch photos, the crooked maps, the leaked dumps and dead handles, the careless dropped moments of a network that thought the size of the beach would keep it safe — there had been pictures. There always are. The world is drowning in pictures, everybody photographing everything, every street and supper uploaded to somewhere and forgotten, and somewhere in that flood, on some street in some south, a stranger with a phone had taken a photograph of nothing in particular and posted it for nobody at all, and never once known what they'd caught at the edge of the frame.

The thing I'd built had caught it for them. Had felt the warm grain in the cold sand. Had pulled one photograph out of the ten million and set it, gently, in the centre of my screen.

It was three days old. The little stamp on it said so, plain and cold. Three days.

And in it — at the edge, half-turned, caught by accident in a stranger's careless shot of a street near the old water — was a woman.

My breath stopped. The radiator ticked. The grey came up over the rooftops and I did not see it.

It was her.

Clara. Three days old. *Alive.* Not a memory I'd been nursing in the dark, not a heartbeat I'd invented to stay upright — Clara, in the world, in the light, in a street in the south, her chin up, her eyes doing the thing her eyes do, alive three days ago and so, by every prayer I had left, alive now.

I would have wept. I think I started to.

And then I saw the rest of the photograph. The part behind her. The reason the thing I'd built had pulled this one frame out of ten million and laid it before me like an offering and like a warning.

Behind her, close, half a step back, watching her the way a keeper watches — a face.

A face I knew.
