# Chapter 27: The Trust Machine

There's a kind of lie that doesn't sound like a lie.

It sounds like your mother's voice. It sounds like the friend you'd loan your last hundred euros. It sounds like the bank you've used for twenty years, the doctor you've trusted with your children, the man on the news with the kind eyes telling you which way to vote. It doesn't break in through the window. You open the door for it. You make it tea. You introduce it to the people you love.

That's the one that gets you. Not the stranger in the alley — the familiar voice that isn't quite who it says it is.

I knew this because I'd built a machine, years before I'd ever build the one I'd built for Clara, whose entire job was to make those voices. And I knew, that night in Marseille — three screens lit against the dark, the harbor wind rattling the shutters, a mug of coffee gone cold an hour ago and forgotten, Basti asleep on the couch with the untroubled face of a man who has never once doubted he's on the side of the angels — that the thing eating PHANTOM MERCY from the inside was the same machine, grown up. The same trick. The same beautiful, patient rot.

I knew it because I'd helped invent it.

The worst thing I ever did was the thing I kept circling back to that night, and the circling was all fear — not fear for me. Fear for them. For the four people I was about to lead into a building, who still thought their phones were on their side.

---

I was twenty-six and brilliant and rotten the way fruit is rotten when it's still beautiful on the outside.

Back then I lived where people like me live — in the seams of the world, in the screen-lit dark, on the forums and the channels you can't find unless someone who trusts you hands you the door. I had a handle they whispered with a little awe and a little fear, and no face anyone could swear to, and that was exactly how I wanted it. The scene thought I was a kind of legend. The scene was wrong about almost everything, including that.

There was a woman. There's always a woman, in the worst stories I tell about myself; the pattern was old even then, and it was a true one. This one was sharp and political and she ran with people who moved money for governments that didn't want their names in it. She thought I was a genius. I needed her to keep thinking that. So when the people around her asked me, almost casually, over a long dinner with very good wine, whether I could *make a town believe something that wasn't true* — I said yes before I'd even worked out how. Because she was watching. Because the alternative was watching her stop watching.

That was always my flaw. It had always been going to be. The mission dissolved in the feeling, every time. Some men drink. I fell in love, and let it spend me.

So I built it.

Not a bomb. Not a gun. Something quieter and far worse. I built a way to take the trust people already had — in their neighbor, their newspaper, their own eyes — and bend it, gently, the way you bend a green branch so slowly it never snaps and never springs back. You don't tell people a lie. You let them tell it to each other in your voice. You don't break the trust. You *borrow* it, and you spend it, and you hand it back so worn out they never notice it's not the same coin.

There was a vote, in a place I won't name, in a year I'd give a great deal to have back. And for a few months, in a few hundred thousand heads, the truth was whatever I'd decided it would be that morning. People marched for it. People shouted it at their televisions. Friends stopped speaking to friends over it. A man I never met lost an election he should have won, and another man took his chair, and the second man was the man my dinner companions wanted in that chair, and the money moved the way money moves when the right hand is on the right lever.

And I had done it without breaking a single law that anyone could prove. Without raising my voice. Without touching a single person. I had simply taught a whole town to mistrust the truth and trust the lie, by wearing the truth's clothes.

When it was over she kissed me and said I was a god.

I went home and was sick. Properly sick, the kind that has nothing to do with the wine. I sat on a cold bathroom floor in a city that wasn't mine, the laptop still humming in the next room with the dump of everything I'd done, and I thought about the man who lost the election he should have won, and I thought about his children, and I thought: *I will never know their names, and I changed their whole lives, and they will never know mine.* That's the obscenity of the thing. You reach into a thousand lives at once and not one of them ever sees your hand. You're a weather system. You're a thing that happens to people. And the woman who'd called me a god was already asleep in the next room, and I understood, sitting on that floor at four in the morning, that I had finally found the one thing I could do that even she couldn't make beautiful.

I left her a week later. Not because I'd grown a conscience overnight — I hadn't, it took years and Clara and a great deal of being sick on a great many floors. I left because every time she looked at me proud, I saw the children whose names I'd never know. That's the closest to a soul I had, back then: I couldn't stand to be admired for it.

---

I never built it again. I want that on the record, somewhere inside me, in the part that keeps the ledger, because there isn't much on that side of the page and this is barely one. I never built it again. But you don't un-invent a thing. You can wipe the drive, burn the handle, go dark for a year — it doesn't matter. You let it out of your hands and it grows up without you, the way a child you abandoned grows up wearing your face and your worst habits and none of your second thoughts.

It grew up. It got patient. It learned to be small.

It got loose the way everything gets loose in our world eventually — passed quietly hand to hand in the dark places, traded on the markets you don't admit you visit, dumped on a forum one night by someone who wanted to watch it burn, until it belonged to no one and everyone. And somewhere in the years I was busy losing myself in one woman after another, the thing I'd made was adopted by people who had no second thoughts at all — and one of them, I was increasingly certain, was the cold surgeon's hand inside PHANTOM MERCY. My dark mirror. The man who'd once stood beside me in the screen-glow and watched me bend a town, and who, unlike me, had never gone home and been sick.

He'd kept the recipe. He'd made it his religion.

---

I had to put words to what the trick actually *does*, and only the words I'm allowed — plain human ones, the way I'd say it to someone I loved who'd never touched a computer in their life. Because Clara made me promise, long ago, in a hotel bed in another country, that I'd never again be a man who hid behind the words only other men like me understand. *If you can't say it to your mother,* she said, *you're hiding something from yourself.*

So. Here is the whole thing, no hiding.

Imagine you get a message from your sister. It's her name. It's her little jokes, the ones only she makes. It knows the thing that happened at the wedding. It knows you're worried about the kids. And it asks you, sweetly, to do one small thing — click here, send this, trust this man, go to that address — and you do it, of course you do it, because it's your *sister.*

Except it isn't.

It's a stranger who's been listening to your family for a long, long time, who's learned your sister well enough to *be* her for ninety seconds. Long enough to spend the trust you spent a whole life building with her. Long enough to walk you, smiling, off a cliff.

That's the machine. It doesn't break locks. It convinces you to hand it the key, because it's wearing the face of the person you'd give your key to without thinking. It turns the best thing about you — that you trust the people you love — into the blade it puts in your back.

I built that. God help me, I built the first one. And I spent that whole long Marseille night with the proof of it glowing on the desk in front of me, the screens throwing their cold light up onto a face I didn't much want to look at, watching the machine I'd fathered do its patient, beautiful work all over PHANTOM MERCY's enemies.

Because that's what I'd found, when I'd gone digging into how the network had stayed invisible for three years. Down through the leaks and the dumps, the dead channels, the forums where the network's own people bragged in code to each other — three years of crumbs. And at the bottom of it, no better locks. No bigger guns. *This.* Every investigator who'd gotten close had, at some point, received a message from someone they trusted — a colleague, a superior, a wife — telling them, in that trusted voice, to back off, to look the wrong way, to mistrust the one honest man in the room. Three years of doors opened from the inside by people who thought they were talking to their own.

It was elegant. It was monstrous. It was *mine,* grown monstrous, and I sat there in the dark with the server fans whispering under the desk and hated myself with a thoroughness I'd had a lot of practice at.

And then Basti rolled over on the couch and said, into the dark, "You're doing the thing where you breathe like a man being strangled. Go to sleep."

I love him. He has no idea how much. I made a sound that was almost a laugh.

"I built the thing that's killing the people we're trying to help," I said.

"Then you know how it works," he said, not even opening his eyes, "so unbuild it. In the morning. Now sleep, you're keeping the wall awake."

That's Basti. A man who'd spent half his life opening locks that weren't his, who'd traded it all in to be the one I called when a door stood between me and someone I loved, and who could still reduce the worst confession of my life to a maintenance problem. *Then you know how it works, so unbuild it.* As if guilt were a jammed mechanism. As if you could pick the lock on your own past with the right tension on the right pin.

The infuriating thing about Basti is that he's usually right.

I thought about Yuri, up in Kyiv, who'd have said the opposite — who'd have said *trust nothing, everyone's compromised, your phone is a snitch and so is your refrigerator, assume the line is rooted and the friend on it is wearing a stranger's smile* — and who was, in his paranoid way, about to be proven more right tonight than he'd ever been in his life. I thought about Ifeoma in Lagos, who kept my whole invisible world running from a back room that hummed like a beehive, who'd have prayed for me and then quietly done three hours of work to fix what I'd broken, the way she always did, the moral center of a thing that had no business having one. I thought about Noa, in Tel Aviv, who'd have answered on the first ring at this hour because she always does, and who'd have heard the strangling-breath in my voice across two thousand miles and said *you're spiraling, come back,* the way she used to when I was hers and the spiraling was the only thing I was reliably good at.

Five people. My family. The one I made, because the one I was born to never quite stuck. Five people scattered across five cities, people who live in the seams the way I do, held together by nothing but a few wires in the dark and a love none of us has ever once said out loud.

And every single thread between us — every call, every message, every handle blinking awake in the night — ran through exactly the kind of friendly voice my machine was built to wear.

---

I didn't sleep.

I sat with the old case — the town, the vote, the worn-out coin of trust passed hand to hand — and I laid it down beside the thing PHANTOM MERCY was doing, the way a doctor lays two scans side by side, the old tumor and the new one, looking for the family resemblance. And the resemblance wasn't a resemblance. It was the same disease. The same fingerprints. The same little signature flourish at the end, the kind of tell every one of us leaves without meaning to, like a man who can't help signing his own forgeries.

The Surgeon hadn't just learned the trick. He'd learned it *from the source.* From standing next to me in the small hours, screens between us and the world, while I did the worst thing I ever did.

And here is the part that made my blood go to water. Here is the whole reason the confession kept running in me that night, the whole ugly thing, instead of letting me skip ahead to the rescue.

Because I understood the machine, I understood exactly one thing nobody else hunting Clara understood: *the trick only works if you don't know it's running.* It's a magician's force. The whole power of it is that you never suspect the friendly voice. Show a man the wires and the mirror, and the illusion dies on the spot. The lie has no power over someone who's been told, in advance, *the next voice you trust might not be who it says it is.*

So all I had to do was warn them. My family. The four people I was about to lead into the worst night of all our lives. I just had to tell them: trust nothing that comes wearing a familiar face tonight. Not a text from me. Not a call from Marchetti. Not a voice that sounds like your own mother. *Verify everything. Trust the room you can touch and nothing through a wire.* The thing Yuri had been screaming into the dark for a decade, finally, this once, gospel.

That was the lesson the old case had taught me, paid for in a man's stolen election and my own ruined sleep. That was what Act after act of my crooked life had been quietly *preparing* me to know, here, now, when it finally mattered: how the lie wears a loved one's face, and how the only cure is to see it coming.

I reached for my phone to send the warning. To Ifeoma. To Yuri. To Basti, snoring three feet away. To Noa, who still picks up. To Marchetti, who I needed and didn't trust and couldn't do this without.

*Trust nothing that sounds like someone you love tonight,* I'd tell them. *I'll explain when it's done. Just — trust the lie that I'm warning you about, and nothing else.*

I had the message half-written. My thumb was over the last word.

And the tool I'd built to find her — the thing I'd stopped explaining and started simply believing, the sixth sense I'd grown in seven sleepless days, the gift, the weapon, the thing whispered about now in rooms I'd never enter as if it were something between a rumor and a god — went very quiet, the way it goes when it's stopped looking outward and turned to look at *us.* The way it goes right before it tells you the thing you cannot survive hearing.

It had been listening, all night, while I turned the old case over and over in the dark. Listening to the silence around my own little band — the traffic between our handles, the late pings city to city — the way it had once listened to silence and found Clara's heartbeat in it.

And in that silence it found a heartbeat that wasn't ours.

Something was already in the messages flying between my people. Something had been there for hours. The friendly voices going back and forth between Lagos and Kyiv and this very room — *some of them weren't us.* Some of them had been quietly replaced, mid-conversation, by a patient stranger wearing our faces, our jokes, the thing that happened at the wedding, spending the trust we'd spent a lifetime building, walking us — smiling, smiling — toward whatever waited at dawn.

I'd spent the whole night marveling at how the Surgeon had used my machine to murder the truth in a town three years ago.

The very same trick was running right now — against my own rescue team's phones.
