# Chapter 27: The Trust Machine

There's a lie that doesn't sound like one.

It sounds like your mother. Like the friend you'd loan your last hundred euros without thinking. Like the bank, the doctor, the man on television with the gentle eyes telling you who to vote for. It doesn't break in. You open the door. You make it coffee. You introduce it to the people you love.

That's the one that owns you. Not the stranger in the alley. The familiar voice that isn't quite who it claims to be.

I knew this because I'd built a machine, years before I built the one for Clara. A machine whose whole job was manufacturing those voices. And that night in Marseille — Basti asleep on the couch, the harbor wind slamming the shutters — I knew something else. The thing devouring PHANTOM MERCY from the inside was my machine. Grown up. The same trick. The same beautiful, patient rot.

I knew it because I helped invent it.

I need to say the worst thing I've ever done. So you understand my fear that night. Not fear for me. Fear for them. For the four people I was about to walk into a building, who still believed their phones were on their side.

---

I was twenty-six. Good with my hands. Rotten the way fruit is rotten when it's still beautiful on the outside.

I lived at night, in the blue glow of screens that bleach all the color out of your face. Among people who only existed as handles and silence. A world where you know someone for ten years without ever seeing their face. Where the name you choose for yourself is worth more than the one you were given at birth. Where trust is worth more than money, because you can't steal it or copy it or sell it.

We lived at the margins. The people who inhabit those margins talk about it like a point of pride. They're wrong. They're right.

There was a woman. There's always a woman in the worst stories I tell about myself. I know that now. This one was sharp. Political. She ran with people who moved money for governments that didn't want their names attached to it. She thought I was a genius. I knew I was just a stubborn tinkerer who hadn't known when to stop. I needed her to keep believing the other thing.

So one night, at a long dinner washed down with very good wine, the people around her asked me. Almost casually. Could I *make a city believe something that wasn't true.*

I said yes before I'd even worked out how. Because she was watching. Because the alternative was watching her stop.

That's my flaw. I've always carried it whole. The mission dissolved in the feeling, every time. Some men drink. I fell in love. And I let it spend me.

So I built it.

Not a bomb. Not a weapon. Something quieter. And far worse.

I built it at night, week after week, in a studio that smelled of cold coffee and the warm plastic of machines stacked under the desk. That hum of cooling fans you eventually stop distinguishing from your own breath.

I found a way to take the trust people already had. In their neighbor. Their newspaper. Their own eyes. And bend it. Gently. The way you bend a green branch so slowly it never snaps and never straightens back.

You don't tell people a lie. You let them whisper it to each other in your voice. You don't break the trust. You *borrow* it. You spend it. And you hand it back so worn down that nobody notices it's not the same coin anymore.

There was a vote. In a place I won't name. A year I'd give a great deal to have back.

And for a few months, in a few hundred thousand heads, the truth was whatever I'd decided it would be that morning. People marched for it. People screamed it at their televisions. Friends stopped speaking over it.

A man I never met lost an election he should have won. Another man took his seat. That second man was the one my dinner companions wanted there. And the money flowed. The way money flows when the right hand is on the right lever.

I did all of it without breaking a single law anyone could prove. Without leaving a trace anyone could walk back to me. Without raising my voice. Without touching anyone. I had simply taught an entire city to mistrust the truth. And to believe the lie, because it wore the truth's clothes.

When it was over she kissed me and said I was a god.

I went home and was sick. Really sick. The kind that has nothing to do with wine.

I sat on the cold tile of a bathroom floor in a city that wasn't mine. I thought about the man who lost the election. I thought about his children. And I told myself: *I will never know their names, and I changed their whole lives, and they will never know mine.*

That's the obscenity of it. You step into a thousand lives at once and not one of them ever sees the hand. You become a storm. A thing that falls on people.

And the woman who called me a god was already asleep in the next room. I understood, on that tile at four in the morning, that I had finally found the one thing I was capable of that even she couldn't make beautiful.

I left her a week later. Not because a conscience woke me in the night. No. That took years. And Clara. And many other bathroom floors.

I left because every time she looked at me with pride, I saw the children whose names I would never know. That was the closest thing to a soul I had back then. I couldn't stand to be admired for it.

---

I never built it again.

I need that written here, in the confession. Because there isn't much on that side of the ledger, and this is barely one entry.

I never built it again. But you can't uninvent a thing. You let it loose and it grows without you. The way an abandoned child grows up wearing your face and your worst habits and none of your regrets.

In our world we call it a leak. A thing escapes. Changes hands. Mutates. And one day you run into it somewhere you should never have been. Unrecognizable. But somewhere it still carries the mark of your hand.

It grew. It became patient. It learned to make itself small.

And somewhere, while I was busy losing myself from one woman to the next, the thing I'd built escaped. It was adopted by people with no regrets at all. One of them, I was increasingly certain, was the cold hand of the Surgeon inside PHANTOM MERCY.

My dark mirror. A man from the same world as me. Who had once stood beside me in the blue glow. Who had watched me bend a city. And who, unlike me, had never gone home to be sick.

He kept the recipe. He made it his religion.

---

I should say what this trick actually *does*. And I'll say it the only way I'm allowed. With human words. Simple ones. The way I'd explain it to someone I love who has never touched a computer.

Because Clara made me promise, long ago, in a hotel bed in another country. Never to be a man who hides behind words only men like me understand. "If you can't say it to your mother," she told me, "you're hiding something from yourself."

So. Here it is. The whole thing. Nothing hidden.

Someone gets a message from their sister. It has her name. Her little jokes, the ones only she makes. It knows what happened at the wedding. It knows about the worry over the kids. And it asks, sweetly, for one small thing. Click here. Send this. Trust this person. Go to this address.

And they do it. Of course they do. Because it's their *sister.*

Except it isn't.

It's a stranger who has been listening to that family for a very, very long time. Who has learned the sister well enough to *be* her for ninety seconds. Long enough to spend the trust built over an entire lifetime. Long enough to walk someone — smiling — right to the edge of a cliff.

That's the machine. It doesn't break locks. It convinces you to hand it the key. Because it's wearing the face of the person you'd give your key to without thinking.

It takes the best thing about you — the fact that you trust the people you love — and turns it into the blade it puts in your back.

I built that. God forgive me, I built the first one.

And I spent that whole long Marseille night with the proof glowing on the desk. In the light of the screen, the only light in the room. Watching the machine I fathered do its patient, magnificent work on PHANTOM MERCY's enemies.

Because that's what I'd found, digging through old leaks. The forgotten archives someone believed had been destroyed. The forums where the network thought its tracks were gone. Looking for how it had stayed invisible for three years.

No better locks. No bigger weapons. *This.*

Every investigator who had gotten close had, at some point, received a message from someone they trusted. A colleague. A superior. A wife. A trusted voice telling them to drop it. To look the wrong way. To mistrust the one honest person in the room.

Three years of doors opened from the inside. By people who believed they were talking to their own.

It was elegant. It was monstrous. It was *mine*, turned monster. And I sat there in the dark, hating myself with a precision I'd had plenty of practice at.

Then Basti shifted on the couch. He said into the dark: "You're doing the thing where you breathe like a man being strangled. Go to sleep."

I love him. He has no idea how much. I made a sound that was almost a laugh.

"I built the thing that's killing the people we're trying to help," I said.

"Then you know how it works." He didn't even open his eyes. "So unbuild it. Tomorrow morning. Now sleep. You're keeping the wall awake."

That's Basti. A man who spent half his life opening locks that weren't his. The running, the nights picking doors for people whose names he didn't want to know. He'd traded all of it to become the one I called when a door stood between me and someone I loved.

And he could still reduce the worst confession of my life to a maintenance problem. *Then you know how it works, so unbuild it.* As if guilt were a jammed mechanism. As if you could pick the lock on your own past with the right pressure on the right pin.

The infuriating thing about Basti is that he's usually right.

I thought about Yuri, up in Kyiv. He would have said the opposite. *Trust nothing, everyone's been turned, your phone listens to you and so does your refrigerator, cut everything off, unplug it all, we start from zero.* He changed handles the way other people change shirts. Never lit up the same device twice in the same city. Tonight, in his paranoid way, he was going to be more right than he'd ever been in his life.

I thought about Ifeoma in Lagos. She would have prayed for me. Then quietly done three hours of work to fix what I'd broken. Like always. The moral center of something that had no business having one.

I thought about Noa in Tel Aviv. She would have picked up on the first ring, even at this hour, because she always picks up. She would have heard the strangling-breath in my voice from three thousand kilometres away. She would have said *you're spiraling, come back.* The way she used to when I was hers, and spiraling was the one thing I was reliably good at.

Four people. My family. The one I built for myself, because the one I was born into never quite held together. Four people scattered across four cities. Held together by nothing. A few threads in the dark. Channels we moved every month. And a love none of us has ever said out loud.

And every one of those threads between us ran exactly through the kind of friendly voice my machine was built to wear.

---

I didn't sleep.

I laid the old case beside what PHANTOM MERCY was doing. The city, the vote, the worn coin of trust passed hand to hand. Like a doctor laying two scans side by side. The old tumor and the new one. Looking for the family resemblance.

It wasn't a resemblance. It was the same disease. The same fingerprints. The same small signature flourish at the end. That tic you can never bring yourself to erase. Like a man who can't stop signing his own forgeries.

The Surgeon hadn't just learned the trick. He'd learned it *at the source.* By standing next to me, in the blue glow, while I did the worst thing of my life.

And here is the part that turned my blood cold. Here is why I kept running through the whole confession that night, instead of charging straight toward the rescue.

Because I understood the machine, I understood one thing nobody else hunting Clara understood. *The trick only works if you don't know it's running.*

It's a magician's force. All its power depends on never suspecting the friendly voice. Show a man the wires and the mirror and the illusion dies on the spot. The lie has no power over someone warned in advance. *The next voice you trust might not be who it says it is.*

So all I had to do was warn them. My family. The four people I was about to lead into the worst night of our lives.

Just tell them: trust nothing that comes with a familiar face tonight. Not a message from me. Not a call from Marchetti. Not a voice that sounds like your own mother. *Verify everything. Trust the room you can touch. Nothing that comes through a wire.*

That was the lesson the old case had taught me. Paid for by a man's stolen election and my own ruined sleep. What my twisted life had quietly *prepared* me to know. Here. Now. How the lie wears the face of someone you love. And that the only cure is seeing it coming.

I reached for my phone to send the warning. To Ifeoma. To Yuri. To Basti, snoring a metre away. To Noa, who always picks up. To Marchetti, who I needed, didn't trust, and couldn't do this without.

*Don't believe anything tonight that sounds like someone you love,* I was going to write. *I'll explain when it's over. Just — believe the lie I'm warning you about, and nothing else.*

The message was half-written. My thumb hung over the last word.

And the tool I'd built to find her went very quiet. The silence it gets when it stops looking outward and turns to look at *us.* The silence before the news you don't survive hearing.

It had been listening all night while I turned the old case over in the dark. Listening to the silence around our little group. The way it had once listened to silence and found Clara's heartbeat inside it.

And in that silence, it found a heartbeat that wasn't ours.

Something was already inside the messages flying between my people. Something had been there for hours. The friendly voices going back and forth between Lagos and Kyiv and this very room — *some of them weren't us.* Some had been quietly replaced, mid-conversation, by a patient stranger wearing our faces, our jokes, the thing that happened at the wedding. Walking us — smiling — toward whatever waited at dawn.

I had spent the entire night marveling at how the Surgeon had used my machine to murder the truth in a city three years ago.

The exact same trick was running right now. Against my own team's phones.
