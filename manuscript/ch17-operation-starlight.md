# Chapter 17: Operation STARLIGHT

The system had given me a building.

Not a city, not a street — a building, a real one, with a roof and a number and a history, sitting in the north of Marseille like a tooth gone bad in a beautiful mouth. An abandoned hospital. The kind of place a city forgets on purpose. The kind of place where, the night before, the thing I'd built to find her had finally stopped pointing at the dark and pointed at *her* — and where the picture it drew was not a woman running.

It was a woman held.

I had stared at that for a long time. There's a particular silence that comes when the search ends. You spend so many days wanting the answer that you forget the answer is a door you have to walk through. Now I had it. She was in that building. She was alive enough to leave a heartbeat for the machine to find. And every cell in my body wanted to put on my coat and walk out into the Brussels cold and not stop walking until I was standing in front of that bad tooth with a crowbar, screaming her name.

That is exactly how you get someone killed.

So I did the hardest thing I have ever done, harder than war-gaming a monster, harder than burying the man I used to be. I sat down. I made tea I would not drink. And I called the people who love me, to plan the impossible.

---

You cannot rescue someone alone. I know that now. I spent my whole life believing the opposite — that the only person you could ever truly count on was the one in the mirror, and even he would let you down for a pretty face — and it took losing Clara to teach me the lesson she'd been trying to teach me for three years.

*You have a family,* she said once, exasperated, after I'd flown to four countries in a month chasing a job and a feeling and forgotten to sleep. *You collect people like other men collect debts. Lagos. Marseille. Kyiv. That girl in Tel Aviv who still answers when you call at three in the morning. They would walk into fire for you. And you treat them like a phone you only pick up when it rings.* She'd looked at me with that exacting tenderness that took the skin off. *One day you're going to need them all at once. I hope you've earned it.*

I hadn't. But I called anyway.

I lit them up one by one, the whole scattered map of my life, and I told them the truth, which is something I almost never do: that I'd found her, that she was being held in a dead building in a dangerous city, and that I had no idea how to get her out without getting everyone I loved put in the ground.

There was a silence on the line. The good kind. The kind that means people are already moving.

"About time," Ifeoma said from Lagos. "I've had the infrastructure warm for a week. I just didn't want to say it, in case you weren't ready to hear it could be done." A pause. "Is she alive?"

"She's alive."

"Then we get her," Ifeoma said, simple as that, and I had to put my hand over my mouth so she wouldn't hear what it did to me.

---

Here is what I will tell you about the next thirty hours, and here is what I won't.

I won't tell you how the tool helped. I made a promise to myself, somewhere around chapter one of this confession, that I would never sit you down and explain the machinery, partly because it would bore the love out of you and partly because the machinery isn't the miracle. The miracle is what it gave.

So let me only tell you what it gave.

It let me see the building before I ever stood in it.

That's the closest I can put it. I had a dead hospital in a foreign city, a place I'd never set foot in, and somehow, by the slow grey hours of that night, I came to know it the way you know a house you grew up in. Where the doors were. Where they used to be before someone bricked them up. Where the power still crept in through one cable nobody had bothered to cut. Where a person could come in unseen, and — this was the thing that kept me breathing — where a person could come *out*. I fed it everything, every scrap and ghost of that building's life, and it gave me back a map of a place I had never been, so true I could walk it with my eyes closed.

And then it did the harder thing. It let me plan the impossible.

You don't plan a rescue the way they show you. You don't draw a clever line and call it a plan. A real plan is a hundred small disasters anticipated in advance — what if the door's locked, what if the door's not there, what if she can't walk, what if she won't come, what if it's not her, what if it's a hundred of *them* — and you sit there, sick, and you answer every single one, because the questions you don't answer at the desk are the ones that answer themselves with a body. The tool didn't make the plan. I want to be clear about that, because it matters to my soul. *I* made the plan. But it held a thousand of those disasters up in front of me, one after another, faster than my fear could, so that by morning I had looked the impossible in the face so many times it had stopped being impossible and started being a list.

We gave the list a name, because you have to. You have to make the terror into a thing with a name or you can't pick it up and carry it.

We called it Operation STARLIGHT.

Basti named it. Of course he did. "Because you find a way out of the dark by following the only light that's left," he said, and then, because he can't bear a tender moment, "also it sounds expensive, and someone is paying for my flight."

---

They came to me, in the end. Not in the body — there was no time, and bodies are how you get caught — but onto the wire, into the small bright room I'd built to find her, until it wasn't a search anymore, it was a war council, and every chair had someone in it I would die for.

Ifeoma ran the spine of it from Lagos — steady, sleepless, the moral compass that kept asking the question none of us wanted: *and if it goes wrong, how do the rest of us get home.* She is the only person I know who plans the failure as carefully as the success, and I have learned, too late and at great cost, that this is what love actually looks like. Not the grand gesture. The quiet planning of your survival when you're too far gone to plan it yourself.

Yuri came in from Kyiv, paranoid as scripture, and Yuri being paranoid is Yuri being right, so I'd learned to treat his fear as a weather report. He listened to the silence around that building for a day and a night — I won't tell you how, I've told you I won't — and what he heard made the hair stand up on him.

"It's too quiet," he said. "A place holding something that valuable should hum. Guards get bored. Bored men make noise. This place makes no noise at all." He said it the way other men say *I think there's someone in the house.* "Either there's nobody there, which means we're wrong and she's already gone — or there's somebody there who knows exactly how to be quiet for a man who's listening. And I only know one man like that."

The Surgeon.

Nobody said it. Nobody had to. He'd been the third person at every table for two weeks now, the empty chair, the cold draft, the reason I'd dug up the man I used to be. My dark mirror. The one who, the rehearsal had told me with sickening certainty, had stopped hunting the children and started hunting me.

"He's expecting you," Yuri said. "I'd bet my life. Which, knowing you, I am."

"Then we don't give him me," Ifeoma said. "We give him the thing he doesn't expect. We give him *all* of us."

---

And then Marchetti came on.

That was the strange one. That was the chair I never thought I'd see filled.

Commandant Thierry Marchetti was Clara's old handler from the days when she carried a flag instead of a conscience — a hard, weary man, French intelligence to the marrow, the kind of operator who's buried so many people he's stopped flinching at the funerals. We'd circled each other for two acts now, him and me, two animals who each had something the other needed and neither could afford to trust. He had authority. The lawful kind, the kind with handcuffs and judges and a country behind it. I had reach — the family, the system, the long invisible arm that goes where flags can't. He needed my reach and my deniability. I needed his authority, because here was the thing I'd promised myself on a desert night three years before and renewed every cold morning since:

I was going to do this *right.*

That's the part you need to understand about me, if you understand nothing else. There was an easier way. There is always an easier way, and I know all of them, I learned them in the life I'm trying to leave, and on the worst nights they sing to me. I could have gone in dirty. I could have burned that building down around the men holding her and called it justice and slept fine. I wanted to. God forgive me, there were hours that night when wanting to do it the dirty way was the only thing keeping me upright, the way hate can be a kind of fuel when love has run dry.

But Clara had spent her whole brilliant life on a single, unfashionable idea: that the truth only counts if you can *prove* it. That a rescue without evidence is just another disappearance going the other way. That you don't beat people who hide behind a flag by becoming a man with no flag at all — you beat them by being the one who can stand in the light and *prove* what they did, so the children go home and stay home and the men who took them go somewhere they can't reach them again.

So I needed Marchetti. I needed a real raid, with real warrants, with a real prosecutor's case stacking up behind it like a wall. I needed the kind of takedown that holds in a courtroom a year later, when the cameras are gone and the lawyers are bored and the only thing left is whether you did it clean.

"You understand what you're asking me," Marchetti said. His voice was gravel and old smoke. "You're asking me to put a French team into a French building on the word of a stateless ghost with a warrant out in three jurisdictions and a network of criminals for friends."

"I'm asking you to bring her home," I said. "And I'm handing you the case to do it clean."

A long silence. I could hear him thinking. I could hear, underneath it, something I hadn't expected — that he wanted her back too. Not the way I did. But she'd been his once, his agent, his responsibility, and a man like Marchetti carries his failures like stones, and Clara going dark on his watch was a stone he'd been carrying a long time.

"STARLIGHT," he said finally, tasting the name, hating it for being romantic. "You children and your operation names." A breath. "All right. We do it your way. We do it clean. God help us both."

And that was the moment it stopped being grief and started being a plan. That was the moment the impossible became a list with a date on it. I had my family on the wire and a flag on my side and a map of a dead building drawn so true I could walk it blind. We had her marker — the bright certain point the system had laid down on that bad tooth in the north of the city — and around it we built everything: the way in, the way out, the lawful hammer falling at the lawful hour.

We worked until the windows went grey. We answered the hundred small disasters one by one. We made it real.

We were going to get her.

---

I should have slept then. I didn't. I never do, not when she's that close.

So I was awake, in the dead hour before dawn, when I did the thing I always do before I let myself believe anything — I went back and looked at her marker one more time. I don't know why. To touch it, I suppose. To prove to myself the heartbeat was still there, that the building still held her, that the whole impossible scaffolding we'd built all night had something true to stand on.

I looked at the marker.

And the marker was in the wrong place.

I want to be careful here, because this is the moment the floor went, and I owe it to you to make you feel it go. The system doesn't move things. The system finds the truth and holds it still and lets *me* be the one who wavers. The marker we'd built the whole operation around — the bright certain point in the north — had sat in that one spot since the night before, since the moment it placed her in that dead hospital and broke my heart and gave it back to me.

Now it sat somewhere else.

Somewhere cleaner. Somewhere easier. Somewhere with one road in and no road out — a place you'd never put a hostage and would absolutely put a trap.

Someone had reached into the one thing in the world I trusted, the thing I'd built with my own sleepless hands to find the woman I loved, and they had moved her, gently, in the dark, the way you'd move a lamp to throw a shadow exactly where you wanted a man to walk.

She wasn't there. She had never been moved. Only the marker had — touched in the night by a hand that knew how I think, a hand that had watched me build my whole rescue around a single bright point and then, patient, bloodless, proud of itself, picked that point up and set it down somewhere I would come running.

Someone had moved her marker overnight.

The location was bait.

And it was set for me.
