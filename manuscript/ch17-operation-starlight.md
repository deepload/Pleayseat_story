# Chapter 17: Operation STARLIGHT

The system had given me a building.

Not a city. Not a street. A building. Real, with a roof and a number and a history. It sat near the Old Port of Marseille like a bad tooth in a beautiful mouth. An abandoned hospital. The kind of place a city forgets on purpose.

The night before, the thing I'd built had finally stopped pointing at the dark. It had pointed at *her*. And the picture it drew was not a woman running.

It was a woman held.

I stared at that for a long time. Face washed blue by the screen. The only light in the apartment. Three in the morning, the way it always is when something real happens.

There's a particular silence when a search ends. You spend so many nights wanting the answer that you forget one thing. The answer is a door. You still have to walk through it.

And I had it. She was in that building. Alive enough to leave a heartbeat for the machine to find. Alive enough that the machine found her.

Every cell in my body wanted to put on my coat. Walk out into the Brussels cold. Walk without stopping until I was standing in front of that bad tooth with a crowbar in my hand, screaming her name.

That is exactly how a man gets someone killed.

So I did the hardest thing I have ever done. Harder than playing a monster. Harder than burying the man I used to be.

I sat down.

I made tea I would never drink. It joined two cold coffees and a cigarette's worth of ash. The small graveyard of sleepless nights.

And I called the people who love me. To plan the impossible.

---

No one saves someone alone. I know that now.

I believed the opposite my whole life. The only person worth counting on was the one in the mirror. And even he would let you down for a pretty face. It took losing Clara to teach me. The lesson she'd been trying to teach me for two and a half years.

"You have a family," she'd said one day, exasperated. That was after I'd crossed four countries in a month. For a job. For a feeling. Forgetting to sleep.

"You collect people like other men collect debts. Lagos. Marseille. Kyiv. That woman in Tel Aviv who still picks up when you call at three in the morning. They'd walk through fire for you. And you treat them like a phone you only touch when it rings." She'd looked at me with that exacting tenderness that took the skin clean off. "One day you'll need them all at once. I hope you've earned it."

I hadn't. I called anyway.

I woke them one by one. The whole scattered map of my life. Friends I'd never seen in the flesh. Known only by handles, on channels nobody else knows how to read. Met at those massive gatherings where thousands of people like us come together once a year. Under false names. To show each other the locks of the world and laugh about breaking them.

And I told them the truth. Which I almost never do. That I'd found her. That she was being held in a dead building in a dangerous city. That I had no idea how to get her out without putting everyone I loved in the ground.

There was a silence on the line. The right kind. The kind that means people are already moving.

"About time," Ifeoma said from Lagos. "I've had everything ready for a week. Machines on, lines warm, nobody listening. I just didn't want to say it. In case you weren't ready to hear it was possible." A pause. "Is she alive?"

"She's alive."

"Then we go get her."

Just like that. I had to put my hand over my mouth so she wouldn't hear what it did to me.

Behind her voice, the deep hum of her machines. The sound of cabinet fans and processors breathing. In our world, there is no more reassuring sound.

---

Here is what I'll keep from the thirty hours that followed. And here is what I leave in the dark.

The how doesn't matter. I made myself a promise at the start of this confession. Never lay out the machinery. Partly because it would bore the love right out of the thing. And partly because the machinery was never the miracle.

The miracle was what it gave.

So I keep only that.

It let me see the building before I ever set foot in it.

That's the closest I can put it. I had a dead hospital in a foreign city. A place I'd never been. And yet, over that slow grey night, I came to know it the way a man knows the house he grew up in. Where the doors were. Where they had been before someone bricked them up. Where the power still crept in through one cable nobody had bothered to cut. Where a man could enter unseen. And — this was the thing keeping me breathing — where a man could *come back out*.

I fed it everything. Every scrap, every ghost, every forgotten record of that building's life. And it gave me back a map of a place I had never been. So true I could have walked it with my eyes shut.

Then it did the harder thing. It let me prepare the impossible.

You don't prepare a rescue the way films show it. You don't draw a clean line and call it a plan. A real plan is a hundred small disasters anticipated in advance.

What if the door is locked. What if the door no longer exists. What if she can't walk. What if she won't come. What if it isn't her. What if there are a hundred of *them*.

You sit there with your heart in your throat. You answer every one. Because the questions you don't answer at the desk, a body answers for you later.

The tool did not make the plan. That point matters to my soul. *I* made the plan. But it held a thousand disasters in front of me, one by one. Faster than my fear could. So that by morning I had looked the impossible in the face so many times it had stopped being impossible. It had become a list.

We gave the list a name. It needs one. In our world everything gets a code name. An old hand's superstition. A way of taking terror and making it something you can hold. Otherwise you can't.

We called it Operation STARLIGHT.

Basti named it. Of course he did. "Because you find the way out of the dark by following the only light that's left," he said. Then, because he cannot stand one second of tenderness: "And it sounds expensive. And someone else is buying my plane ticket."

---

They came to me, in the end. Not in the body. There was no time, and bodies are how you get caught. On the wire. Through those locked conversations where my family lives. Threads no one can follow, moved to a new address every month out of pure paranoia. The way you move apartments so no one finds you.

Into the small bright room I'd built to find her. Until it wasn't a search anymore. It was a war council. Every chair filled by someone I would die for.

Ifeoma held the spine of it from Lagos. Steady. Sleepless. The moral conscience who kept asking the question none of us wanted to hear. *And if this goes wrong, how do the rest of us get home?*

She is the only person I know who prepares for failure with as much care as success. Before we moved a single piece, she had already built the last-resort switch. One gesture, and every trace of us vanished from the world. If the night turned to nightmare.

I learned this too late, and at a terrible price. That is what love actually looks like. Not the grand gesture. The quiet planning of your survival, when you're too far gone to do it yourself.

Yuri came in from Kyiv. Suspicious as a prophet who only predicts disaster. And Yuri suspicious is Yuri right. I'd learned to treat his fear like a weather report.

He listened to the silence around that building. A day and a night. The how of it is his alone, and I won't dig it up here. What he heard made the hair stand up on him.

"It's too quiet," he said. "A place holding something that valuable should hum. Guards get bored. Bored men make noise. This place makes no noise at all."

He said it the way you'd say *I think there's someone in the house.*

"Either nobody's there — which means we're wrong, she's already gone. Or there's someone who knows exactly how to stay quiet for a man who's listening. A professional. Someone from our world. And I only know one man like that."

The Surgeon.

No one said it. No one had to.

From the first day, he'd been the third at every table. The empty chair. The cold draft. The reason I'd dug up the man I used to be. My dark mirror. A ghost from my old life — the days I cracked the world's safes for money and for the rush. He had recognized my way of working the way you recognize old handwriting. A handle I'd left lying on a dead forum years ago. The rehearsal had told me with sickening certainty: he had stopped hunting children. He was hunting me.

"He's waiting for you," Yuri said. "I'd bet my life on it. Which, knowing you, I am."

"Then we don't give him me," Ifeoma said. "We give him what he doesn't expect. We give him *all of us*."

---

And then Marchetti came on the line.

That was the strange one. The chair I never expected to see filled. A man with a badge, stepping into a room full of people who had spent their lives staying off every list a badge can pull.

Commandant Thierry Marchetti was Clara's old handler. From the days when she carried a flag instead of a conscience. A hard, worn man. DGSE through and through. The kind of operator who has buried so many people he stopped flinching at funerals.

We had circled each other for two acts now. Two animals, each holding what the other needed, neither able to trust. He had authority. The lawful kind. With handcuffs and judges and a country behind it. I had reach — the family, the system, the long invisible arm that goes where flags can't. Into the seams of the world, where warrants mean nothing.

He needed my reach and my deniability. I needed his authority. Because here was what I had sworn to myself on a desert night two and a half years ago. Renewed every cold morning since.

I was going to do this *right*.

That is the part of me that contains everything else. Without it, none of this story means anything.

There was an easier way. There always is. I could have gone dirty.

Clara had spent her whole life teaching me the opposite. We prove them. We don't take them down in the dark. A rescue without proof is just another disappearance going the other direction. A leak into the dark that no one can ever trace back.

So I needed Marchetti. A real raid. Real warrants. A real prosecutor's case stacking up behind it like a wall. The kind of takedown that holds in a courtroom a year later. When the cameras are gone and the lawyers are bored and the only question left is whether you did it clean.

"You understand what you're asking," Marchetti said.

His voice was gravel and old smoke.

"You're asking me to send a French team into a French building. On the word of a stateless ghost with warrants out in three jurisdictions and a network of criminals for friends."

"I'm asking you to bring her home," I said. "And I'm handing you the case to do it clean."

A long silence. I could hear him thinking. And underneath that silence, something I hadn't counted on. He wanted her back too. Not the way I did. But she had been his once. His agent. His responsibility. A man like Marchetti carries his failures like stones. Clara going dark on his watch was a stone he'd been carrying a long time.

"STARLIGHT," he said finally. Tasting the name. Hating it for being romantic. "You and your operation names. Children." A breath. "Fine. We do it your way. We do it clean. God help us both."

And in that moment it stopped being grief and became a plan. The impossible became a list with a date on it.

I had my family on the wire. A flag on my side. A map of a dead building drawn so true I could walk it blind. We had her marker — the bright certain point the system had laid down on that bad tooth near the Old Port, the one true thing in a world that lies for a living. Around it we built everything. The way in. The way out. The lawful hammer falling at the lawful hour.

We worked until the windows turned grey. Screens burning, coffee gone cold, six time zones awake inside one small room. We answered the hundred small disasters one by one. We made it real.

We were going to get her.

---

I should have slept. I didn't. I never do. Not when she's that close.

I was awake in the dead hour before dawn. Just me and the screen, the last two things still lit in the world. And I did what I always do before I let myself believe anything.

I went back and looked at her marker one more time.

I don't know why. To touch it, I suppose. To prove the heartbeat was still there. That the building still held her. That all the scaffolding we'd built through the night rested on something true.

I looked at the marker.

And the marker was in the wrong place.

I have to go carefully here. This is the moment the floor gave way. And the giving of it deserves to be felt right.

The system doesn't move things. The system finds the truth and holds it still and lets *me* be the one who wavers. The marker we'd built the whole operation around — the bright certain point near the Old Port — had sat in the same spot since the night before. Since the moment it placed her in that dead hospital. Broke my heart. Gave it back to me.

Now it sat somewhere else.

Somewhere cleaner. Easier. One road in. No road out. A place you would never put a hostage. A place you would absolutely put a trap.

Someone had reached inside the one thing in the world I trusted. The thing I'd built with my own sleepless hands to find the woman I loved. And they had moved her marker. Gently. In the dark. The way you move a lamp to throw a shadow exactly where you want a man to walk.

She wasn't there. She had never been moved. Only the marker had — touched in the night by a hand that knew exactly how I think. A hand that had watched me build my entire rescue around one bright point. Then, patient, bloodless, satisfied with itself, had picked that point up and set it down somewhere I would come running.

Someone had moved her marker overnight.

The location was bait.

And it was set for me.
