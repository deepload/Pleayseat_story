# Chapter 14: Trust No One

The photograph was three days old.

I've tried to name what those words did to me. Four words. Alone in a grey room in Brussels. The hour when the rest of the world is asleep and people like me are not.

Three days. Seventy-two hours earlier, while I was war-gaming a monster, while I was counting poisoned gifts, while I was pacing six steps back and forth like a man awaiting his verdict — Clara was *alive*.

Not a memory. Not a trail. Not a heartbeat I kept forcing myself to feel because I needed it to exist. Alive. In a body. Breathing the same air as me, three days ago. Close enough to a lens for someone to capture her.

The room around me held the only light I've ever really known. Screen light. Blue. No mercy in it. A half-drunk cup of cold coffee keeping watch near the keyboard, forgotten the way everything gets forgotten that isn't her. And below the floorboards, the deep murmur of machines. That server hum that is, for people like me, the sound silence makes.

I had been hunting a ghost so long I had nearly forgotten she was a woman. The photograph put her back in the world. Her shoulder. The way she held herself, even there — chin up, spine straight, that exhausted defiance I had fallen in love with across a card table in a desert two and a half years ago.

She was thinner. She was holding something in her hand I couldn't make out. She wasn't looking at the lens. She was looking past it. To the left. The way she looked at a problem she had already solved three moves ahead.

That alone would have broken me.

But it wasn't what broke me.

What broke me was the man behind her shoulder. Blurred. Caught mid-movement. Never meant to be in the frame.

I knew that face.

I knew it the way you know a face from the other side of a dinner table. I knew it the way you know a voice that has called you *brother* down a bad line at three in the morning, on a channel that five people in the whole world knew existed.

I knew him.

I sat completely still. The way you sit still when a wasp lands on your hand. And I did not let myself finish the thought. Because the thought was a door. And I have spent my whole life being the man who cannot stop himself from opening doors.

---

I have to talk about trust. This is the chapter where I have no choice.

I broke locks for a living. A hacker. The word the newspapers write with a shiver, the word we say quietly in the industry, the way you say a surname.

You would think it makes you suspicious of everyone. For a while, yes. But here is the secret nobody admits: the breaking life runs entirely on trust. You cannot do it alone. You trust the man watching the street. You trust the woman who carries the thing away so your hands stay clean. You trust the voice that says *go* and the voice that says *stop*.

Get burned once and you are finished. Not arrested. *Finished.* A ghost among ghosts. A handle nobody dares to say, going dark channel by channel until the last door closes.

So we trusted each other the way drowning men grip a rope. Completely. With all our weight. Because the other option was the water.

That is how I had built my family. Not on contracts. On the rope. People who live in the cracks, like me. People who closed one door behind them to open another one under a new name. Three times, ten times, until they were nothing but a rumour on the network.

Ifeoma in Lagos, who had watched my back for fifteen years and prayed for my soul the whole time. Yuri in Kyiv — suspicious as a cat in a thunderstorm, a man who never spoke twice on the same channel and changed his handle the way others change clothes; he was right to be that way. Basti in Marseille, who had served time for me and never breathed a word of it. Noa in Tel Aviv, who still picked up when I called, even though I had given her every reason in the world to let it ring.

And Marchetti. Not family, never family. A rope of different weight. The hard, tired hand of the state, which I needed if I ever wanted to do this the right way.

The right way. I kept repeating it: do things the right way. Because the wrong way was so close. Always. Right under my skin.

The wrong way was to find the Surgeon and undo him in silence, in the dark, the way the man I had buried knew how. No proof. No trial. Just him and me and the long memory of a world that never forgets a debt.

The right way was slower. Crueler. The right way was building something a court could hold. Evidence that didn't lie. A truth so solid no protected hand could wave it aside.

Clara had taught me the right way. *The evidence is the only weapon they can never take from you,* she had said. *Everyone lies. Build the thing that proves it, and you hold the only gun in the room.*

I had built that thing.

And now, at the bottom of this night, with a three-day-old photograph glowing on the screen and a face I loved standing behind the woman I loved harder, I was going to have to point it at my own family.

---

I didn't want to.

And that has to be said. Said plainly. Because what I did next was the bravest and the most cowardly thing I have ever done. Both at the same time. The only honest kind of courage.

The cowardly part: I almost talked myself out of it. *Coincidence*, I told the empty room, the screen, the cold cup. *The world is small. The road runs through that city. Everyone who works the road ends up in that frame eventually. It means nothing. You're exhausted. You've been seeing the Surgeon in every shadow. Go sleep. In the morning it'll be someone else's face.*

I even half-stood. Ready to go and not-sleep on the couch. To let it wait until morning. To give the man behind her shoulder the only thing I had left to give: the benefit of the doubt.

Then I sat back down. Because of Clara.

Because Clara would not have let it wait. Clara, who had let herself be turned into cargo to get inside the belly of the beast, did not operate on the benefit of the doubt. She operated on proof. *Someone has to protect the children*, she would say. And then she would do the unbearable thing, the exact thing, the thing that cost her everything. Because the children could not afford her comfort.

The least I could do was refuse my own. For one more hour.

So I asked the system the question I had been refusing to ask.

The *how* doesn't matter. I won't say it. The *how* is a burial. The *what* is everything.

I asked it: *Is someone lying to me?*

---

Here is the thing about lies. A person can tell you they were here when they were somewhere else, and you believe them. Love makes you stupid. Friendship makes you soft. The human face is an excellent liar.

But the world keeps its own records. The world loves nobody. It notes where a man actually was, and when. In a thousand small honest ways, without meaning to. A light that turns on. A footprint. A crumb left somewhere no one was supposed to be.

And almost nobody thinks to check. Because checking on someone you love feels like a sin.

I committed the sin.

I took the things my family had told me. *I was here. I was there. I couldn't reach you. I was dark all week. I didn't see the message until —* All those small true-sounding phrases I had swallowed without chewing. Because they came from mouths I trusted. From channels I believed were clean.

And I laid them beside what the world had recorded in silence. I asked the tool to do the one thing it does better than any human alive: look at a story and at the truth side by side, without love, without tenderness, without flinching, and tell me where the two came apart.

It is a horrible thing to do. I want that on the record. The loneliest thing I have ever asked a machine. Lonelier than finding her heartbeat in the silence. Lonelier than mapping the trucks. Because this time I was not hunting a monster I had never met. I was checking my own people. Putting the rope under a microscope. Looking for the fray.

It didn't take long. It never does. That is the cruelty of what I built. It doesn't agonise. It doesn't hope. It doesn't lie awake at four in the morning with a cold cup and a burning screen. It *answers*. Flatly. A bird dropped at your feet, wanted or not.

And the answer was: yes.

Someone was lying to me.

The stories didn't all match the world. Most did — most of my family had been exactly where they said, loving me from a distance exactly the way they claimed. But not all. Somewhere in the warm braid of voices I leaned on, one thread ran backward. One person had named a place. The world had recorded another. One mouth had said *dark all week* when the world remembered a light. A presence somewhere that should have been dark.

The tool did not give me a name. I want that to be clear. Because later it matters. Because later it nearly kills me. No face. No handle. It is not an oracle. I am not a man who gets to skip to the end. It told me only what it could prove: that the leak was real. That a traitor existed. That somewhere in the family I had built from broken people, from rope and fifteen years of trust, one hand was passing our secrets to the dark. One by one. Under the table.

I sat with that for a long time. The screen still burning. The photograph still glowing. Clara's shoulder. The half-turned face behind her I refused to name.

A traitor. In the family.

It is one thing to know, coldly, as an abstraction, that anyone can be turned. That there is always a lever, a debt, a fear, a price. It is another to carry the proof of it in your chest at four in the morning. To feel it settle there like a swallowed stone.

Because if one of them was lying — if one of the voices that called me *brother*, that hung up first so I wouldn't hear what the love cost them, that prayed for my soul and did time in my place — if *one* of them had a hand in the dark, then I had led the people I loved most into a burning building while one of them held the door open.

And worse. Far worse. The worst didn't land until the final line.

If one of them was leaking — then everything I had told them, every plan, every step, every crumb of how close I was getting to her —

PHANTOM MERCY knew it too.

Which meant Clara, inside the body of the beast, holding her breath and her count and her chain of evidence, somewhere she was never supposed to reach — was three days from a mistake I had helped set up.

---

The phone rang.

Not the Signal phone. Not the family line. The other one. The cold one. The official one. The heavy-rope line that only one man in the world had the number to.

I had given it to him myself. In an underground parking garage in Geneva that smelled of concrete and old rain. Against every instinct I had. Against everything my years in the cracks had taught me about men who carry badges. Because doing things the right way meant keeping one foot inside the law. Even when the law was the last place I wanted to put it.

Four-oh-three in the morning.

I watched it ring. I have a rule about phones that ring at four in the morning. Same rule I have about doors in the dark and channels that light up when they should be dead: never, ever open them. I have broken that rule every single time. That is the full story of my life. And probably the reason for all of it.

I answered.

"You're awake," said Commandant Thierry Marchetti. That voice like a man dragging a chair across stone. Tired. Always tired. Tired the way a country is tired.

"Good. I hoped you would be. The ones who sleep at four in the morning are no use to me."

"It's a charming hour to call, Commandant."

"It's a charming hour to be alive," he said. "Most of the people who do what we do aren't, at this stage. Be grateful."

Silence. I could hear something behind him. Not Kyiv drones. Not Marseille gulls. Just the flat hum of a room with no windows. A room that exists only to hold men who don't sleep.

"I'll be brief. Brief is all I have left. We need to meet. Not by phone. The phone is a confessional where the priest sells the tickets."

My skin had gone cold. I didn't yet know why. That not-knowing is the worst part of any night. The gap between the bad feeling and the bad thing.

"Why now?"

"Because you've gotten close," Marchetti said. "Closer than you should be able to. Closer than is *safe*. For you. And for someone we both care about."

He stopped. He chose a smaller word than the one he was reaching for. The way the state always does.

"I've watched you move. I've watched you build whatever you've built. I don't understand it and I don't want to. I only know that you found things my entire service couldn't find. That frightens me. Very little frightens me anymore. So when something does, I pay attention."

He let that sit. He was good at that. That is how hard tired men get you — they leave a silence, and you fill it with your own fear.

"Clara trusted you," he said, gentler than I had ever heard him. "She told me once that if everything went wrong, she would have left something behind. A door only one man could open. A message for the one who loved her, somewhere no one else would look, locked with a thing only the two of them knew."

A breath, dragged across stone.

"She wouldn't tell me what was inside. Or where. Only that it existed. Her insurance, she called it. Against the day the network reached inside her own house. *Her dead drop.* So I'm calling to ask you, man to man, at this charming hour —"

And the floor dropped out from under me a second time that night. Slowly. The way the worst things always come. A photograph developing in its bath. Grey water remembering it was a face.

Because I had never told him.

I had never told anyone outside my circle about the dead drop. Not that it existed. Not what shape it took. Not that it was locked with the date of a card table in a desert. It was the most secret thing I carried. Clara's last gift. Her insurance. The door with both our names on it. I had opened it in the dark with my own hands. And I had told exactly four people in the world it was real. Four handles. Four voices. Every one of them had called me *brother* or *my love*. Every one of them was family. And the tool had just finished proving that one of them was a liar.

Marchetti already knew about the dead drop.

Which meant he had learned it from someone close enough to know.

Which meant the leak was not out there in the dark, with the Surgeon and the trucks and the poisoned gifts.

The leak was inside my own circle.
