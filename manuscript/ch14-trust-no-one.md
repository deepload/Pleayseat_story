# Chapter 14: Trust No One

The photograph was three days old.

I want you to understand what those four words did to me, sitting alone in a grey Brussels room at the bottom of a night with no floor in it. Three days old. Which meant that seventy-two hours ago, while I was war-gaming a monster and counting poisoned gifts and pacing six steps like a man waiting for terrible news — Clara was *alive*. Not a memory. Not a trail. Not a heartbeat I'd convinced myself I could still feel through the wire because I needed to. Alive, in a body, breathing the same cruel air I was, three days ago, close enough to a camera to be caught by it.

I'd been hunting a ghost for so long I'd half-forgotten she was a woman. The photograph put her back in the world. It showed me her shoulder, the set of it, the way she held herself even now — chin up, spine a line, that exhausted defiance I'd fallen in love with across a card table in a desert three years ago. She was thinner. There was something in her hand I couldn't make out. She wasn't looking at the camera. She was looking past it, at something to the left, the way she looked at a problem she was already three moves into solving.

That alone would have undone me.

But it wasn't what undid me.

What undid me was the man behind her shoulder, slightly out of focus, caught mid-turn, not meant to be in the frame at all — and I knew his face. I knew it the way you know a face you've eaten dinner across from. I knew it the way you know a voice that's said *brother* to you down a bad line at three in the morning.

I knew him.

And I sat very still, the way you sit when a wasp lands on the back of your hand, and I did not let myself finish the thought, because the thought was a door, and I had spent my whole life being the kind of man who can't stop himself from opening doors.

---

Let me tell you about trust, since this is the chapter where I have to.

I am a man who broke into things for a living. You'd think that would make me suspicious of everyone, and once it did. But the secret nobody tells you about the breaking life is that it runs entirely on trust. You can't do it alone. You trust the man who watches the street while you work. You trust the woman who carries the thing out so your hands stay clean. You trust the voice that says *go now* and the voice that says *don't.* Betray once in that world and you're finished — not arrested, *finished,* a ghost among ghosts, a name people stop saying. So we trusted each other the way drowning men hold a rope: completely, and with everything, because the alternative was the water.

That's how I'd built my family. Not on contracts. On the rope. Ifeoma in Lagos, who'd watched my back for fifteen years and prayed for my soul the whole time. Yuri in Kyiv, paranoid as a cat in a thunderstorm and right about it more often than I could bear. Basti in Marseille, who'd done time so I wouldn't have to and never once mentioned it. Noa in Tel Aviv, who still picked up when I called even though I'd given her every reason in the book to let it ring. And Marchetti — not family, never family, but rope of a different weight, the hard tired hand of the state I needed if I was ever going to do this the right way.

The right way. I kept telling myself the right way. Because the wrong way was so close, always, just under my skin. The wrong way was to find the Surgeon and unmake him, quietly, the way the buried me knew how. The right way was slower and crueler and meant building a thing a court could hold, evidence that didn't lie, a truth so solid that no protected hand could wave it away. Clara had taught me the right way. *The evidence is the only weapon they can't take from you,* she'd said. *Everyone lies. Build the thing that proves it, and you hold the only gun in the room.*

I had built that thing.

And now, at the bottom of a floorless night, with a three-day-old photograph glowing on the screen and a face I loved standing one step behind the woman I loved harder, I was going to have to point it at my own family.

---

I didn't want to.

I need you to know how much I didn't want to, because what I did next was the bravest and most cowardly thing I have ever done, and it was both at the same time, which is the only honest kind of brave.

The cowardly part: I almost talked myself out of it. *Coincidence,* I said to the empty room. *The world is small. The road runs through that city; everyone who works the road passes through that frame eventually. It means nothing. You're tired. You're seeing the Surgeon in every shadow because you spent a morning becoming him. Go to sleep. Wake up. The face will be someone else's.*

I even half-stood, to go and not-sleep on the couch instead, to let it wait until morning, to give the man behind her shoulder the benefit of the only thing I had left to give anyone: doubt.

And then I sat back down, because of Clara.

Because Clara wouldn't have let it wait. Clara, who let herself be turned into cargo to get inside the body of the beast, did not deal in the benefit of the doubt. She dealt in proof. *Someone has to protect the children,* she'd say, and then she'd do the unbearable thing, the exact thing, the thing that cost her everything, because the children couldn't afford her comfort. The least I could do — the very least — was refuse my own comfort for one more hour.

So I asked the system the question I'd been refusing to ask.

I won't tell you how it does what it does. I promised you that a long time ago and I'll keep promising it, because the *how* is a funeral I won't make you sit through, and the *what* is the only thing that matters anyway. So let me just tell you what I asked it, in the only words that are true.

I asked it: *Is someone lying to me?*

---

Here is the thing about lies. A person can tell you they were in one place when they were in another, and you'll believe them, because love makes you stupid and friendship makes you kind and the human face is a very good liar. But the world keeps its own quiet records. The world doesn't love anybody. The world notices where a man actually was, and when, and it writes it down in a thousand small honest ways without meaning to — and most people never think to check, because checking on someone you love feels like a sin.

I committed the sin.

I took the things my family had told me — the *I was here, I was there, I couldn't reach you because, I was dark all week, I didn't see the message until* — all the small true-sounding sentences I'd swallowed without chewing because they came from mouths I trusted. And I laid them next to what the world had quietly recorded. And I asked the tool to do the one thing it does better than any human being who ever lived: to look at a story and at the truth side by side, without love, without kindness, without flinching, and to tell me where the two peeled apart.

It is a horrible thing to do. I want that on the record. It is the loneliest thing I have ever asked a machine to do for me, lonelier than finding her heartbeat in the silence, lonelier than mapping the trucks, because this time I wasn't asking it to find a monster I'd never met. I was asking it to check my own people. To put the rope under a microscope and look for the fray.

It didn't take long. It never takes long. That's the cruelty of the thing I built — it doesn't agonise, it doesn't hope, it doesn't lie awake. It just *answers,* flatly, a bird laid at your feet, whether you wanted the bird or not.

And the answer was: yes.

Someone was lying to me.

The stories didn't all line up with the world. Most of them did — most of my family had been exactly where they said, doing exactly what they said, loving me from a distance exactly the way they'd claimed. But not all of them. Somewhere in the warm braid of voices I'd been leaning on, one thread ran the wrong way. One person had told me a place and the world had quietly written down a different place. One mouth had said *dark all week* while the world remembered the light.

The tool didn't tell me a name. I want to be clear about that, because later it matters, because later it nearly kills me. It didn't hand me a face. It is not an oracle and I am not a man who gets to skip to the end. It told me only the thing it could prove: that the leak was real. That a traitor existed. That somewhere in the family I'd built out of broken people and rope and fifteen years of trust, there was a hand passing things to the dark.

I sat with that for a long time. The photograph still glowing. Clara's shoulder. The half-turned face behind it I refused to name.

A traitor. In the family.

It is one thing to know, in the abstract cold way you know about gravity, that anyone can be turned. It is another to hold the proof of it in your chest at four in the morning and feel it sit there like a stone you've swallowed. Because if one of them was lying — if one of the voices that called me *brother,* that hung up first so I wouldn't hear what the love cost them, that prayed for my soul and watched my back and did time in my place — if *one* of them had a hand in the dark, then I had led the people I loved most into a fire while one of them held the door open for the thing that was burning us.

And worse. Far worse, the worse that didn't fully land until the chapter's last line. If one of them was leaking — then everything I'd told them, every door I'd let them stand in, every plan, every step, every breadcrumb of how close I was getting to her —

PHANTOM MERCY knew it too.

Which meant Clara, inside the body of the beast, holding her breath and her count, was three days from a frame and a noose I'd helped tie.

---

The phone rang.

Not the Signal phone. Not the family line. A different phone — the cold, official one, the one with the heavy rope, the one only one man in the world had the number to. I'd given it to him myself, in a parking garage in Geneva that smelled of concrete and rain, against every instinct I had, because doing this the right way meant having one foot inside the law even when the law was the last place I wanted my foot.

It was three minutes past four in the morning.

I looked at it ring. I have a rule about phones that ring at four in the morning, which is the same as my rule about doors in the dark, which is that you should never, ever open them, and I have broken that rule every single time, which is the entire story of my life and very possibly the reason for all of it.

I answered.

"You're awake," said Commandant Thierry Marchetti, in that voice like a man dragging a chair across a stone floor. Tired. Always tired. Tired the way a country is tired, a tiredness with no bottom to it. "Good. I hoped you would be. The ones who sleep at four in the morning are no use to me."

"It's a charming hour to call, Commandant."

"It's a charming hour to be alive," he said. "Most people aren't, by now, who do what we do. Be grateful." A pause. I could hear something behind him — not Kyiv drones, not Marseille gulls, just the flat hum of a room with no windows, a room that existed only to hold men who couldn't sleep. "I'll be brief, because brief is all I have left. We need to meet. Not on the phone. The phone is a confession box with the priest selling tickets."

My skin had gone cold, and I didn't yet know why, and the not-knowing is the worst part of any night, the gap between the bad feeling and the bad thing. "Why now?"

"Because you've gotten close," Marchetti said. "Closer than you should be able to. Closer than is *safe* — for you, and for someone we both — " he stopped, and chose a smaller word than the one he'd been reaching for, the way the state always does — "someone we both have an interest in. I've watched you move. I've watched you build whatever it is you've built. I don't understand it and I don't want to. I only know that you've found things my entire service couldn't, and that frightens me, and very little frightens me anymore, so when it does I pay attention."

He let that sit. He was good at letting things sit. It's how the hard tired men get you — they leave a silence and let you fill it with your own fear.

"Clara trusted you," he said, gentler than I'd ever heard him. "She told me, once, that if it ever went all the way wrong, there'd be a thing she left behind. A door only one man could open. A message for the one who loved her, in a place no one else would think to look, locked with a thing only the two of them knew." A breath, dragged across stone. "She wouldn't tell me what was in it. She wouldn't even tell me where. She only told me it existed — her insurance, she called it, against the day the network reached inside her own house. *Her dead drop.* So I'm calling to ask you, man to man, at this charming hour — "

And the floor went out from under me a second time that night, slower, the way the worst ones always come, a developing photograph, grey water remembering it was a face.

Because I had never told him.

I had never told anyone outside my own circle about the dead drop. Not the existence of it, not the shape of it, not that it was locked with the date of a card table in a desert. It was the most secret thing I carried — Clara's last gift, her insurance, the door with both our names on it. I'd opened it in the dark with my own hands and told four people in the whole world it was real, and every one of those four people had called me *brother* or *love,* and every one of them was family, and the tool had just finished proving that one of them was a liar.

Marchetti already knew about the dead drop.

Which meant he'd learned it from someone close enough to know.

Which meant the leak wasn't out there in the dark with the Surgeon and the trucks and the poisoned gifts.

The leak was inside my own circle.
