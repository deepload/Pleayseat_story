# Chapter 9: The Diversion

The dead drop opened at 3:11 in the morning, and for exactly nineteen seconds I was the happiest man alive.

I had typed the date with a shaking hand — the day we met, the only password she would ever have trusted me to remember — and the little locked thing she'd left me unfolded like a flower in the dark. I didn't read it yet. I just sat there, breathing, the way you breathe when a doctor says the word *benign*. She had left me something. She had known I would come. Across all that distance, across whatever room they were keeping her in, she had reached out one hand in the dark and left it open for mine to find.

Nineteen seconds.

Then every screen I owned turned red.

---

I have been in a lot of bad rooms in my life. I have been on the wrong end of men with guns and the wrong end of women with reasons, and I once spent a memorable forty minutes locked in a meat freezer in Antwerp by a man who, in fairness, had warned me. So I know the feeling of the floor tilting. I know what it is to have the ground decide, all at once, that it is no longer on your side.

This was that. The red was that.

Somewhere out in the dark, far away and very close, the thing I had built — the thing that was my eyes and my ears and my sixth sense, the thing that listened to a whole country's silence and found her heartbeat in it — was being set on fire.

I don't mean a real fire. There was no smoke in the Brussels apartment, just the rain and the radiator and the cold sweat starting between my shoulder blades. But it was a fire all the same. Someone had thrown a torch into the house I'd built to find her, and the house was screaming.

I want to tell you what that's like without telling you how it works, because the *how* doesn't matter and would only bore you, and because in that moment I didn't care how. I cared that it was happening. Imagine you're a lighthouse keeper, and your whole job, your whole reason for existing, is to keep one light burning so a ship can find its way home in the storm. And imagine someone starts a fire in your lighthouse — not to burn it down, not at first, but to fill it with smoke. To blind you. To make so much noise and heat and panic that you forget about the light, that you drop everything and run for the buckets, and while you're choking and shouting and fighting the flames, the ship slips past in the dark and is gone.

That's what it was. A diversion. A fire set to make me look away.

And the worst part — the part that made my hands go cold around the coffee cup — was that it worked, for a second. Because the first thing the fire did was reach for the one place I couldn't bear to lose.

It reached for the message Clara had left me.

---

There's a thing they do, the people who hurt the world. The polished ones. The ones with budgets and patience. They don't just attack you. They attack you *while you're reaching for what you love.* They wait until your hand is out, until you're vulnerable and soft and human, and then they kick.

The Surgeon had done it. I knew it was him the way you know a smell from childhood. The timing was too clean, too cruel, too *surgical.* He hadn't set the fire when I was hunting him. He'd set it the instant the dead drop opened — the instant I had something to protect. He'd been watching. He'd been waiting for me to find her hand in the dark, just so he could try to take it.

That's a particular kind of cruelty, and I'll be honest with you: for one ugly second it didn't make me afraid. It made me want to kill him.

I have told you about that want. It lives in me. It is the old life, the buried life, the fast and dirty and satisfying life, and it never fully dies, it just sleeps, and it has very light sleep. In that second it woke all the way up and stretched and said, in its reasonable voice: *He's right there. He left the door open setting this fire. Reach across and burn his whole world down. Make him feel it. You can do it in your sleep.*

And I could have. God help me, I could have.

But Clara built me to believe proof. And a man fighting a fire does not get to also be an arsonist. So I put the want back in its cage — not gently, I never can — and I did the only thing that was left.

I held the line.

---

Here is what holding the line looks like, told as a feeling, because that's the only true way to tell it.

You are alone in a small room at three in the morning and the whole world is on fire and there is nobody coming to help. You cannot call the fire department. There is no fire department for this. There is just you, and the thing you built, and the people who love you who are all asleep in time zones where the sun is up and the day is ordinary, and you are not going to wake them, because if you wake them you bring them into the fire too, and you swore you wouldn't.

So you sit very still. You stop panicking — not because you feel calm, you don't, you feel like a man drowning in a phone booth, but because panic is exactly what the fire wants. The fire is *made* of panic. It is shouting at you, *Look here! No, here! No, over HERE!* — a hundred alarms going off at once, every one of them screaming that something precious is being destroyed, and you understand, slowly, coldly, that ninety-nine of those alarms are lies. That's the whole trick of it. Drown the one real signal in a hundred fake ones. Make the truth impossible to hear.

So you stop listening for the alarms. You start listening for *her.*

I built it to find her heartbeat in the silence of a country. Now I asked it to find one true thing in a roomful of liars. Find the one alarm that isn't shouting. Find the quiet thing under all the noise. Hold the door she left open. Hold it shut against everyone else and open for her, and do not, do not, do *not* let go.

I leaned my forehead against the cool glass of the laptop screen, like a man praying at a window, and I said it out loud to an empty room.

"Hold the line."

And the thing I built — the thing a machine had helped me build, in seven sleepless days, for exactly this, for *her* — held the line.

---

I won't pretend it was clean.

It was the longest forty minutes of my life and I aged a year in every one of them. The fire pushed and the line bent and there were moments — three of them, I counted, I'll remember them when I'm old — where I thought I'd lost her message entirely, where the smoke filled everything and the open door she'd left me seemed to vanish into the roar, and a sound came out of me that I'm not proud of and won't describe.

But here's the thing about a fire set to distract you: it tells on the man who set it.

You can't shout *look away* without revealing that there's something you don't want looked at. You can't blind a man without standing in his light. The Surgeon set his fire to make me drop the thread — and in setting it, his hand was all over it, that scarless surgical hand I'd have known anywhere, the hand I'd helped to shape in a life I'd buried. He was so busy making me look away that he forgot I'd built the whole thing to remember a touch. Every time he pushed the fire toward Clara's open door, he left a fingerprint on the door.

I stopped fighting the flames. I started *photographing* them. Not running for buckets — standing in the middle of it, soaking it all in, every cruel clever push, and letting the thing I built do what Clara made it for: turn it all into proof. Sealed, stamped, court-cold proof that couldn't be charmed or bought or burned. He thought he was blinding me. He was signing a confession in front of a witness who never blinks.

*Do it right,* I told myself, in her voice, because I'd lost my own again. *He wants you to do it fast. So do it right. Make him hate how right you do it.*

There was a moment, near the end, when I almost called Basti.

Basti is my man in Marseille — a locksmith now, a smuggler once, a friend always, a big laughing bear of a man who has opened more doors that didn't belong to him than any honest person should admit knowing. If anyone could put boots on the ground in the city where she'd vanished, where the fire seemed to be blowing in from, it was him. My thumb was already on his name. The phone was already warm in my hand.

And then I thought of the meat freezer in Antwerp, and the man who'd warned me, and how I'd walked in anyway because I never could leave a door alone. I thought of all the people I'd loved into the line of fire because I couldn't bear to be afraid by myself. And I put the phone down, third time that week, because waking Basti meant pointing the fire at someone I loved, and I had sworn — to her, to the empty room, to the man I was trying to become — that this time the cost would land on me and only me until I knew exactly what I was dragging people into.

It cost me. It always costs me. But I put it down.

The fire began to fail. Not because I beat it — you don't beat a fire like that, you outlast it, you make holding the line cost him more than the fire is worth. Slowly, sullenly, the alarms went quiet, one by one, the lies running out of air. The smoke thinned. And the door — her door, the one she'd left open for me in the dark — was still open. Bent. Scorched at the edges. Held.

And in the holding, I noticed something I'd remember later as the first loose thread of everything that came after.

The fire was too good. Too practised. You don't learn to set a fire like that overnight — to know exactly when a man's hand will be out, exactly which door he'll be reaching through, exactly how long he can hold a line before he breaks. You learn it by watching. For a long time. By living in the walls of a house long before you decide to burn it. The Surgeon hadn't found me last night. He'd been in my walls for *years,* patient as rot, and the fire wasn't the beginning of anything. It was the moment the rot finally moved. Whatever PHANTOM MERCY was, it had roots that went down further and older than I'd let myself imagine, and I had the cold sense, standing in the smoke, that I was about to start pulling on one and find no bottom.

But that was a fear for tomorrow.

I had held the line. The light was still burning. The ship had not slipped past.

I sat back. My shirt was wet through. My hands wouldn't quite stop. And I started, finally, to laugh — that terrible cracked laugh that comes out of a man who has been afraid for too long and has nowhere left to put it — because I'd done it, I'd held it, and somewhere out there the Surgeon was learning that his old brother didn't break the way he used to.

And that's exactly when it landed.

---

In the centre of it all — in the eye of the dead fire, in the quiet after the storm, through the one door that hadn't closed — a message arrived.

Not the old dead drop. Not the thing she'd left me weeks ago and locked with the day we met. Something new. Something *now.* It came in while the smoke was still clearing, threaded right through the middle of the chaos, as if it had used the Surgeon's own fire for cover — as if someone had waited for exactly this storm to slip a note under exactly this door.

My laugh died in my throat.

Three lines. That's all it was. Three short lines in the dark, and the whole room went silent around them, the rain and the radiator and my own heartbeat, all of it, gone.

*They know you opened it.*

*Stop looking, or the children pay for it.*

*This is your only warning.*

I read it once. I read it again. I read it a third time, the way I read everything that matters, until the words wore smooth.

A threat. Plain and cold. *Stop looking.* Of course it was a threat — the fire had failed to blind me, so now they'd try to scare me, and a man who hurts children does not bluff about hurting children. Every nerve in my body understood it. *Walk away. Walk away or they pay.*

I should have been afraid. I was afraid. But that's not what stopped my heart.

What stopped my heart was the last thing on the message. After the three lines. After the threat. Down in the corner, small and quiet, the way you sign something you mean — a single mark. A signature I had read a hundred times on a hundred messages in a better life. The one she used instead of her name, because she said names could be stolen but a *manner* couldn't, the slant of a hand, the family resemblance of a person.

A lowercase delta.

Her delta. Drawn the way only she drew it.

The threat that told me to stop looking for her — the threat that promised to hurt the children if I didn't walk away — was signed in her own hand.

I sat in the dark, soaked through, the fire dead around me, and the floor tilted one last time and would not come back level. Because there were only two ways that mark got onto that message, and I could not breathe past either one.

Either she had sent it herself — Clara, reaching out of the dark, *warning* me, telling me with the only sign she trusted that this was real, that the danger was real, that for the love of God I should stop.

Or they had found out what her sign meant.

Which meant they had her hands.
