# Chapter 9: The Diversion

The dead drop opened at 3:11 in the morning, and for exactly nineteen seconds I was the happiest man alive.

I had typed the date with a shaking hand — the day we met, the only password she would ever have trusted me to remember — and the little locked thing she'd left me unfolded like a flower in the dark. I didn't read it yet. I just sat there, breathing, the way a man breathes when a doctor says the word *benign*. The apartment was dark the way it had been dark for seven nights now — every light off, every curtain drawn, only the cold blue wash of the screens on my face, the way we all live, the people who work in the seams of the world, faces lit from below like men telling ghost stories. She had left me something. She had known I would come. Across all that distance, across whatever room they were keeping her in, she had reached out one hand in the dark and left it open for mine to find.

Nineteen seconds.

Then every screen I owned turned red.

---

I have been in a lot of bad rooms in my life. I have been on the wrong end of men with guns and the wrong end of women with reasons, and I once spent a memorable forty minutes locked in a meat freezer in Antwerp by a man who, in fairness, had warned me. So I know the feeling of the floor tilting. I know what it is to have the ground decide, all at once, that it is no longer on my side.

This was that. The red was that.

Somewhere out in the dark — in that other country, the one without maps, the one where people like me go by names our mothers never gave us and trade in things that don't show up in the daylight — the thing I had built was being set on fire. The thing that was my eyes and my ears and my sixth sense, the thing that listened to a whole country's silence and found her heartbeat in it. They were burning it down.

I don't mean a real fire. There was no smoke in the Brussels apartment, just the rain on the glass and the radiator ticking and the low animal hum of the machines under the desk, the sound that had been the only company I'd kept for a week, and the cold sweat starting between my shoulder blades. The coffee at my elbow had gone cold hours ago and I drank it anyway, because that's what the night asks of a man. But it was a fire all the same. Someone had thrown a torch into the house I'd built to find her, and the house was screaming.

There's no clean way to say what that's like, and the *how* of it doesn't matter and never did, because in that moment I didn't care how. I cared that it was happening. I thought of a lighthouse keeper whose whole job, whose whole reason for existing, is to keep one light burning so a ship can find its way home in the storm. And someone starts a fire in his lighthouse — not to burn it down, not at first, but to fill it with smoke. To blind him. To make so much noise and heat and panic that he forgets about the light, that he drops everything and runs for the buckets, and while he's choking and shouting and fighting the flames, the ship slips past in the dark and is gone.

That's what it was. A diversion. A fire set to make me look away.

And the worst part — the part that made my hands go cold around the cup — was that it worked, for a second. Because the first thing the fire did was reach for the one place I couldn't bear to lose.

It reached for the message Clara had left me.

---

There's a thing they do, the people who hurt the world. The polished ones. The ones with budgets and patience, the ones who buy the silence of whole cities and never once leave a fingerprint. They don't just attack a man. They attack him *while he's reaching for what he loves.* They wait until his hand is out, until he's vulnerable and soft and human, and then they kick.

The Surgeon had done it. I knew it was him the way a man knows a smell from childhood. The timing was too clean, too cruel, too *surgical.* He hadn't lit the match when I was hunting him, out in the dark of those hidden rooms where men like us have always met, the locked channels and the back doors and the forums that don't have names a man could say out loud. He'd lit it the instant the dead drop opened — the instant I had something to protect. He'd been watching. He'd been waiting for me to find her hand in the dark, just so he could try to take it.

That's a particular kind of cruelty, and the truth of it is ugly: for one ugly second it didn't make me afraid. It made me want to kill him.

I knew that want. It lives in me. It is the old life, the buried life, the fast and dirty and satisfying life — the life of break, not build — and it never fully dies, it just sleeps, and it has very light sleep. In that second it woke all the way up and stretched and said, in its reasonable voice: *He's right there. He left the door open setting this fire. Reach across and burn his whole world down. Make him feel it. You still know every back way into a man's life. You could do it in your sleep.*

And I could have. God help me, I could have. The hands remember. They always remember.

But Clara built me to believe proof. And a man fighting a fire does not get to also be an arsonist. So I put the want back in its cage — not gently, I never can — and I did the only thing that was left.

I held the line.

---

Holding the line was a feeling more than an act, and the feeling is the only true way to set it down.

I was alone in a small room at three in the morning and the whole world was on fire and there was nobody coming to help. I could not call the police. I could not call anyone with a uniform and a badge — that's not who we are, that's never been who we are; we are the ones the world calls when the people with badges have already failed. There is no fire department for this. There was just me, and the thing I built, and the people who loved me who were all asleep in time zones where the sun was up and the day was ordinary — Ifeoma in Lagos with the servers humming around her like a congregation, Yuri in Kyiv who would already be telling me I'd been careless — and I was not going to wake them, because if I woke them I brought them into the fire too, and I had sworn I wouldn't.

So I sat very still. I stopped panicking — not because I felt calm, I didn't, I felt like a man drowning in a phone booth, but because panic is exactly what the fire wants. The fire is *made* of panic. It was shouting, *Look here! No, here! No, over HERE!* — a hundred alarms going off at once, every one of them screaming that something precious was being destroyed, and I understood, slowly, coldly, that ninety-nine of those alarms were lies. That's the whole trick of it. Drown the one real signal in a hundred fake ones. Make the truth impossible to hear.

So I stopped listening for the alarms. I started listening for *her.*

I built it to find her heartbeat in the silence of a country. Now I asked it to find one true thing in a roomful of liars. Find the one alarm that isn't shouting. Find the quiet thing under all the noise. Hold the door she left open. Hold it shut against everyone else and open for her, and do not, do not, do *not* let go.

I leaned my forehead against the cool glass of the screen, like a man praying at a window, the blue light burning my eyes raw, and I said it out loud to an empty room.

"Hold the line."

And the thing I built — the thing a machine had helped me build, in seven sleepless days, for exactly this, for *her* — held the line.

---

I won't pretend it was clean.

It was the longest forty minutes of my life and I aged a year in every one of them. The fire pushed and the line bent and there were moments — three of them, I counted, I'll remember them when I'm old — where I thought I'd lost her message entirely, where the smoke filled everything and the open door she'd left me seemed to vanish into the roar, and a sound came out of me that I'm not proud of and won't describe.

But there's a thing about a fire set to distract a man: it tells on the man who set it.

No man can shout *look away* without revealing that there's something he doesn't want looked at. No man can blind another without standing in his light. That was the law of it, and the Surgeon had broken it the moment he lit the match. He set his fire to make me drop the thread — and in setting it, his hand was all over it, that scarless surgical hand I'd have known anywhere, the signature in the work, the little flourishes a man can't help leaving even when he means to leave nothing, the hand I'd helped to shape in a life I'd buried. He was so busy making me look away that he forgot I'd built the whole thing to remember a touch. Every time he pushed the fire toward Clara's open door, he left a fingerprint on the door.

I stopped fighting the flames. I started *photographing* them. Not running for buckets — standing in the middle of it, soaking it all in, every cruel clever push, and letting the thing I built do what Clara made it for: turn it all into proof. Sealed, stamped, court-cold proof that couldn't be charmed or bought or burned — the one kind of weapon that still means something in a world where everyone lies. He thought he was blinding me. He was signing a confession in front of a witness who never blinks.

*Do it right,* I told myself, in her voice, because I'd lost my own again. *He wants you to do it fast. Fast and dirty, the old way, your way. So do it right. Make him hate how right you do it.*

There was a moment, near the end, when I almost called Basti.

Basti is my man in Marseille — a locksmith now, a smuggler once, a friend always, a big laughing bear of a man who has opened more doors that didn't belong to him than any honest person should admit knowing. One of mine. One of the family I made out of marks and ghosts and people the straight world threw away. If anyone could put boots on the ground in the city where she'd vanished, where the fire seemed to be blowing in from, it was him. My thumb was already on his name. The phone was already warm in my hand.

And then I thought of the meat freezer in Antwerp, and the man who'd warned me, and how I'd walked in anyway because I never could leave a door alone. I thought of all the people I'd loved into the line of fire because I couldn't bear to be afraid by myself. And I put the phone down, third time that week, because waking Basti meant pointing the fire at someone I loved, and I had sworn — to her, to the empty room, to the man I was trying to become — that this time the cost would land on me and only me until I knew exactly what I was dragging people into.

It cost me. It always costs me. But I put it down.

The fire began to fail. Not because I beat it — no one beats a fire like that, a man only outlasts it, makes holding the line cost the other man more than the fire is worth. Slowly, sullenly, the alarms went quiet, one by one, the lies running out of air. The smoke thinned. And the door — her door, the one she'd left open for me in the dark — was still open. Bent. Scorched at the edges. Held.

And in the holding, I noticed something I'd remember later as the first loose thread of everything that came after.

The fire was too good. Too practised. No one learns to set a fire like that overnight — to know exactly when a man's hand will be out, exactly which door he'll be reaching through, exactly how long he can hold a line before he breaks. It's learned by watching. For a long time. By living in the walls of a house long before the decision comes to burn it. The Surgeon hadn't found me last night. He'd been in my walls for *years,* patient as rot, a quiet presence in the dark behind everything, and the fire wasn't the beginning of anything. It was the moment the rot finally moved. Whatever PHANTOM MERCY was, it had roots that went down further and older than I'd let myself imagine, down into that buried country where the real powers never show their faces, and I had the cold sense, standing in the smoke, that I was about to start pulling on one and find no bottom.

But that was a fear for tomorrow.

I had held the line. The light was still burning. The ship had not slipped past.

I sat back. My shirt was wet through. My hands wouldn't quite stop. The machines under the desk had settled back to their patient hum, the only thing in the room that wasn't shaking. And I started, finally, to laugh — that terrible cracked laugh that comes out of a man who has been afraid for too long and has nowhere left to put it — because I'd done it, I'd held it, and somewhere out there in the dark the Surgeon was learning that his old brother didn't break the way he used to.

And that's exactly when it landed.

---

In the centre of it all — in the eye of the dead fire, in the quiet after the storm, through the one door that hadn't closed — a message arrived.

Not the old dead drop. Not the thing she'd left me weeks ago and locked with the day we met. Something new. Something *now.* It came in while the smoke was still clearing, threaded right through the middle of the chaos, slipped in along one of those hidden channels only a handful of people on earth knew how to reach me on — as if it had used the Surgeon's own fire for cover, as if someone had waited for exactly this storm to slip a note under exactly this door.

My laugh died in my throat.

Three lines. That's all it was. Three short lines in the dark, and the whole room went silent around them, the rain and the radiator and the hum of the machines and my own heartbeat, all of it, gone.

*They know you opened it.*

*Stop looking, or the children pay for it.*

*This is your only warning.*

I read it once. I read it again. I read it a third time, the way I read everything that matters, until the words wore smooth.

A threat. Plain and cold. *Stop looking.* Of course it was a threat — the fire had failed to blind me, so now they'd try to scare me, and a man who hurts children does not bluff about hurting children. Every nerve in my body understood it. *Walk away. Walk away or they pay.*

I should have been afraid. I was afraid. But that's not what stopped my heart.

What stopped my heart was the last thing on the message. After the three lines. After the threat. Down in the corner, small and quiet, the way a hand signs something it means — a single mark. A signature I had read a hundred times on a hundred messages in a better life, passed back and forth through the dark between two people who trusted nothing in this world but each other. The one she used instead of her name, because she said names could be stolen, leaked, dumped, traded like everything else down in the seams — but a *manner* couldn't, the slant of a hand, the family resemblance of a person.

A lowercase delta.

Her delta. Drawn the way only she drew it.

The threat that told me to stop looking for her — the threat that promised to hurt the children if I didn't walk away — was signed in her own hand.

I sat in the dark, soaked through, the fire dead around me, and the floor tilted one last time and would not come back level. Because there were only two ways that mark got onto that message, and I could not breathe past either one.

Either she had sent it herself — Clara, reaching out of the dark, *warning* me, telling me with the only sign she trusted that this was real, that the danger was real, that for the love of God I should stop.

Or they had found out what her sign meant.

Which meant they had her hands.
