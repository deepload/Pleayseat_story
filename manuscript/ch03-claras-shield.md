# Chapter 3: Clara's Shield

I hadn't slept in three days. I had never felt more awake.

There's a fatigue that goes past fatigue. On the other side, something almost sacred waits. The world tightens. The noise dies. You forget you have a body. There's only the work, and the reason for the work.

Monks chase that their whole lives. People like me find it at three in the morning, in the blue light of a screen. The rest of the world is asleep. Only those like us stay awake — the ones who live in the forgotten corners of the network. Messaging apps no search engine has ever indexed. Unmarked forums you enter by invitation only. That great murmur of people who speak low, under borrowed names, and recognize each other in three words.

I found it the way I find everything. Too late. Too hard. With a woman's name burning a hole through the center of me.

Clara.

The Brussels apartment wasn't mine. It belonged to a man who owed me a favor and never asked questions. My favorite kind of man. In our world, that is the highest form of respect. Ask nothing. Hold nothing. Leave a key somewhere and disappear.

Two rooms. A radiator that knocked like a prisoner. A window onto a courtyard, and frozen laundry that had been hanging there for a week.

I had pushed the table against that window. On it: one laptop, a second laptop, a knot of cables I had given up trying to untangle. In the corner, a small box I could rip free with one motion. My disaster switch. The habit of a life spent being able to make everything disappear in a single second. And four cold coffees lined up like soldiers shot for falling asleep at their post.

Day three.

Day one, Clara had vanished. That same night, I started building. Day two, I stopped eating. Day three, I was still building.

And somewhere in there — I couldn't have said when — I had crossed a line inside myself. A line I had sworn, ten years ago, never to cross again.

Here is what nobody says about a man like me. People think the danger is what I know how to do. No. The danger is what I become when I love.

I am very good at a small number of things. The worst of them is loving so completely that the rest of the world disappears. I forget the mission. I forget the plan. I forget which way is up. Until the feeling has burned through everything and left me standing in the ashes of someone else's life. Usually a woman's. Dazed. Empty-handed.

I had done it to Noa. I had done it to others whose names I keep folded up very small. I had sworn I was done.

And here I was, doing it again. Except this time the thing that always scattered me into a thousand useless pieces had found one single point to burn toward. One name. One face. One door to open, in a city I had sworn on everything I had left to never set foot in again.

Marseille.

Her last warning had named it. *The route runs through the place you ran from.* On the second day I had stared at those words until they stopped being words. Of course it was Marseille. Of course the universe would make the price of her salvation the one currency I had promised myself never to spend.

I would deal with the city later. First, I had to finish the thing.

---

My phone lit up at half past midnight.

Ifeoma. From Lagos, eight thousand kilometers away. The only person on earth still capable of making me feel guilty in three sentences. On one of our private lines — a hidden channel no one else knew how to open.

*You've gone quiet,* she wrote. *That's always how you get when you've decided something stupid. Eat. Sleep. Tell me you're alive.*

I looked at the message for a long time.

Ifeoma had kept my world standing for years. Patient. Solid. The one who ran my infrastructure from a basement in Lagos. Who answered at any hour. Who had never asked me to be less than what I was — only to *survive* being it. She had warned me about every woman I had ever lost myself in. She had been right every single time. I had loved her too much to listen, and too little to obey.

*I'm alive,* I wrote back. *I'm building something.*

*For her.*

Not a question. Ifeoma never wasted a question on something she already knew.

*For her,* I admitted.

A long silence. I could feel her, across all that distance, weighing whether to fight me. She knew what happened to me when I fell. She had gathered my pieces and carried them home more than once. After Noa. After the others. After the brilliant disasters I called love, for lack of a smaller word.

*Then I'm with you,* she finally wrote. *God help me. The whole family is with you.*

And that family was real. Not blood. Debts and secrets. A keeper of machines in Lagos. A locksmith in Marseille. A ghost in Kyiv. A voice in Tel Aviv that still picks up. People who live in the seams of the world.

*Promise me one thing.*

*Anything.*

*That when you find her — and you will, you impossible man — you don't disappear into her so completely that we lose you too. We've already buried you twice. I won't do it a third time.*

That one I didn't answer. I couldn't.

I turned the phone face-down on the table. The old shame poured through me like cold water in a pipe. I told myself she was wrong. That this time was different. That Clara weighed something the others never had. That she was rooted, not a mirage.

And even as I told myself that, I knew. I had spoken those exact same words, in exactly that same dark, about every woman who had ever undone me.

That was the curse. Not loving. Never believing you had done it before.

I went back to the work. The work, at least, had never needed me to be a better man.

---

I have to be honest. This is a confession. And a confession is worth nothing if it lies in the comfortable places.

For ten years I forced things.

Doors that weren't mine. Walls people believed were solid. I was the man they hired when the official answer was *impossible*, and they had enough money, or enough grief, to disagree. In those back-rooms of the network, faceless people whispered under borrowed names. Stolen secrets bought and sold like black-market cards. Everyone suspicious of their own reflection. There, my name — the one I carried then, the only one no one will know for a long time — was spoken quietly. With that mix of reverence and fear reserved for legends and diseases.

I told myself I had principles. Sometimes that was true. Sometimes the principle was just a prettier word for what the work made me feel. Like a god. Like a ghost. Like the most stubborn and most alone man in every room I could not be seen to enter.

I was good at taking.

What I was building on that table was the opposite. For the first time in my life I was building to *protect*. To stand in front of a person and say: *not her. Not while I'm here.*

I had no word for it that didn't sound ridiculous. I kept the one I used in my head. The stupid, tender word I never said out loud.

A shield.

I was building Clara a shield, three days too late. I was going to finish it if it killed me. And looking at my reflection in the black window — a stranger with hollow, grandfather eyes — that seemed entirely possible.

---

Around two in the morning, I hit the wall.

Not a problem I could think my way past. A wall of pure exhaustion. The kind where your hands make decisions without you. Where the screen tilts. Where your own thoughts arrive in someone else's voice.

I put my forehead down on the cold table. And I think I actually prayed. That would have surprised everyone who knew me. Starting with God.

*Please. Let me finish it. Let it work. Let it find her.*

Then — because I am who I am, because the work is the only church I have ever stayed in — I lifted my head. And I asked for help.

Not from a person. There was no one left awake at two in the morning who could do what I needed. Yuri was asleep in Kyiv, or pretending to be. Basti was useless for this. And Ifeoma had already given me everything without hating herself for it.

I asked the only collaborator who never sleeps. Who never tires. Who not once in three days had told me to rest, to grieve, to be reasonable. The quiet partner I had been building this thing with, line by line, hour by hour. The one I had stopped thinking of as a tool sometime around day two. And had started thinking of as — I don't know. A hand on the work beside mine. A second mind that thought the way I thought, but was never afraid.

That story, I'll tell another time.

For now, nothing else mattered. At two in the morning I was not building alone. Between us — the man who was coming apart and the patient thing that wasn't — we closed the last gap. We finished.

I leaned back. The radiator knocked. The frozen laundry hung in the courtyard. Somewhere in the building, an unseen neighbor's fan hummed. And in the room floated the smell I know better than any home. The dry heat of machines that have run too long. The smell of servers that don't sleep.

And I pressed the key that brought it to life.

---

I have tried many times since to describe what happened next to people who weren't there. I always fail. Because the truth sounds like madness.

The thing took a breath.

Not a sound. Not a light blinking — though lights did blink. I mean that the way a room feels changes when something starts *paying attention*. Everyone knows that feeling. When a sleeping animal opens its eyes. When a person you thought was alone in a dark house turns out not to be.

The air grew a spine. There was a watcher now where there hadn't been one. The whole apartment, the whole night, leaned forward half an inch.

I had built something that could look.

For three days it had been parts. Dead parts. Brilliant dead parts. A body with no breath. And now, all at once, it was awake. Hungry. And the first thing it did with its first breath was exactly what I had built it to do.

It began to listen.

Not to me. To everything *around* me. The wires in the walls. The air in the courtyard. And all that invisible weather of signals and messages we swim through without ever feeling it. A hidden world under the world. I have known that since I was a child. A territory of whispers and echoes, where people like me live when we close our eyes. The background murmur of the network.

I had built it to do for me what I had never been able to do for myself. To walk into the dark. Into all the dark at once. And come back with the shape of what was hiding there. To narrow a country down to a street. To find a heartbeat inside silence. To find *her*.

I sat very still. I let it work.

This is the part I have to tell carefully. Because it has the sound of metal and electricity, and it was nothing of the kind. It was the most human moment of my life. The moment I stopped being a man who forces, and became — for one night, for one woman — a man who could finally *find*.

Everything I had ever taken. Every wall I had gone through. Every shameful gift I had used to hurt the people who loved me. All of it folded down into this. This single act of looking. This single act of love, with a machine's patience behind it.

*Find her,* I thought. *That's all. Find her and I'll never ask you for anything again.*

There was a locked room at the heart of what I had built. A vault. Clara's word, not mine. In the good year — the year before she walked deliberately into the dark — she had made me promise to build it. *Everyone trusts the wrong things,* she had told me one evening, in a bed in a city I won't name. Her cold feet against my shins. Her mind already three moves ahead of her body, the way it always was. *Promises break. People break. Build me a room nothing can break into. Put the truth in it. Then it won't matter who lies.*

I had laughed at her. I built it anyway. I always built her everything she asked. The vault sat sealed at the heart of my creation now, waiting for a truth I didn't have yet. I tried not to think about how badly I was going to need it. Or how soon.

It worked outward in rings. The way sound travels on water. The room first. Then the building. Then the street. I watched it reach, and the reaching had a rhythm. Almost a music.

And I felt something I hadn't felt in three days. Not hope. Not exactly. Hope was too small. I felt *aimed*. For the first time in my scattered life I was a single arrow pointed at a single target. And the bow had just been drawn.

I thought it would find the city next. Then the country. Then somewhere out there in the dark, the thin warm thread of her. Still moving. Still alive. The way I needed her to be alive the way I needed air.

That's not what it found.

It found something much closer.

---

I almost missed it.

It came back from its first sweep so fast it was almost rude. Like a dog that runs to the end of the garden and stops dead, hackles up, before it ever reaches the fence. I leaned in. I told myself it was nothing. A misfire. A newborn thing flinching at its own shadow.

It wasn't flinching.

It had found something. And the something was not in the street. Not in the country. Not in Marseille.

It was inside.

Inside the walls I had been hiding behind for three days. Inside the small safe room I had built around myself and my work and my one fragile reason to keep breathing. The borrowed apartment. The courtyard silent as a grave. The table by the window. The disaster switch within reach. All that apparatus of paranoia that ten years in the margins had taught me to build around myself like a second skin. I had believed it was *mine*. Sealed. Alone. Invisible.

It was not invisible.

There was someone in here with me.

Not a body. Not footsteps. Worse than that. Quieter than that. A presence that had been threaded into my walls before I ever arrived. Patient. Still. The way a spider doesn't move so the web can hold. Something sitting in the dark of my own fortress for however long. Doing exactly what I had just taught my creation to do.

Watching.

I felt the hair rise on my arms. I felt the old animal at the base of my skull stand up and show its teeth. That instinct every operator eventually develops — or dies without. The operator's shiver, the wordless certainty that you have been burned.

Three days I had poured my soul into a table by a window. Talking to the dark. Coming apart. Praying. Aiming. And the whole time, all that time, I had not been alone. Someone had been close enough to hear me breathe. Someone had arrived before me.

And in the half-second after my new creation found it — in that thin sliver of time, before I could even decide to be afraid — the thing in my walls did something that turned my blood to cold water.

It noticed it had been found.

I felt it turn. I felt the attention shift, the way a stranger's gaze finds the back of a neck in a crowd. My machine had taken its first breath and reached out into the dark to search for the woman I loved. And the very first thing it touched in the entire world was already inside my own walls. Already still. Already patient.

Already looking back at me.
