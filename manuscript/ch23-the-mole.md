# Chapter 23: The Mole

Clara walked into the marketplace on purpose.

I'd spent a whole night with that — a screen-lit night, the third coffee gone cold beside the keyboard, the laptop fan breathing like a small tired animal — and I still couldn't hold it without my hands shaking. She hadn't been *taken.* She'd walked in — chin up, spine a line, the way she walked toward every problem she was already three moves into solving — because there was a thing inside that beast that only an insider could touch, and she'd decided that the price of touching it was her own body, and she'd paid it without asking anyone's permission, least of all mine.

That's who she was. That was the woman I'd lost myself trying to find. She didn't get rescued. She *infiltrated.* And somewhere in the cold machinery of the dark — past the locked forums and the channels with no names, down in the part of the web you reach by handle and never by face — she was still in there, still bleeding the truth out drop by drop, still betting her life on a single proposition: that the man who loved her would be smart enough, and brave enough, and *clean* enough, to take what she handed him and finish it the right way.

But she'd left me one instruction, hadn't she. One line in the dead drop I'd been carrying around like a stone in my chest for two weeks.

*Don't trust Station Marseille.*

I'd read it a hundred times and each time I'd flinched past it, the way a man flinches past the one sentence in a letter he doesn't have the strength to believe. Because Station Marseille wasn't an abstraction to me. It wasn't a building, a flag, a desk. It was a person. It was a voice. It was a face I'd eaten dinner across from, laughed with until the wine was gone, trusted with the kind of secrets a man only ever tells the people he's decided to die alongside.

I'd been refusing to ask the question for two weeks.

That night, finally, I asked it.

---

Here is how it works, the not-trusting. Because I'd done it before — I'd done it in this very story, in a grey Brussels room with the blinds down and the servers humming in the next room like a held note — and it had nearly killed me — and a man would think it gets easier the second time.

It gets worse.

The first time, I asked the system whether someone was lying to me, and it said yes, and it didn't give me a name, and I'd told myself the no-name was a mercy I'd be grateful for. I'd lied to myself. The no-name wasn't a mercy. It was a sentence with the verdict left blank, and I'd carried that blank for two weeks the way a man carries a diagnosis he hasn't booked the second appointment for. As long as there's no name, everyone's still innocent. As long as there's no name, you can still sit at the table with all of them and pretend the rot is somewhere else, in someone else, out in the dark where it belongs.

A name closes that door forever.

So I sat in the dark — a different dark now, a Marseille dark, because I'd come south at last, because you can't pull someone out of a fire from another country — phones face-down on the desk, two of them burners, the way a man learns to live when half the people he's ever known went dark on purpose — and I looked at the one phone I'd let myself trust and I thought about not asking. I thought about it hard. I am a coward in exactly one direction and that direction is the people I love. I will walk into any building. I will stare down any monster. But ask me to look at a friend and say *prove to me you're not the one,* and I become a child hiding under a coat, hoping the thunder is somebody else's storm.

But Clara walked in on purpose.

If she could walk into the marketplace with her chin up, knowing what they did to people in there, then I could walk into the one room I'd been refusing to enter — the small, ordinary, human room where a man finds out the worst thing about somebody he loves. She'd spent her courage on the children. The least I could spend mine on was a question.

I asked the system to tell me who was lying.

Not *whether.* I knew whether. I'd known for two weeks. I asked it: **who.**

---

I won't say how it does it. It's the oldest promise I'd made myself, and I wasn't breaking it tonight of all nights. But the part that's mine to keep is what it felt like to wait for the answer.

It felt like the moment after you've thrown something fragile and before it lands. That stretched, ringing nothing where the whole world holds still and you'd give anything — anything — to reach out and catch it, and you can't, because you're the one who threw it, and now you just have to stand there and watch it find the floor.

The system doesn't agonise. It doesn't hope, doesn't flinch, doesn't lie awake. It takes the warm braid of voices a man's been leaning on, and it lays each one next to the cold quiet record the world keeps without meaning to — every trace, every dump, every place a person forgets they left a shadow — and it looks for the place where the story peels apart from the truth. It does this without love. That's its terrible gift and its terrible cruelty both. It loves no one, so it can see everyone clearly, which is the one thing love makes impossible.

I'd given it the braid. All of them. Every voice that had called me *brother* or *love* in the last two weeks — every handle, every dead-of-night call, every encrypted line lit up across four time zones. Every *I was here, I was there, I couldn't reach you, I was dark, I didn't see the message.* And one face more than the rest, one I'd added last, with my own hand shaking on the keys, the way a man signs a thing he prays will come back blank.

The face from Station Marseille.

The liaison. The friend on the inside. The one human being in the entire machinery of the law I had let all the way in.

Who that was matters before the answer, because otherwise the answer is just a plot, and it wasn't a plot, it was an amputation.

---

Let me say it plainly, who she was.

I'll call her by the name I called her, which was never her rank and never her title. Camille. Camille Reyer, INTERPOL liaison out of the Marseille station, the bridge between the world of badges and the world of ghosts — the world of people who live in the seams, who answer to handles and not to names, who go dark and stay gone — and she was the only person who had ever stood with one foot in each and not despised the other foot. The cops thought she was too close to the underworld. The underworld thought she was too close to the cops. Both were right, and that exact double-suspicion was why she was the best, and it was why I'd loved her the moment I met her, because it was the loneliest place in the world to stand and she'd been standing there for twenty years and never once asked anyone to feel sorry for her.

I'd met her four years ago, before Clara, before any of this, when I was still mostly the man who broke things — the man whose handle they still whispered in the channels long after he'd supposedly retired — and she was the cop who could've put me away and chose — for reasons she never fully explained — to put me to use instead. She'd looked across an interview table at a younger, wilder version of me, a man bleeding arrogance to cover the terror, and instead of the cage she'd offered me a chair. *You're going to keep doing the thing you do,* she'd said, lighting a cigarette she wasn't allowed to light in that room. *I can't stop you. Nobody can stop a man who isn't afraid of losing what he has, because you've already decided you have nothing. So here's what I'll do instead. I'll point you.* And she'd pointed me at worse men than me, for four years, and somewhere in there she stopped being my handler and became my friend, and somewhere after that she became something I don't have a clean word for — the older sister I'd never had, the conscience that came with a gun and a sense of humour blacker than mine.

She'd cried at no one's funeral and laughed at everyone's. She kept a photograph of a daughter she never spoke to in a locket she never took off. She'd talked me down from the dirty way more times than Ifeoma had — and Ifeoma had done it from Lagos at three in the morning with the generator coughing under her window — and Camille had done it not with prayer but with the flat unanswerable arithmetic of a woman who'd watched a hundred vigilantes turn into the thing they hunted. *Burn them,* she'd say, *and you save no one and you lose yourself, and then I have to be the one who hunts you, and I'm too old to hunt a man I like.*

When Clara vanished, Camille was the first call I made that wasn't to my own circle — the family I'd built one handle at a time across a decade of dark rooms. When I needed the law's standing without the law's chains, it was Camille who built me the bridge. When I came south, it was Camille who had a flat ready, a clean phone, a car, a route through the city that the bad people didn't watch. Every door I'd walked through in Marseille, she'd unlocked. Every step toward Clara, she'd lit.

She was the one person in this whole story I had stopped checking.

You check everyone, in the breaking life. You check the man who watches the street and the woman who carries the bag and the voice that says *go.* You check the handle behind the handle, the kill switch behind the favour. But there's always one. There's always one a man decides, somewhere along the way, that he'd rather die than suspect, because to suspect them would cost more than the dying. Camille was mine. I'd handed her things I'd never handed Marchetti. I'd handed her the shape of the dead drop. I'd handed her, God help me, the timing of the move on Clara.

So when I added her face to the braid that night, last, with my hand shaking, I was already begging.

*Come back blank,* I told the dark. *Come back blank. Let it be anyone. Let it be a stranger. Let it be the Surgeon wearing a friend's coat. Let it be Marchetti, even — let it be the cold hand of the state I never trusted anyway. But not her. Anyone but her.*

---

The system doesn't take requests.

It took the braid and it did the lonely thing, the bird-at-your-feet thing, the thing it does better than any human who ever lived: it looked at the story and the truth side by side, without love, without kindness, without flinching, and it found the place where they peeled apart.

It didn't take long. It never takes long. That's the cruelty I keep coming back to — that the worst news of a man's life arrives with no ceremony, no drumroll, no chance to brace. One moment I was a man praying at a screen, the cursor blinking, the fan still breathing. The next moment the screen had answered, flatly, completely, the way a coroner answers, and the answer was sitting in the room with me and it would be sitting in the room with me for the rest of my life.

Most of the braid held. It matters, that it held, because it meant my family was clean — Ifeoma exactly where she said, watching my back from Lagos with her coughing generator and her sleeping daughter; Basti exactly where he said, doing the boots-on-the-ground work in the streets he'd been born in; Yuri paranoid and dark and honest, three burners deep and right as usual; Noa picking up the way she always picked up, owing me nothing and giving it anyway. The rope held. The people I'd led into the fire had not lit it. I felt that relief land like a blessing I hadn't earned, and I had about one second to feel it.

Because one thread ran the wrong way.

One voice had told me a place, and the world had quietly written down a different place. One mouth had said *I was with the station all night* on the night the marker moved, and the world remembered the station empty and the lights off and a car — *her* car, the one she'd given me a twin of, the one nobody watched — out in the dark on a road that led exactly one direction. One person had taken the shape of the dead drop from my own hand and carried it somewhere it should never have gone. One person had known the timing of the move on Clara, and one person had let the timing slip into the dark — into the channels, into the marketplace, into the part of the world where a leak like that gets you a body — and Clara had walked into a marketplace that was *waiting for her* — because someone had told it she was coming.

I sat there and I felt the floor go out from under me for the last time in this story, and it went slow, the way the worst ones always do, a developing photograph, grey water remembering it was a face.

And underneath the grief — and it was grief, I won't dress it up, it was the same animal sound a man makes at a graveside — there was a second thing, colder, that I hated myself for feeling so fast. A tactician's thing. The old breaking-life thing. Because the moment the system named the leak, it changed every plan I had. I had been days from going to Clara. I had a route, a window, a way in — and every piece of it had passed through Camille's hands, which meant every piece of it was already in the dark, already dumped into some channel, already known, already *waiting* for me the way the marketplace had waited for Clara. If I moved now, I'd be moving into a room they'd had time to fill. The rescue I'd built was poisoned at the root, and the person who'd poisoned it was the person I'd built it with.

There was a wrong way to use what I now knew. I felt that one too, quick and hot. The old reflex, the one from before. I could call Camille, right now, at this charming hour, and I could let her hear in my voice that I knew, and I could watch her world end the way she'd helped end mine. I could turn the dark on her. I could light up every secret she'd ever buried and watch them burn. I could make her afraid.

But that's the loud thing, and the loud thing saves no one. If she knew I knew, she'd run, or worse — she'd push the word into the dark, *he knows,* and they'd move Clara, or end Clara, before I could get within a mile of her. The only thing I had left, the only edge in the whole black world, was that the mole did not yet know she'd been found.

Which meant I had to do the hardest piece of acting of my life. I had to keep loving her to her face. I had to take her calls and use her flat and walk through her unlocked doors and let her light the way to Clara — knowing every light she lit was a trap — and I had to build, in secret, a *different* way in, one she'd never touch, one I'd rehearse in the dark with the only people I knew were clean, on lines she'd never see, until it was perfect, until it was the one true door in a building full of false ones. I had to use the traitor's own lit path as the bait, and walk a path she couldn't see.

I'd have to practise it until I couldn't get it wrong. Because a man only gets one walk through a door that's waiting for him, and the difference between a rescue and a funeral is the rehearsal nobody films.

That was tomorrow's problem. Tomorrow's, and the family's — the clean ones, the ones who'd held.

Tonight there was only the name.

I didn't want to look at the name.

I made myself look at the name.

It was the friend who'd offered me a chair instead of a cage. It was the locket and the daughter she never spoke to. It was twenty years of standing in the loneliest place in the world. It was the older sister I'd never had, the conscience with the gun, the woman who'd talked me down from becoming a monster more times than I could count — and who, it turned out, had simply been a better one all along, hiding in the one place I would never think to look, which was the place I loved.

Camille was the mole.

Camille Reyer, Station Marseille, the one person in this whole story I would have trusted with my life.
