# Chapter 23: The Mole

Clara walked into the marketplace on her own.

I spent the night with that. One of those nights where the screen is the only light. The city sleeps. Just me, the hum of the machines, the cold coffee in the mug. And paranoia burning like an ember that never goes out.

I still couldn't hold the thought without my hands shaking.

She hadn't been *taken.* She'd walked in. Chin up. Back straight. The way she moved toward every problem she'd already solved three moves out. Because somewhere inside the beast there was something only an insider could reach. She'd decided the price was her body. She'd paid it. Without asking anyone. Least of all me.

That was her. All of her. The woman I'd lost myself trying to find.

They didn't save her. She *infiltrated.*

And somewhere in the deep — that hidden web people imagine as a place but is really just silence, buried messaging apps, forums that open and close at ghost hours, markets that only light their stalls in the dead of night — she was still in there. Bleeding the truth out drop by drop. Betting her life on one certainty. That the man who loved her would be stubborn enough, brave enough, and *clean* enough to take what she was handing him and carry it to the end.

But she'd left me one instruction. One line. In the dead drop I'd been carrying for days like a stone in my chest.

*Don't trust Marseille.*

I'd read it a hundred times. Flinched past it a hundred times. The way you skip the one sentence in a letter you don't have the strength to believe.

Because Marseille wasn't an abstraction to me. Not a building, not a badge, not a desk. It was a person. A voice. A face. Someone I'd had dinner with, laughed with until the last drop of wine was gone, someone I'd shared the kind of secrets you only tell the people you've decided to die beside.

I'd been refusing to ask the question for days.

That night, I asked it.

---

I need to say what it is — this thing, this not-trusting. Because I'd done it before. A grey Brussels room, the screen too bright in the dark, paranoia rising like a tide. It had almost finished me.

You'd think it gets easier the second time.

It gets worse.

The first time, I asked the system whether someone was lying to me. It said yes. It didn't give a name. I told myself the absence was a mercy.

I lied to myself.

The no-name wasn't mercy. It was a stay of execution. I'd dragged that stay around for days, like a test result you're too scared to go pick up. As long as there's no name, everyone's still innocent. You can still sit at the table with all of them and pretend the rot is somewhere else. Outside. In someone else.

A name closes that door forever.

So I sat in the dark. A different dark. A Marseille dark, because I'd finally come south. You don't pull someone out of a fire from another country.

I looked at the phone on the desk. Next to the second one, the burner you never bring back online. And I thought seriously about not asking.

I'm a coward in exactly one direction. The people I love. I'll walk into any building. I'll hold the gaze of any monster. But ask me to look at a friend and say *prove it wasn't you* — and I become a child hiding under a coat, praying the storm belongs to someone else.

But Clara walked in on purpose.

If she could enter that marketplace with her chin up, knowing what they did to people inside, then I could enter the one room I'd been refusing to go into. The small, ordinary room where you find out the worst thing about someone you love.

She'd spent her courage on the children. The least I could spend of mine was a question.

I asked the system to tell me who was lying.

Not *whether.* I already knew whether. I'd known for days.

I asked it: **who.**

---

How it works doesn't matter. It never has. What belongs to me is what it felt like to wait for the answer. That, I can tell you.

It was the instant after you've let go of something fragile. Before it hits the floor. That stretched nothing where the world freezes, where you'd give anything to reach out and catch it. You can't. You were the one who let it go. All you can do is watch it find the tile.

In the room: only the breath of the fans. The smell of servers that never sleep. Warm ozone, hot dust. The smell of nights when you wait for the world to deliver its verdict.

The system doesn't agonise. I'd finally understood that. It doesn't hope. It doesn't flinch. It doesn't lie awake. It takes the warm braid of voices you've been leaning on. It lays each one next to the cold record the world keeps without meaning to. And it looks for the place where the story peels apart from the truth.

It does this without love. That's its terrible gift, and its cruelty. It loves no one. Which means it can see everyone clearly. The one thing love makes impossible.

I'd given it the braid. All the voices. Every one that had called me *brother* or *my love* in those last days. Every *I was here, I was there, I couldn't reach you, I missed your message.*

And one more face. Added last. With a hand that trembled on the keys. The way you sign something you're praying comes back blank.

The face from Marseille.

The liaison. The friend on the inside. The one human being in the entire machinery of the law I had ever let all the way in.

I need to say who that was before the answer. Otherwise the answer is just a plot twist. It wasn't a plot twist. It was an amputation.

---

Here's who she was.

I'll call her by the name I used for her. Never her rank. Never her title. Camille. Camille Reyer, liaison officer at the Marseille station.

The bridge between the world of badges and the world of ghosts. Between those who carry ID and those who live in the cracks — under handles, behind aliases never written the same way twice. The only person who'd ever kept one foot in each world without despising the other. The cops thought she was too close to the underworld. The underworld thought she was too close to the cops. Both were right. That double suspicion was exactly what made her the best. It was why I'd loved her from the first day. Because it was the loneliest place in the world to stand. And she'd been standing there for twenty years without once asking anyone to feel sorry about it.

I met her four years ago. Before Clara. Before all of this.

I was still mostly the man who tested every limit. The one who found the door others thought locked. Who forced the world's locks and sold in the dark what he took there. For money. On principle. For the thrill.

She was the cop who could have put me away. She chose to put me to work.

She looked across an interrogation table at a younger, wilder version of me. A man sweating arrogance to cover the fear. Instead of a cell, she offered me a chair.

"You're going to keep doing what you do." She was lighting a cigarette she had no right to light in there. "I can't stop you. Nobody stops a man who isn't afraid of losing what he has, because you've already decided you have nothing. So here's what I'm going to do. I'm going to aim you."

And she aimed me. At men worse than me. For four years.

Somewhere in there, she stopped being my handler and became my friend. And after that, something I don't have a word for. The older sister I never had. The conscience that came with a gun and a sense of humour darker than mine.

She cried at no one's funeral. She laughed at everyone's. She kept a photo of a daughter she never spoke to in a locket she never took off.

She'd talked me back from the dirty path more times than Ifeoma had. Not through prayer. Through the flat arithmetic of a woman who'd watched a hundred vigilantes become the thing they hunted.

"Burn them down and you save no one and you lose yourself. And then I'm the one who has to hunt you. I'm too old to hunt a man I like."

When Clara disappeared, Camille was my first call outside my own circle. When I needed the reach of the law without the chains of the law, Camille built the bridge. When I came south, Camille had everything ready. An apartment. A clean phone — new, never connected anywhere, never seen by any network. A car. A route through the city that the bad people didn't watch.

Every door I walked through in Marseille, she'd unlocked. Every step toward Clara, she'd lit.

She was the one person in this whole story I had stopped checking.

You check everyone in the breaking life. The man who watches the street. The woman who carries the bag. The voice that says *go.* That's the first law of the trade, learned before you even learn to keep quiet. Paranoia isn't a sickness. It's hygiene.

But there's always one. Always one person you decide, somewhere along the way, you'd rather die than suspect. Because suspecting them would cost more than your own death.

Camille was mine.

I'd told her things I'd never told Marchetti. I'd told her the shape of the dead drop. I'd told her — God forgive me — the timing of the move on Clara.

So when I added her face to the braid, last, with a shaking hand, I was already begging.

*Come back blank,* I told the dark. *Come back blank. Anyone. A stranger. The Surgeon wearing a friend's coat. Marchetti even — the cold hand of a state I never trusted anyway. But not her. Anyone but her.*

---

The system doesn't take requests.

It took the braid. It did its solitary work, the thing it does better than any human alive. It laid the story and the truth side by side. Without love, without gentleness, without trembling. It found the place where they came apart.

It didn't take long. It never takes long. That's the cruelty I keep coming back to. The worst news of a life arrives without ceremony. Without a drumroll. Without a single moment to brace.

One second I'm a man praying at a screen. Face blue in the cold light. The world drowned in dark behind me.

The next second the screen answered. Flatly. Completely. The way a coroner answers. And the answer sat down in the room with me. For the rest of my days.

Most of the braid held. I need to say that. Because it mattered. Because it meant my family was clean.

Ifeoma exactly where she'd said — watching over my infrastructure from Lagos, her generator coughing, her daughter asleep. Basti exactly where he'd said — on the ground, in the streets where he was born. Yuri paranoid, unreachable, honest — a guy in Kyiv you can never pin down. Noa picking up the way she always picks up, owing me nothing, giving it anyway.

The rope held. The people I'd led into the fire hadn't lit it. That relief landed on me like a blessing I hadn't earned.

I had one second to feel it.

Because one thread ran the wrong way.

One voice had told me a location. The world had quietly recorded a different one. One mouth had said *I was at the station all night* on the night the safe house moved. And the world remembered the station empty. Lights off. And a car. *Her* car. The one she'd given me a copy of the key to. The one nobody watched. Out in the dark, on a road that only went one direction.

One person had taken the shape of the dead drop from my own hands and carried it somewhere it should never have gone. One person had known the timing of the move on Clara. One person had let that timing bleed into the dark.

And Clara had walked into a marketplace that was *waiting for her.* Because someone had told it she was coming.

I sat there. The floor left from under me. Slowly, the way it always goes on the worst ones. Like a photograph developing. Revealing what you didn't want to see.

Underneath the grief — because it was grief, I won't dress it up, the same animal sound you make at the edge of a grave — there was something else. Colder. That I hated myself for feeling so fast.

A tactician's thought.

Because the instant the system named the leak, it changed every plan I had. I was hours away from going for Clara. I had a route, a window, a way in. And every piece had passed through Camille's hands. Which meant every piece was already in the dark. Already known. Already *waiting for me,* the way the marketplace had waited for Clara.

If I moved now, I'd be walking into a room they'd had time to fill. The rescue I'd built was poisoned at the root. And the person who'd poisoned it was the person I'd built it with.

There was a wrong way to use what I knew. I felt that too. Hot and quick. The old reflex. The temptation from the first time I ever cracked anything open. I could call Camille. Right now. At this dead hour before dawn. Let her hear in my voice that I knew. Watch her world collapse the way she'd helped collapse mine. Turn the dark back on her. Cut her power. Short her circuits. Make her afraid.

But that's the loud move. And the loud move saves no one.

If she knew I knew, she'd run. Or worse. She'd tell *them* that I knew. They'd move Clara. Or they'd be done with Clara. Before I got within a mile.

The only thing I had left — the only edge in the whole black world — was that the mole didn't yet know she'd been found.

Which meant the hardest performance of my life.

Keep loving her to her face. Take her calls. Use her apartment. Walk through her unlocked doors. Let her light the way to Clara — knowing every light she lit was a trap. And build, in secret, a *different* way in. One she'd never touch. Rehearsed in the dark with the only people I knew were clean. Until it was perfect. The one real door in a building full of false ones.

Use the traitor's lit path as bait. And walk a road she couldn't see.

I'd have to rehearse it until I couldn't get it wrong. Because you only walk through a door that's holding its breath for you once. And the difference between a rescue and a funeral is the rehearsal nobody films.

That was tomorrow's problem. Tomorrow's, and the family's. The ones who'd held.

Tonight there was only the name.

I didn't want to look at it.

I made myself look.

It was the friend who'd offered me a chair instead of a cage. The locket and the daughter she never spoke to. Twenty years of standing in the loneliest place in the world. The older sister I never had. The conscience with the gun. The woman who'd talked me back from becoming a monster more times than I could count.

Who had, it turned out, simply been a better monster all along. Hidden in the one place I would never have thought to look.

The place I loved.

Camille was the mole.

Camille Reyer, Marseille station. The one person in this whole story I would have trusted with my life.

And she had a phone in her pocket right now. And she was going to use it.
