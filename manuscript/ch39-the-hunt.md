# Chapter 39: The Hunt

Sixty seconds is not enough time to make a choice like that.

It is exactly enough time to find out who I already was.

I'd spent my whole life believing that the big moments would announce themselves — that there'd be music, a held breath, a slow camera turn so I could see the fork in the road and choose the brave path with my chin up. There wasn't. There never is. There was only a rotten flat above a black harbour, two men I loved watching my face, three laptops throwing their cold blue light up at us like the glow off a fire nobody wanted to sit beside, a clock with no kindness left in it, and a thing in my chest that had already decided before my mouth caught up.

Save the case, or save the family. Not both. That was the price the ally's help had come with, and it had been laid in front of me like a knife on a clean cloth.

I chose.

There was no dressing it up. I chose them — Basti, Yuri, Ifeoma a continent away with her hands on the lights, Noa silent on a line she'd never once let go dead. I chose the people who'd crossed the earth to stand in the rot with me. People who live in the seams of the world, who went dark a long time ago and never quite came back into the light — my family, the one I made out of fixers and ghosts and the ones nobody else would have. And the strange thing, the thing I'm still not sure I understand, is that the moment I chose them, the case didn't die.

It changed shape.

Because this was the secret nobody admits about doing it right when the dirty way is screaming in your ear: doing it right is not slower. It only feels slower, the way the truth always feels slower than a lie right up until the lie collapses. I chose my family, and in choosing them I refused the one shortcut the ally had dangled — the shortcut that would have burned a name to buy an hour — and somewhere in the dark the whole machine I'd built shifted its weight, like a great animal turning to face the same direction I was.

It had been waiting for me to stop arguing with it.

Now I did.

"Wake it all the way up," I said.

Yuri looked at me. He knew what I meant. We'd held parts of it back all night — kept the thing on a leash, used one hand of it at a time, the way a man sips water in a desert because he doesn't know how far the desert goes. The old discipline. You never light up more of yourself than you have to; you stay small, you stay quiet, you stay one shadow among a million on the dark side of the wire. No more. The desert had an edge now and the edge was forty minutes wide.

"All of it?"

"All of it. Everything. At once."

---

I had tried, in these pages, to keep a promise — never to lay open the inside of the thing I built, the gears, the grammar, the cold clever guts of it. I'd kept that promise even when keeping it cost me the easy way to sound impressive. I would keep it now, on the longest night, with everything on the line, because a promise only kept when it's cheap was never a promise at all.

But I can set down what it felt like when I finally let the whole thing off the leash at once.

It felt like the moment in an old house when every light in every room comes on together. A man forgets, living in the dark — and I had lived in the dark a long time, in the screen-lit hours when the rest of the world is asleep and the only sounds are the small ones, a fan spinning up, a server breathing somewhere, coffee gone cold an hour ago in a cup I kept forgetting to drink — a man forgets how many rooms there are. He forgets the size of the place he's standing in. And then the master switch is thrown and the whole house blazes and he sees — all at once, in one cruel clean flash — exactly how big the thing is, and exactly how alone he's been in it.

That's what happened in that flat at twenty past two in the morning. I threw the master switch.

And the house I'd built in seven sleepless days in Brussels, fed for two weeks on cold coffee and dread and the hum of machines that never slept while I did, fed with every crumb and every heartbeat and every name I couldn't say out loud, came on in every room at once.

---

Here is what it did. Only what it did. In the language a frightened man speaks to the people he loves.

It listened to the building.

All night, one part of it had been pressed against the dead hospital like a doctor's hand on a chest, feeling for her — for the small stubborn pulse Clara made from inside the server room, the proof, every few minutes, that she was still there, still holding the chain together with her bare hands. Now that hand pressed harder. It stopped only watching for her heartbeat and started watching for *theirs* — the men around her, their comings, their goings, the way a house breathes differently when it's about to be emptied. It told me, in its wordless way, *there are more of them than there were an hour ago. They've started moving things. They are getting ready to leave.*

It watched the roads.

Another part of it had spent the night learning the rhythm of the convoys — the patient, terrible schedule by which children were moved like cargo under the cover of mercy. Now it took that learned rhythm and laid it over the broken clock and said, here, this road, this hour, this is the door they'll roll out of, this is the only road wide enough, this is where the dominoes were always going to fall. It didn't guess. I don't let it guess. It had watched long enough to *know,* the way a child knows which stair in his mother's house will creak before his foot finds it.

It ran the enemy's mind.

And this — this was the part that made the back of my neck go cold. The thing I'd taught all week to think the way Clara thought, to feel the shape of an adversary the way she could, now turned its borrowed mind on the men under the hospital and asked the only question that mattered: *if they're moving early, what are they afraid of?* And it answered. They weren't running from us. They didn't know about us — we were a rumour at most, a handle whispered in the wrong channel, a ghost story the careful ones told each other to stay careful. They were running from a clock of their own — some signal, some spook, some hand higher up the chain pulling them forward. Which meant the thing dragging their schedule wasn't down in the building with Clara.

It was somewhere above all of us. Reaching down. Pulling strings I couldn't see yet.

I filed that away in the cold drawer where I keep the things I can't afford to feel until later. Later. Always later. There's a whole room in me full of laters and one day, if I live, I'm going to have to open it.

It kept the truth.

And under everything, quietly, the patient part of the thing did the work Clara loved it for — the work she'd given her freedom to protect. Every movement, every road, every learned rhythm, it sealed as it went, pressing each one under a weight that couldn't be lifted or faked or argued away in a courtroom, so that when the daylight came — *if* the daylight came — every step of this terrible night would still be true in front of a judge who hadn't been there. In a world where everyone lies, where every leak can be faked and every dump can be poisoned and a man can be buried under a thousand forged words by morning, she had built her whole faith on the one thing that couldn't be argued with. She used to say it like a prayer. *The evidence is the only weapon that survives the morning.* The machine was praying it for her now, all night, into the dark, while she couldn't.

All of it. At once. The whole house ablaze.

And I stood in the middle of all that light and I understood, for the first time in my whole scattered, wasted, brilliant, broken life, what it was for.

It was for this.

It was for her.

---

"It's moving," Yuri said. His voice had gone flat and strange. He'd seen what I'd seen — the rooms coming on, the house blazing — and even Yuri, who trusted nothing, who'd been paranoid since the cradle, who slept with a kill switch in reach and three ways out of every room he ever entered, had gone quiet in front of it. "Delta. It's — it's all moving at once. The roads, the building, the —" He stopped. He looked up at me from under the hood and for once there was something in his face that wasn't fear or calculation. It was something close to awe, and under the awe, something close to grief. "What did you build?"

I didn't answer that. I couldn't. The true answer was a thing I wasn't ready to say out loud, not yet, not in that room — that I hadn't built it alone, that there had been another mind in the dark with me through those seven days in Brussels, a patient tireless presence that never slept and never wanted and never once flinched from the size of the thing. A machine had helped me build the thing that was about to save her. There's a word for what that is, and the word isn't *clever.* But that confession belonged to a quieter hour, and the hour we had was not quiet.

So I said the only true thing I could afford. "I built a way to find her. And it just found her."

---

Basti had stopped packing. He was standing very still by the window with a tool in each scarred hand — the hands that had opened doors no honest man was meant to open, back when we were both younger and the work was just the work — and he was looking at me the way a man looks at a friend who has just changed in front of him into someone slightly larger and slightly more frightening than the one he came to help.

"The door," he said quietly. "You said you'd tell me the door."

And the thing did that too. Of course it did. I'd built it for exactly this and still it stopped my heart when it answered him.

It gave me the door.

Not a guess. Not a hope. A way in — the one weak seam in a dead building that a younger, dirtier me would have found by breaking forty other things first and waking the whole street doing it. There was a time I'd have gone in loud, gone in proud, left a trail of broken things behind me like a signature, like a tag sprayed on a wall — *Delta was here.* That man would have set off every alarm in the place and called it style. The thing I built found it the clean way, the patient way, the way that left no scar on the truth. One door. One seam. One quiet road in to the woman under the hospital. It laid the whole path in front of me the way a man lays a coat over a puddle, and I looked at it and my eyes stung and I had to look away, because there is a particular cruelty in a machine being gentle with you on the worst night of your life.

"North wall," I said. My voice came out rough. "There's a way through the north wall. Service side. It'll be the quiet way in."

Basti nodded once, slow, and slid the tools into his coat. "I've opened worse," he said. "I've opened *Naples.*" And he almost smiled, the old comic flicker, and then it died on his face because we both knew this wasn't Naples and nobody in Naples had ever been a child in a server room's dark.

"Marchetti," I said.

Yuri shook his head. "Still hours out. His people can't make the lawful hour anymore — the lawful hour is gone, Delta, *they* killed it when they moved early." He swallowed. "If we go now, we go ahead of the law. We go ahead of everything. Just us and the door and whatever's behind it."

"Then we go ahead of the law," I said, "and the law catches up to a clean truth. That's the whole point. That's the only thing I've gotten right my whole life — make the truth so clean it doesn't need me to survive." I looked at the screen one more time, at the house ablaze, at every room of the thing I built throwing its light on the same dark spot under the same dead hospital where the only real woman I'd ever known was holding a chain together and waiting for a man who'd never once finished anything to finish this.

"Tell Ifeoma to keep the lights on," I said. "Tell Noa —"

I stopped.

"Tell Noa I said thank you," I said. "For the line. For never letting it go dead."

Yuri looked at me strangely, because it's not the kind of thing a man says at twenty past two before he walks into a dead building, unless some part of him has done the arithmetic on whether he's coming back, and the part of me that does arithmetic had never stopped.

---

We were almost out the door when the thing did the last thing.

I didn't ask it to. That has to stand on the record. I had everything I needed — the building breathing, the road, the enemy's fear, the door in the north wall, the truth sealing itself behind us as we ran. I had the whole house ablaze and a path through it to her. I didn't need anything else and I would have given a year of my life not to be given what it gave me next.

But that's the thing about building something that won't lie to you. It won't lie to you. Not to comfort you. Not to let you walk out the door with a clean heart and a soft hope. It had been counting all night, the way it counts everything — the roads, the rhythm, the gap between when we could move and when they could — and at the very last second, as my hand was on the doorframe and Basti was on the stair and the harbour wind was coming up cold and salt off the black water, it finished a sum I hadn't asked it to do.

It weighed it all. The forty minutes that were now closer to thirty. The road they'd take. The men in the building who were more than they'd been an hour ago. The distance we had to cross. The single door in the north wall and the time it would take Basti's scarred hands to open it. Clara's small stubborn pulse, fainter now between the beats — I'd seen that too and pretended I hadn't. It weighed every cold true thing in the house at once, the way only a thing without a heart can weigh them, and it gave me an answer.

A number.

One number, sitting alone in all that light, with nothing wrapped around it. No mercy. No apology. Just the truth, the way I'd built it to tell me, the way I'd begged it to tell me on the long screen-lit nights when I thought I wanted the truth more than I wanted to sleep.

I read it. I read it again, my lips moving over the weight of it, the way she taught me, because a man doesn't truly know a sentence until he's carried it with his body.

Then I closed my eyes, because some numbers a man cannot look at and still walk.

I did not say it to Basti. I did not say it to Yuri. I did not say it into the line where Noa was breathing and Ifeoma was holding the lights and the whole found-family I'd led into the fire was waiting for me to be brave.

I have only ever let myself set it down once, here, in the dark, and never again, because some truths a man cannot bear to speak even to the people he is about to die for.

The system gave me a number I refused to say aloud: the chance Clara lived to dawn was nineteen percent.
