# Chapter 39: The Hunt

Sixty seconds is not enough time to make a choice like that.

It is exactly enough time to discover who you already are.

My whole life I believed the big moments would announce themselves. Some music. A held breath. A slow camera move to show me the crossroads, time to pick the brave path with my chin up.

There was nothing. There never is.

Just a rotten flat above a black harbour. A screen washing us in blue light. A row of cold coffee cups lined up like spent casings. The warm smell of machines pushed hard — hot dust and metal. For people like me, that is the smell of home.

Two men I loved watching my face. A clock with no mercy left in it. And something in me that had already chosen. Before I opened my mouth.

Save the investigation, or save the family. Not both. That was the price our ally's help had finally come to demand. Laid in front of me like a knife on a clean cloth.

I chose.

I won't dress it up. I chose them. Basti. Yuri. Ifeoma a continent away, hands on the lights, deep in the Lagos night. Noa, silent on an open line she had never once let die. I chose the people who had crossed the earth to rot here with me.

My accidental family. People who live in the margins. Who erased their names from the registries. Who work while the world sleeps. Who recognise each other by a fake handle dropped in the right channel, and who owe each other debts no contract will ever name.

And the strangest thing — the thing I still don't fully understand — is that the moment I chose them, the investigation didn't die.

It changed shape.

Nobody ever says this about doing things right when the dirty way is screaming in your ear. Doing it right is not slower. It only looks slower. The way the truth always looks slower than a lie — right up until the lie collapses.

I chose my family. And in choosing them, I refused the one shortcut our ally had dangled in the light. The one that would have burned a name to buy an hour.

And in the dark, the machine I had built turned. Toward the same direction as me.

It had been waiting for me to stop arguing.

So I did.

"Wake it all the way up," I said.

Yuri looked at me. He knew. We had kept parts in reserve all night. Held the thing on a leash. Used one hand of it at a time, finger on the kill switch, the way you ration water in a desert when you don't know how far it goes.

No more. The desert had an edge now. And that edge was thirty-one minutes wide.

"Everything?"

"Everything. All at once."

---

I had kept a promise all night. Not to look inside the thing I built. The gears, the cold guts of it. And not to expose it to anyone.

I kept it even when it cost me the easy way to seem impressive. It is a discipline of the trade, older than me. You never show the mechanism. Never the underside of the cards. What you explain, you give away. What you give away, you lose.

You learn that suspicion early. In those hidden forums where every word is read twice, copied, archived by some stranger. I kept it through the longest night, with everything at stake. Because a promise you only keep when it costs nothing was never a promise.

But I can say what it felt like when I finally let go of all of it at once.

It was like the moment in an old house when every light in every room comes on together. You forget, living in the dark, how many rooms there are. Then everything blazes at once. And you see — sharp, cruel — the full size of the thing. And how alone you were inside it.

That is what happened in that flat, at twenty past two in the morning, under the screen that was the only star in the room.

I had built it in seven sleepless days. In Brussels, by the light of that screen, on cold coffee. Then fed it day after day, every crumb, every name I couldn't say out loud. And there, all at once, every room came on.

---

What it did, I can say. Only what it did. In the language a frightened man speaks to the people he loves.

It listened to the building.

All night, one part of it had been pressed against the dead hospital like a doctor's hand on a chest. Searching for the small stubborn pulse Clara made from inside the server room. Proof, every few minutes, that she was still there. That she was still holding the chain with bare hands.

Now that hand pressed harder. It stopped watching for Clara's heartbeat and started watching for *theirs*. The men around her. Their comings and goings. The way a building breathes differently when it is about to be emptied.

It told me, without words: *There are more of them than an hour ago. They are moving things. They are getting ready to leave.*

It watched the roads.

Another part of it had spent the night learning the rhythm of the convoys. The patient, terrible schedule by which children were moved like cargo, under the cover of mercy. Now it laid that rhythm over the broken clock. And it said: here. This road. This hour. This door. The only one wide enough.

It didn't guess. I don't let it guess. Guessing is for amateurs. We watch until we know. It had watched long enough to *know.*

It ran the enemy's mind.

And that — that was the part that made the back of my neck go cold. The thing I had spent a week training to think like Clara was now turning that borrowed mind on the men beneath the hospital. And asking the only question that mattered. *If they are leaving early, what are they afraid of?*

It answered. It wasn't us they were running from. They didn't know we existed. To them, we weren't even a rumour dropped in a forum at three in the morning. Not a fake name whispered in the wrong channel. They were running from a clock of their own. A signal. A hand higher up the chain pulling them forward.

Which meant that whatever was pushing their schedule wasn't down below, in the building, with Clara.

It was above all of us. Leaning down. Pulling strings I couldn't see yet.

I filed that in the cold drawer where I keep the things I can't afford to feel right now. Later. Always later. There is a whole room in me full of laters. One day, if I survive, I will have to open it.

It kept the truth.

And beneath all of it, in silence, the patient part of the machine did the work for which Clara had given her freedom. Every movement, every road, every learned rhythm, it sealed as it went. Under a weight that cannot be lifted or faked or contested in a courtroom. So that when the day came — *if* it came — every step of this terrible night would still be true before a judge who wasn't there.

It is the opposite of everything I had done before. The young idiot I was erased his tracks. Burned the records. Disappeared without a shadow. The machine engraved. It kept. *The evidence is the only weapon that survives the morning.*

Everything. All at once.

And I stood in the middle of all that light. And I understood, for the first time in my whole wasted brilliant life, what it was for.

It was for this.

It was for her.

---

"It's moving," Yuri said. His voice had gone flat. Strange. He had seen what I had seen.

And even Yuri, who trusted nothing, who had grown up in Kyiv surrounded by microphones and men who listened to others for a living, who took it as given that every line leaked and the oldest friend might be hiding a traitor — even he had gone silent in front of it.

"Delta. It's moving everywhere at once. The roads, the building, the —" He stopped. He looked up at me from under his hood, his face cut by the light of the screen. And for once, something other than fear or calculation. Something close to wonder. And beneath the wonder, something close to grief. "What did you build?"

I didn't answer. I couldn't.

The true answer wasn't ready for that room. That I hadn't built it alone. That another mind had been with me in the dark, through all seven days in Brussels. A patient, tireless presence. One that never slept. Never drank cold coffee. Never wanted anything. And not once flinched from the scale of the thing.

A machine had helped me build the thing that was about to save her. There is a word for that, and the word is not *clever.* But that admission belonged to a quieter hour. And the hour we had was not quiet.

So I said the only true thing I could afford. "I built a way to find her. And it just found her."

---

Basti had stopped packing. He stood very still by the window, a tool in each scarred hand, looking at me the way you look at a friend who has just become, right in front of you, slightly larger and slightly more frightening than the man you came to help.

"The door," he said quietly. "You said you'd tell me the door."

And the machine did that too. Of course it did. I had built it for exactly this. And it still stopped my heart when it answered.

It gave me the door.

Not a guess. Not a hope. A way in. The one weak seam in a dead building. The younger, dirtier me would have found it by breaking forty other things first. Forcing everything in his path. Waking the whole street.

The machine found it the clean way. The patient way. The way that leaves no scar on the truth. One door. One seam. One quiet road to the woman beneath the hospital.

It unrolled the whole path in front of me the way you spread a coat over a puddle. I looked at it. My eyes burned. I had to look away. There is a particular cruelty in a machine being gentle with you on the worst night of your life.

"North wall," I said. My voice came out rough. "There's a way through the north wall. Service side. That's the quiet route."

Basti nodded once, slow, and slid the tools into his coat. "I've opened worse," he said. "I've opened *Naples.*" He almost smiled, the old sardonic flicker at the corner of his mouth. Then it died. Because we both knew this wasn't Naples. And nobody in Naples had ever been a child in the dark of a server room.

"Marchetti," I said.

Yuri shook his head. "Still hours out. His people can't make the lawful hour anymore. The lawful hour is dead, Delta. *They* killed it when they moved early." He swallowed. "If we go now, we go ahead of the law. Ahead of everything. Just us and the door and whatever is behind it."

"Then we go ahead of the law," I said. "And the law catches up to a clean truth. That is the whole point. That is the only thing I have ever gotten right in my life — make the truth so clean it doesn't need me to survive."

I looked at the screen one last time. Everything converging on the same dark point, beneath the same dead hospital. Where the only real woman I had ever known was holding a chain in her hands. And waiting for a man who had never finished anything, not once, to finish this.

"Tell Ifeoma to keep the lights on," I said. "Tell Noa —"

I stopped.

"Tell Noa thank you. For the line. For never letting it die."

Yuri looked at me strangely. Because that is not the kind of thing you say at twenty past two before walking into a dead building. Unless some part of you has already done the arithmetic on whether you're coming back. And the part of me that does arithmetic had never stopped running.

---

We were almost out the door when the machine did the last thing.

I had not asked it to. I want that on the record. I had everything I needed. The building breathing. The road. The enemy's fear. The door in the north wall. The truth sealing itself behind us as we ran. I had a path to her.

I didn't need anything else. And I would have given a year of my life not to receive what it gave me next.

But that is the problem with building something that won't lie to you. It won't lie to you. Not to comfort you. Not to let you walk out the door with a clean heart and a soft hope.

It had been counting all night. The way it counts everything. The roads. The rhythm. The gap between when we could move and when they could. And at the very last second, with my hand on the doorframe and Basti already on the stairs and the harbour wind coming up cold and salt from the black water below — it finished a calculation I had never asked it to make.

It weighed everything. The thirty-one minutes that were now twenty-nine. The road they would take. The men in the building, more of them than an hour ago. The distance to cross. The one door in the north wall, and how long it would take Basti's scarred hands to open it. Clara's small stubborn pulse, fainter now between beats — I had seen that too. I had pretended I hadn't.

It weighed every cold true thing at once. The way only a thing without a heart can weigh them. And it gave me an answer.

A number.

One number, alone in all that screen light. Nothing around it. No mercy. No apology. Just the truth, the way I had built it to tell me. The way I had begged it to tell me, on the long screen-lit nights when I thought I wanted the truth more than I wanted sleep.

I read it. I read it again, my lips moving over the weight of it, the way she had taught me. Because you don't truly know a sentence until you've carried it with your body.

Then I closed my eyes. Because there are numbers you cannot look at and still walk.

I did not say it to Basti. Not to Yuri. Not into the line where Noa was breathing and Ifeoma was holding the lights and the whole family I had made — and led into the fire — was waiting for me to be brave.

I said it to myself. Once. And never again. I had carried it so far into the dark. It needed to be named, at least in the silence inside me. Even when I could not bear to say it aloud.

The system gave me a number I refused to speak: the chance that Clara lived to dawn was nineteen percent.
