# Chapter 35: Go Now

There is a kind of patience that is really just fear wearing a uniform.

I'd been living inside it for hours. We all had. The flat above the harbour had gone quiet in the way a room goes quiet when too many people are holding the same breath — Basti at the window with his shoulder against the frame and a cigarette he kept forgetting to light, Yuri hunched in the corner under his hood like a man praying to a god he'd stopped believing in, and me in the middle of it, in the blue dark, with the whole night narrowing down to a single line on a screen and a plan that needed one more thing before it could be born.

Time.

That was the deal we'd made with ourselves. We go at dawn. That was the word — *dawn* — and we'd said it so many times across the night that it had stopped being an hour and become a promise, a small warm light hung at the end of a tunnel that smelled of harbour rot and cold coffee. Dawn. The light would be soft. The streets would be empty. Marchetti's people would be in position. Clara would still be breathing in her server room under the dead hospital, holding the chain together with her own two hands the way she held everything — completely, brutally, with love. And at dawn, with the city half-asleep, we would go in and bring her home.

Eight hours. That's what stood between us and dawn when I last let myself believe in it. Eight hours to rest the body, prime the family, walk the plan one more time. Eight hours of the worst kind of patience, the kind that is just fear in a uniform, standing at attention, waiting for the order that never quite comes.

Eight hours felt like an ocean.

I should have known better than to trust an ocean.

---

You want to understand what the night had become, you have to understand what the system had become.

For two weeks I'd fed it everything. The crumbs from the dead drop. The pulse in the dark that was her heart. The money I followed through the burning house. The names — all twelve of them, even the one I couldn't say out loud. The building. The relay points. The convoys that carried children the way a smile carries a lie. I'd poured the whole shape of the beast into it, drop by bleeding drop, and somewhere in the dark of that machine it had been doing the one thing I built it to do.

It had been listening.

Not the way a person listens, with their want and their grief getting in the way. It listened the way the tide listens to the moon — patient, total, indifferent to whether I could bear the answer. All week it had sat in the corner of every room I was in, turning the world over and over, watching for the one thing that would change everything. And I'd come, over those two weeks, to trust it the way you trust a dog that growls at a door before you've heard the footsteps. It knew things before I did. It felt the floor of the world shift while I was still standing flat.

I won't tell you how it works. You know the promise. I've kept it this long and I'm not breaking it now, on the worst night of my life, just to make myself sound clever. So let me tell you what it *did,* in the only language that matters — the language a frightened man speaks at three in the morning when the thing he loves is underground.

It had been counting.

Somewhere in all that listening, it had found a rhythm in the beast. A heartbeat under the heartbeat. The convoys moved on a schedule, and the money moved with them, and the silences between the movements had a shape, and the system had learned the shape the way I'd once learned the shape of Clara's breathing in the dark — so well I could tell, with my eyes closed, whether she was asleep or only pretending. All night it had been holding that rhythm against its ear, watching for the next beat, telling me, in its wordless way, *you have time, you have time, the next thing happens at dawn, you have until dawn.*

And then, at four minutes past two in the morning, the rhythm broke.

---

I felt it before I saw it.

That sounds like a lie, the kind of thing a man says afterward to make himself sound like he was ever in control. But it's true, and I've stopped being able to afford lies, even the flattering ones. I was standing at the table with a cold cup in my hand, half-listening to Basti tell Yuri some story about a door in Naples that had to be opened with a violin, and the room was warm and human and almost — *almost* — bearable, and then something went cold in the middle of my chest, the way the air goes cold and wrong a half-second before a glass falls off a shelf, before you've heard it, before your eyes have even found it, when your body simply knows the world is about to break.

I put the cup down. I didn't decide to. My hand just did it.

Then I looked.

I'm not going to describe what was on the screen, because describing it would mean explaining it, and I don't explain. But I'll tell you what it told me, in plain words, the way it would tell me if it could speak, if I'd built it with a mouth instead of an eye.

It told me the schedule had moved.

Not the rescue. *Theirs.* The beast's. The thing under the building, the thing with the children in it, the thing whose heartbeat I'd been counting on to stay slow and steady until the light came — it had just woken up. Somewhere in the dark machinery of PHANTOM MERCY a switch had been thrown, and the patient, terrible rhythm I'd trusted all night had lurched forward, the way a clock lurches when a hand reaches in and drags it.

The convoy that was supposed to roll at dawn was going to roll before dawn.

And the children — the forty-seven children I had carried in my chest for two weeks like stones, the children Clara had let herself be taken to protect — were going to be on it.

"Delta." Yuri had stopped under his hood. He was watching my face the way you watch the face of a man at a deathbed, reading the news off him before anyone says it. "What."

I didn't answer. I couldn't yet. I was still doing the thing the system had just done in a heartbeat and that my human, grieving, exhausted mind needed three full seconds to do behind it — I was subtracting.

---

Here is the cruelty of it, and I want you to feel it the way I felt it, standing in that blue room with the harbour breathing through the cracked glass.

When something moves up in this world — when an operation pulls forward, when the timetable lurches early — it doesn't just steal your hours. It rearranges *everything that depends on those hours.* The rescue Marchetti had built was a row of dominoes laid down with surgeon's hands across a whole night. His people, in position by dawn. The lawful door, opened at the lawful hour. The arrests, clean, prosecutable, with the evidence chain that Clara had spent her freedom holding together so that no clever lawyer could ever say it was broken. Every piece of it had been balanced against that one word.

Dawn.

Take dawn away and the whole row of dominoes doesn't just shorten. It *falls.*

Because here's the thing nobody tells you about a ticking clock — the clock isn't the enemy. The clock is just a clock. The enemy is the gap between when *you* can move and when *they* can move. All night that gap had been a luxury, hours wide, room to breathe in. And the system, in its cold and total way, was now closing that gap in front of my eyes, line by line, telling me not how long until dawn but how long until *they* were gone — how long until the convoy doors closed and the building emptied and Clara, who would have done everything, risked everything, *held the chain to the end*, found herself alone in a server room under a hospital that no longer had any reason to keep her alive.

Eight hours until dawn.

That number was dead now. It had been a comfort and it was a corpse and I let it go.

The new number was the only one that mattered, and the system gave it to me the way it gave me everything — without mercy, without apology, the truth and nothing wrapped around it. I read it. I read it again, the way Clara taught me, my lips moving over the weight of it, because she said you don't truly know a sentence until you've carried it with your body.

Then I said it out loud, into the room, into the faces of the two men I loved who had crossed half the earth to stand in this rotten flat with me, and saying it out loud was the first true thing I'd said in hours.

"We can't wait for dawn."

---

The room did not explode. That's the part the films get wrong. When the floor finally drops, people don't shout. They go very still and very gentle, like people who have just understood that the next few minutes will be the ones they're judged by for the rest of their lives.

Basti lit the cigarette at last. One match, cupped in both scarred hands, and the little flame lit his face from below, and he was not the comic anymore, the man who opened doors with violins. He was just a man from Marseille who had chosen the right side too late in life to make it look easy. "Then we don't wait," he said, simple as a key turning. "I've been ready since I was twelve. Tell me the door."

Yuri was already moving, already paranoid, already — I would bet my life on it, and in about an hour I more or less would — already right. "Marchetti," he said. "Marchetti's people aren't in position. They're hours out. If we go now we go *without* him." He looked at me from under the hood, and there was no fear in it, only the old terrible question. "Without the law. Without the lawful door. Just us and a building full of them."

And there it was. The thing that had been waiting under the whole night like a second heartbeat, the choice I'd been making over and over my whole life and getting wrong every single time.

The dirty way was right there. I could feel it, warm and familiar as an old coat. Go now, go hard, go the way I used to go when I was a younger and emptier man — break the door, break the rules, break whatever stood between me and the room with her in it, and let the evidence and the lawyers and the lawful hour go hang. She was *under that building.* Every cell I had was screaming to take the fast road, the broken road, the road that had ruined every good thing I ever touched because I always, always forgot the target in the heat of the want.

I'd promised her I wouldn't. Not in words. In something deeper than words. I'd built an entire government out of grief precisely so I would never again be the man who burned the truth down to save the moment. The whole point of the thing I made — the whole soul of it, the thing that made it *hers* — was that it brought the truth out clean enough to survive daylight. To take the dirty road now would be to save her body and lose the reason I deserved to.

But there was a clock now, and the clock did not care about my soul.

I looked at the new number. I looked at the two of them. And somewhere under the floor of the city, somewhere in a dead hospital, a woman I loved was holding a chain together with her bare hands, not knowing — *not knowing* — that the night had just lurched out from under all of us.

"Get Marchetti on the line," I said. My voice didn't shake. I have no idea why. "Tell him the timetable's broken. Tell him I'm not asking him to bless what I'm about to do. I'm asking him to be at the lawful door when I open it from the inside."

Yuri stared at me. "And if he says no?"

"Then I do it right anyway," I said, "and I do it alone."

It was the truest thing I'd ever said, and the most frightened, and the most free.

---

I want to mark this moment, because everything that comes after turns on it, and because I have spent my whole life not marking the moments that mattered and only seeing them later, in the wreckage, in the rearview.

We had a plan built for eight hours.

We had — the system told me, and I made myself believe it, made myself stop arguing with a thing that had never once been wrong about a clock — a great deal less than that. The patient ocean of the night had drained out between one breath and the next, and what was left was a strip of wet sand and a tide coming in fast and the whole family standing on it.

Basti was packing his hands' worth of tools into a coat that had seen worse harbours than this one. Yuri was already speaking low and fast into a phone, and I heard the word *Commandant,* and I heard the pause that came after it, the pause of a tired and weary man somewhere across the city being woken into the worst version of the night he'd been dreading. Out past the cracked window the harbour lights smeared on black water, and somewhere beyond them, north, in the dead part of the city where the hospital rotted, the building I'd memorized from a flashback I'd survived years ago was about to empty itself of everything Clara had bled to save.

I looked one last time at the screen, at the cold and total truth of it, at the gift and the curse of having built a thing that would never lie to me even when the lie would have let me sleep.

I had told myself we had until dawn. I had believed it. I had hung the whole bruised night on that one soft word.

The system had just taken it back.

The window to move had just collapsed from eight hours to forty minutes.
