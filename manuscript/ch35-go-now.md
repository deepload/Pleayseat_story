# Chapter 35: Go Now

There is a patience that is just fear wearing a uniform.

I had lived inside it for hours. We all had. The flat above the harbor had gone quiet in the specific way rooms go quiet when everyone is holding the same breath.

Basti at the window. Shoulder against the frame. A cigarette he kept forgetting to light. Yuri folded under his hood like a man praying to a god he'd already stopped believing in. And me in the middle of it, in the blue dark, in the only light any of us had trusted all night.

The whole night was narrowing to a single bright line. And a plan that still needed one thing before it could live.

Time.

That was the deal we'd made with ourselves. We go at dawn.

*Dawn.* We'd said it so many times it had stopped being an hour. It had become a promise. A small warm light at the end of a tunnel that smelled of harbor rot and cold coffee. At dawn the light would be soft. The streets empty. Marchetti's people in position. Clara still breathing in her engine room under the dead hospital, holding the chain with both hands the way she held everything — completely, brutally, with love.

We would go in. We would bring her home.

Almost two hours. That was all that stood between us and dawn. Almost two hours to rest, to walk the plan one more time, to wait out the worst kind of patience — the kind that waits for an order that never quite comes.

Almost two hours felt like an ocean.

I should have known better than to trust an ocean.

---

To understand what the night had become, you have to understand what the system had become.

For seven days I had fed it everything. The crumbs of the hidden message, gathered one by one from the corners of the network. Old stolen files nobody was looking at anymore. Leaks nobody had bothered to open. The money, which I had followed from account to account through the burning house. The names. All twelve. Even the one I could not say out loud.

The building. The relay points. The convoys that carried children the way a smile carries a lie.

I had poured the whole shape of the beast into it, drop by bleeding drop. And somewhere in the dark of that machine, it had done the one thing I had built it to do.

It had listened.

Not the way a person listens, with their want and their grief in the way. It listened the way the tide listens to the moon. Patient. Total. Indifferent to whether I could bear the answer.

All week it had sat in the corner of every room. It searched the buried forums where faceless people trade in whispers. The locked channels where one door opens onto another door. The markets at the edge of the internet where everything is for sale, including silence.

I had come to trust it the way you trust a dog that growls at a door before you've heard the footsteps. It knew things before I did. It felt the floor shift a half-second before I did.

I won't explain how it works. That's a promise I made. What matters is what it *did.* In the only language that counts. The language of a terrified man at three in the morning when the thing he loves is underground.

It had been counting.

Somewhere in all that listening, it had found a rhythm in the beast. A heartbeat under the heartbeat. The convoys moved on a schedule. The money moved with them. The silences between movements had a shape. And the system had learned that shape the way I had once learned Clara's breathing in the dark — well enough to know, eyes closed, whether she was asleep or only pretending.

All night it had held that rhythm against its ear. Watching for the next beat. Telling me, in its wordless way: *you have time. The next thing happens at dawn. You have until dawn.*

And then, at five twenty in the morning, the rhythm broke.

---

I felt it before I saw it.

That sounds like a lie. The kind of thing a man says afterward to make it seem like he was ever in control. But it's true. And I no longer have the means to lie.

I was standing at the table, a cold cup in my hand. Half-listening to Basti telling Yuri about a door in Naples that had to be opened with a violin string. The room was warm. Human. Almost bearable.

That's the part nobody tells you about people like us — people who live the night behind screens. That even in the worst nights, there are these pockets of warmth. A stupid story. A shared cold coffee. The brotherhood of people who have nowhere else to go but toward each other.

And then something went cold at the center of my chest. The way the air goes wrong a half-second before a glass falls off a shelf. Before you hear it. When the body simply knows the world is about to break.

I set the cup down. I didn't decide to. My hand did it on its own.

Then I looked.

I won't describe the screen. What matters is what it told me. In plain words. The way it would have told me if it had a mouth instead of an eye.

It told me the schedule had changed.

Not the rescue. *Theirs.* The beast's schedule. The thing under the building, the thing with the children inside, the thing whose heartbeat I had been counting on to stay slow until the light came — it had just woken up.

Somewhere inside the dark machinery of PHANTOM MERCY, a switch had been thrown. The kind of kill switch a man throws only when he's felt a breath on the back of his neck. And the patient rhythm I had trusted all night lurched forward. The way a clock lurches when a hand reaches in and drags it.

The convoy that was supposed to roll at dawn was going to roll before dawn.

And the children would be on it. The forty-seven children I had carried in my chest for seven days like stones. The children Clara had let herself be taken to protect.

"Delta." Yuri had gone still under his hood. He was reading my face the way you read the face of a man at a deathbed — pulling the news off him before anyone speaks. That's a gift paranoid men have. Too many years watching for betrayal behind every handle. The man you work beside all night might be a friend, a cop, or a trap. You never know which. They read a face the way I read a line of code. "What."

I didn't answer. Not yet. I was doing what the system had just done in a single heartbeat, the thing my human, grieving, exhausted mind needed three full seconds to do behind it.

I was subtracting.

---

Here is the cruelty of it. The thing I felt in my body, standing in that blue room with the harbor breathing through the cracked glass.

When something moves up in this world — when a timetable lurches forward — it doesn't just steal hours. It rearranges *everything that depends on those hours.*

Marchetti's rescue was a row of dominoes. Laid by surgeon's hands across an entire night. His people in position by dawn. The lawful door opened at the lawful hour. Clean arrests. Prosecutable. With the evidence chain that Clara had spent her freedom keeping intact. So that no clever lawyer could ever say it was broken.

Every piece balanced on a single word.

Dawn.

Take away dawn and the row of dominoes doesn't just shorten. It *falls.*

Because here is what nobody ever says about a ticking clock. The clock is not the enemy. The clock is just a clock. The enemy is the gap. The gap between when *I* could move and when *they* could move. All night that gap had been a luxury. Hours wide. Room to breathe. And the system, in its cold and total way, was closing that gap in front of my eyes. Line by line. No longer telling me how long until dawn. Telling me how long until *they* were gone. How long until the convoy doors shut. Until the building emptied. Until Clara — who would have risked everything, who would have *held the chain to the end* — found herself alone in an engine room under a hospital that had no reason left to keep her alive.

Almost two hours until dawn.

That number was dead. It had been a comfort and it was a corpse. I let it go.

The new number was the only one that mattered. The system gave it to me the way it gave me everything. Without mercy. Without apology. The truth and nothing wrapped around it.

I read it. I read it again, the way Clara taught me — lips moving over the weight of it — because she said you don't truly know a sentence until you've carried it with your body.

Then I said it out loud. Into the room. Into the faces of the two men I loved, who had crossed half the earth to stand with me in this rotten flat.

Saying it out loud was the first true thing I had spoken in hours.

"We can't wait for dawn."

---

The room did not explode. That's where the films get it wrong. When the floor finally drops, people don't shout. They go very still and very quiet. Like people who have just understood that the next few minutes will decide the rest of their lives.

Basti lit the cigarette at last. One match, cupped in both scarred hands. The little flame lit his face from below. He wasn't the comic anymore. Not the man who opened doors with violin strings. He was just a man from Marseille who had chosen the right side too late in life for it to look easy.

"Then we don't wait." Simple as a key turning. "I've been ready since I was twelve. Tell me the door."

Yuri was already moving. Already paranoid. Already ahead of us. "Marchetti. His people aren't in position. They're hours out. If we go now, we go *without* him." He looked at me from under the hood. No fear in it. Only the old terrible question. "Without the law. Without the lawful door. Just us and a building full of them."

And there it was. The thing that had been waiting under the whole night like a second heartbeat. The choice I had been making my entire life and getting wrong every single time.

The dirty road was right there. Warm and familiar as an old coat. Go now. Go hard. Go the way I went when I was younger and emptier. Break the door. Break the rules. Break whatever stood between me and the room where she was. And let the evidence and the lawyers and the lawful hour go to hell.

That old vertigo — the one that whispers you can smash everything and there will never be a bill.

She was *under that building.* Every cell in my body was screaming to take the fast road. The broken road. The road that had destroyed every good thing I had ever touched, because I always, always forgot the target in the heat of the wanting.

I had promised her I wouldn't. Not in words. In something deeper than words. I had built an entire structure out of grief precisely so I would never again be the man who burns the truth to ash to save the moment.

The whole point of what I had built — its whole soul, the thing that made it *hers* — was that it brought the truth out clean enough to survive daylight. Clean enough to stand in front of a judge. To take the dirty road now would be to save her body and lose the reason I deserved to.

But there was a clock now, and the clock did not care about my soul.

I looked at the new number. I looked at the two of them. And somewhere under the floor of this city, in a dead hospital, a woman I loved was holding a chain together with her bare hands. Not knowing — *not knowing* — that the night had just lurched out from under all of us.

"Get Marchetti on the line." My voice didn't shake. I don't know why. "Tell him the timetable's broken. Tell him I'm not asking him to bless what I'm about to do. I'm asking him to be at the lawful door when I open it from the inside."

Yuri stared at me. "And if he says no?"

"Then I do it right anyway. And I do it alone."

It was the truest thing I had ever said. The most frightened. And the most free.

---

I want to mark this moment. Because everything that follows turns on it. And because I have spent my whole life not marking the moments that mattered — only seeing them later, in the wreckage, in the rearview mirror.

We had a plan built for almost two hours.

We had much less than that. The system had told me, and I forced myself to believe it, forced myself to stop arguing with something that had never once been wrong about a clock. The patient ocean of the night had drained between one breath and the next. What was left was a strip of wet sand and a fast-rising tide, and the whole family standing on it.

Basti packed his tools into a coat that had known worse harbors. Yuri was already speaking low and fast into a phone — one of those cheap phones you pay for in cash, burn after one use, never switch on twice in the same place. I heard the word *Commandant.* And I heard the silence that followed it. The silence of a tired man somewhere across the city, being woken into the worst version of the night he had been dreading.

Through the cracked window the harbor lights bled onto black water. And beyond them, in the dead part of the city where the hospital rotted near the old port, the building I had memorized was about to empty itself of everything Clara had bled to save.

I looked one last time at the screen. At its cold and total truth. At the gift and the curse of having built something that would never lie to me, even when the lie would have let me sleep.

I had told myself we had until dawn. I had believed it. I had hung the whole bruised night on that one soft word.

The system had just taken it back. Not the convoy timer. Not yet. The time I had left — the time *I* had — to open that door from the inside.

Our window had just collapsed from almost two hours to forty minutes.

And somewhere in that building, Marchetti's mole was already moving.
