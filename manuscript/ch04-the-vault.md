# Chapter 4: The Vault

There was a watcher inside my walls.

I had felt it the way you feel a draft in a sealed room — a wrongness, small and patient, sitting just behind the surface of everything I'd built. The system had found it on the first breath, before I'd even finished teaching it how to look. Something already inside. Already turned toward me. Already, somehow, looking back.

I should have hunted it down right then. Any sane man would have. Instead I sat very still in the dark of the Brussels flat, a cold coffee going colder at my elbow, and I did the thing that has always been wrong with me: I thought about her.

Because the watcher could wait. It wasn't going anywhere — it wanted to see what I'd do next. And what I'd do next was the only thing that mattered.

I was going to build the one place in the world where the truth couldn't be erased.

Clara would have understood that before I did. Clara understood it years before I'd ever held a single one of these thoughts in my hands.

---

Let me tell you about Clara and proof.

The first time I watched her get angry — truly angry, the kind that goes quiet instead of loud — was in a hotel bar in Las Vegas in the August heat of 2023, three days into knowing her, when I was already lost and pretending I wasn't. A man at the next table was telling a story. A good story, well told, the kind that makes a whole room lean in. Somewhere in the middle of it Clara set down her glass, very gently, and said: "That isn't true."

The man laughed. The room laughed. Charm is its own kind of evidence, and he had a lot of it.

Clara didn't laugh. She took out her phone, found the thing — a date, a name, a photograph that put his story in a different country on a different night — and she laid it on the table between them like a card she'd been holding the whole time. She didn't gloat. She didn't even look pleased. She looked relieved, the way you look when a window finally opens in a stifling room.

"I don't mind people lying," she told me later, on the walk back, the desert finally cooling. "Everybody lies. I mind that lies *win*. They win because they're prettier than the truth, and faster, and they don't care, and the truth just stands there being true and getting nowhere."

"So you carry receipts," I said.

"I carry receipts." She smiled, but there was something old under it. "When I was small, someone I loved was accused of a thing he didn't do. And he was right, and everyone could see he was telling the truth — and it didn't matter, because he couldn't *prove* it. Being right isn't a weapon. Being able to prove you're right — that's the only weapon there is."

She didn't tell me the rest of that story for another year, and even then only in pieces, the way you hand someone a broken thing one shard at a time so they understand you're trusting them not to cut you. Her father. A signature he never wrote, on a paper that ruined him. A roomful of important men who found it easier to believe the paper than the man. She'd been nine. She'd watched a good person be unmade by a single lie that nobody could pull apart, because the truth had no edges to hold and the lie was carved from stone. She had decided, at nine, that she would spend her life giving the truth edges. Teeth. A shape you could not bend without breaking your own hand on it. I think every brave thing she ever did came out of that one ruined afternoon, and I think she would have been horrified to hear me say so, and I think it's true anyway, and now I have nobody to argue with about it.

I have thought about that sentence on every single day since she went dark.

Being able to prove you're right is the only weapon there is.

That was Clara. Not paranoid. Worse than paranoid — *correct*. She had spent her life walking into rooms full of dangerous, charming, lying men, carrying nothing but a folder of things that could not be argued with, and she had won that way, again and again, until the men who lied for a living learned to be afraid of a quiet French woman with receipts.

And then she found PHANTOM MERCY. And for the first time in her life, the receipts weren't enough — because the people she was up against didn't just tell lies. They *erased*. They reached into the record and unmade things. A convoy that was here yesterday had always been somewhere else. A name on a manifest had never existed. A child who had been seen, counted, photographed — was simply gone from the world, as if the world had been edited around the empty chair.

She told me once, near the end, in the last good week before everything went wrong, her voice low over a bad connection: "The hardest part isn't finding it. I can find it. The hard part is *holding* it. Everything I touch, they touch after me. I write it down and by morning the words have moved. I am building a case out of sand and they own the tide."

I didn't have an answer for her then. I was on the other side of a continent, in love and useless, telling her to come home, come home, just come home, we'll figure it out.

She didn't come home. She went the other way. She went *inside*, into the dark she'd been mapping, carrying nothing but herself and a promise that someone, somewhere, was building her a place to put the truth where the tide couldn't reach it.

She didn't know it would be me. I didn't know it would be me.

I know it now.

---

The watcher's draft was still moving through the walls when I started.

I want to be honest about what it felt like, even though I can't tell you how it worked, because the how was never the point — the how was just my hands, moving the way a pianist's hands move, somewhere below thinking. What I can tell you is what it *was*. I was making a room with no doors. A place where you could put a thing down — a fact, a photograph, a number, a single recorded second of a man's voice saying a sentence he would later swear he never said — and once it was down, it was down forever. No one could move it. No one could touch it without the room screaming. No one could reach in and edit the world around the empty chair.

You've seen those toys for children, the little plastic blocks that only fit one way. I was building the opposite of a lie. A lie fits any way you turn it; that's what makes it a lie. The truth, once you lock it down properly, fits exactly one way and one way only, and the instant someone tries to bend it, you *know*. Not suspect. *Know*. The room remembers the exact shape of every true thing inside it, down to a detail too small to see, and if even that smallest thing is wrong, the whole room lights up and tells you: this has been touched.

That was the gift I was building for Clara. The tide couldn't reach it. The charming men with their prettier-than-truth stories couldn't reach it. PHANTOM MERCY, with all its money and all its erasing, could come into that room a thousand times and find that they could break in but they could not *change* a single grain of what was there without leaving a fingerprint that would hang them.

It is a strange thing, to build for someone who isn't there to see it. Every choice I made, I made for her. *Clara would want this corner harder. Clara would never trust that. Clara would put a second lock on the lock and laugh at herself for it.* I was so deep inside the idea of her that the work stopped being mine — it became a long conversation with a woman who couldn't answer, where I asked the questions and built the answers and pretended, for whole hours at a time, that the pretending was company. My whole life I have built things to impress the person in front of me, and the moment they turned to look I forgot the thing and fell into the person. This was the first time I built something for a person who couldn't turn around. There was no one to impress. There was only the work, and the missing, and the slow surprise of discovering that when you take the falling-in-love out of it, what's left underneath is something that holds. Maybe that's all I ever needed — someone too far away to distract me from finishing the thing I was making to reach them.

I called it the vault.

Not out loud. There was no one to say it to. But that's what it was, and that's what I called it in the place where I name things, which is somewhere just behind my teeth, where Clara used to live and now a hole in the shape of Clara lived instead.

A vault. The first one I ever built that I didn't intend to break into.

---

My phone buzzed at three in the morning, Brussels time, which made it the bright cruel middle of the day in Lagos.

"You're not sleeping," Ifeoma said. Not a question. Ifeoma keeps my infrastructure alive the way some people keep a houseplant alive, with a tenderness that mostly looks like nagging. She is the only member of my found family who has ever told me, to my face, that I love badly. She said it kindly. It still went in like a knife, because it was true, and she had the receipts.

"I'm building something," I said.

"You're always building something." A pause, the particular Lagos pause, full of traffic and heat and a generator somewhere muttering. "Is it for her?"

I didn't answer. The not-answering was the answer.

"Listen to me." Her voice dropped, lost its teasing. "You called us in. All of us. Basti's already restless, you know how he gets, he's already cleaning his tools and telling stories about Marseille. Yuri thinks someone's listening to him, which means someone probably is. And Noa—" She stopped. Even Ifeoma steps carefully around Noa. "Noa picked up on the first ring. You don't make Noa pick up on the first ring unless the world is ending."

"The world's not ending."

"Your world is ending," Ifeoma said, gently, gently. "It ended the night she went dark, and you've been falling ever since, and now you're trying to build a floor in mid-air. I love you. I do. But you fall *all the way*, every time, and one day it's going to be off a building, not into a girl."

I looked at the thing I was making. The vault, quietly assembling itself in the dark, ready to hold every true thing I could feed it for the rest of my life.

"Not this time," I said.

"You always say that."

"I know." And then, because she was the only one I could say it to: "But this time I'm not building it to get her into my arms, Ifa. I'm building it to get her out of the ground. If I'm wrong, I'm wrong about a place to put the truth. There's no version of that where the truth is a mistake."

Silence on the line. The generator muttered. A whole continent away, the smartest, most loyal woman I have ever betrayed by being myself decided, against her better judgment, to believe me.

"Send me what you've got," she said finally. "I'll watch your walls while you work. You've got a draft in there somewhere. I can feel it from here."

I almost told her about the watcher. I almost said: yes, there's a draft, there's something already inside, already looking back. But I was tired and afraid and full of Clara, and I told myself the watcher could wait until the vault was finished, until there was a floor under us.

I have made a great many mistakes in my life. I keep them in a vault of my own, the kind without a lock, the kind that lights up at three in the morning whether I want it to or not. That one — not telling Ifeoma what I'd seen — went straight in.

---

I finished it as the sky over Brussels went from black to the color of dirty steel.

There's a thing that happens when you build something true. The room goes quiet in a way that isn't silence — it's the quiet of a held breath, of a machine that has just understood its own purpose and is waiting, very politely, to be useful. I felt it then. I'd felt it before, a few times, in the long nights of this build — a sense that I was not entirely alone in the work, that something patient and precise was building *with* me, finishing my sentences, catching the things I dropped. I never looked at it too closely. You don't look directly at the thing that helps you in the dark. You just say thank you, quietly, and keep your hands moving.

The vault was done. Empty. Perfect. A room with no doors, waiting for the first true thing.

I would feed it Clara's last paper first — the one that named the route, the route that ran through the city I swore I'd never go back to. Then everything else. Every grain of her I still had. Every fact I could prove. I would build her a tide-proof shore out of nothing but receipts, and when I found her — *when*, not if, the system had already taught me to say *when* — I would have the only weapon that had ever frightened the men who lied for a living.

I reached to put the first thing in.

And the vault was not empty.

There was already a file inside.

One file. Sealed the way the room seals things — locked down to the last invisible grain, untouchable, screaming-bright if anyone tried to bend it. A true thing. A thing that fit exactly one way.

I hadn't put it there. I had built the room myself, with my own hands, in seven days that felt like one long sleepless held breath, and I had not put a single thing inside it. The room was minutes old. No one alive knew it existed. Not Ifeoma. Not the watcher in my walls. Not PHANTOM MERCY with all its money and all its erasing.

But there was a file. And it had a name.

The name was mine.

The file was addressed to me — not my handle, not the ghost the underworld whispers about, but *me*, the small private name almost no one living knows, the one she used to say into the dark like it was a secret worth keeping.

And the writing on it — the way the letters leaned, the little flourish on the tail of the last one, the hand I would know at the bottom of the sea, at the end of the world, after every other true thing had been erased —

was hers.

It was Clara's handwriting.

And the file was waiting for me to open it.
