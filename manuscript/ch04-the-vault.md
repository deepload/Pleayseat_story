# Chapter 4: The Vault

There was a watcher inside my walls.

I felt it the way you feel a draft in a sealed room. Wrong. Small. Patient. Buried under everything I had built. The system caught it on the first breath, before I'd even finished teaching it to see. Something already there. Already turned toward me. Already looking back.

I should have hunted it down right then. Any hacker worth the name would have. First rule of the channels where I grew up: you sense a pair of eyes, you follow the thread before it follows you.

I didn't move.

I sat in the dark of the Brussels flat, face blue from the screen. A coffee going cold at my elbow. Third of the night. Or fourth. You stop counting.

And I did what I have always done.

I thought about her.

The watcher could wait. It wasn't going anywhere. It wanted to see what I did next. That's their patience, the people who live in the seams — they can sit on a wire for days without making a sound. Just watching.

And what I did next was the only thing that mattered.

I was going to build the one place in the world where the truth couldn't be erased.

Clara would have understood that before I did. Clara understood it years before I'd even touched the idea.

---

I need to say who she was. And what proof meant to her.

The first time I watched her get angry — really angry, the kind that goes quiet instead of loud — was in a hotel bar in Las Vegas. August 2023. The big annual gathering of people like us. Thousands of tinkerers from everywhere, badges with fake names, taking the stage to show the flaw they'd found in the lock everyone thought was solid.

Three days since I'd met her. I was already lost and pretending otherwise.

A man at the next table was telling a story. A good one. The kind that makes a whole room lean forward. Clara set down her glass in the middle of it. Very gently. And said: "That isn't true."

The man laughed. The room laughed. Charm is its own kind of evidence, and he had plenty to sell.

Clara didn't laugh. She took out her phone. She found the thing — a date, a name, a photograph that put his story in another country on another night. She laid it on the table like a card she'd been holding from the start.

She didn't gloat. She didn't even look satisfied. She looked relieved. The way a person looks when a window finally opens in a stifling room.

"I don't mind that people lie," she told me later, walking back, the desert finally cooling around us. "Everyone lies. What I mind is that lies *win*. They win because they're prettier than the truth. Faster. They don't care. While the truth just stands there, being true, getting nowhere."

"So you keep the receipts," I said.

"I keep the receipts." She smiled. Something old lived underneath it. "When I was small, someone I loved was accused of something he didn't do. He was right. Everyone could see he was telling the truth. Didn't matter. Because he couldn't *prove* it."

She looked at me.

"Being right isn't a weapon. Being able to prove you're right — that's the only weapon there is."

She didn't tell me the rest until a year later. Piece by piece. The way you hand someone a broken thing one shard at a time — trusting them not to get cut.

Her father. A signature he never wrote, at the bottom of a paper that destroyed him. A room full of important men who found it easier to believe the paper than the man.

She was nine. She watched a good person come apart under a single lie no one could unravel. The truth had nothing to grip. The lie was carved from stone.

At nine, she decided to spend her life giving the truth edges. Teeth. A shape that couldn't be bent without breaking your hand on it.

I believe every brave thing she ever did was born from that one ruined afternoon. I believe she would have been horrified to hear me say it. I believe it's true anyway. And I have no one left to argue with about it.

I have thought about that sentence every single day since she disappeared.

*Being able to prove you're right is the only weapon there is.*

That was Clara. Not paranoid. Worse: *correct*. She had spent her life walking into rooms full of dangerous, charming, lying men, carrying nothing but a thin sheaf of facts that couldn't be disputed. And she won. Again and again. Until the men who lied for a living learned to fear a quiet French woman with her receipts.

And then she found PHANTOM MERCY.

For the first time, the receipts weren't enough. Because the people across from her didn't just lie. They *erased*. They reached into the record and unmade things. A convoy that had been here yesterday had always been somewhere else. A name on a manifest had never existed. A child who had been seen, counted, photographed — gone from the world. As if the world had been redrawn around the empty chair.

I had seen erasures. I'd sold my own, once, to people whose handles I prefer to forget. But this was something else. Patient. Industrial. An eraser passed over the world until nothing remained but bare paper.

She told me once, near the end. The last good week before everything broke. Her voice low, over a bad connection, the kind of channel you use when you don't trust anything else.

"The hardest part isn't finding it. I can find it. The hardest part is *holding* it. Everything I touch, they touch after me. I write it down and by morning the words have moved. I'm building a case in sand and the tide belongs to them."

I had no answer. I was on the other side of a continent. In love and useless. Telling her to come home. Come home. Come back. We'll figure it out.

She didn't come home. She went the other way. She went *inside*. Into the dark she'd been mapping. Carrying nothing but herself and the promise that someone, somewhere, was building her a place to put the truth. Where the tide couldn't reach it.

She didn't know it would be me. I didn't know it would be me.

I know it now.

---

The watcher's draft was still moving through the walls when I started.

The how never mattered. The how was just my hands. Running across the keys somewhere below thought, in the state my kind calls *the tunnel*. The night folding in. The screen becoming the only light. The world reduced to the hum of the fans and the dry heat of the machines. The long nights in the forums where no one has a face. Only a handle and a paranoia that never sleeps.

What mattered was what it *was*.

I was building a room with no doors. A place to set something down — a fact, a photograph, a number, one recorded second of a man's voice saying something he would later swear he never said. And once it was set down, it stayed down. Forever. No one could move it. No one could touch it without the room screaming. No one could reach in and redraw the world around the empty chair.

There are those children's toys, the little cubes that only fit one way. I was building the opposite of a lie.

A lie fits any way you turn it. That's what makes it a lie. The truth, once it's locked down properly, fits exactly one way. One way only. And the instant someone tries to bend it, you don't suspect. You *know*. The room remembers the exact shape of every true thing inside it. Down to the detail too small to see. If that detail is wrong, the whole room lights up and says so plainly: this has been touched.

That was the gift I was building for Clara.

The tide couldn't reach it. The charming men with their prettier-than-truth stories couldn't reach it. PHANTOM MERCY, with all its money and all its erasing, could enter that room a thousand times. And find that while they could break in, they couldn't *change* a single grain without leaving a print that would hang them. No escape without a signature. No copy that could be disguised. Not one file that could be drowned in noise.

The exact opposite of everything I had built when I was young. When I sold doors, not walls.

It is a strange thing, to build for someone who isn't there to see it. Every choice I made, I made for her. *Clara would want this corner harder. Clara would never trust that. Clara would put a second lock on the lock, and laugh at herself for it.*

I was so deep inside the idea of her that the work stopped being mine. It became a long conversation with a woman who couldn't answer. I asked the questions. I built the answers. And I pretended, for hours at a time, that the pretending was company.

My whole life I have built things to dazzle the person in front of me. The moment they turned to look, I forgot the thing and fell into the person. This was the first time I built for someone who couldn't turn around. No one to impress. Just the work. And the missing. For once, what was left underneath held.

I called it the vault.

Not out loud. There was no one to say it to. But that's what it was, and that's the name I gave it in the place where I name things — just behind my teeth. Where Clara used to live. Where now, in her place, lived a hole in the shape of Clara.

A vault. The first one I had ever built without intending to break in.

---

My phone buzzed at three in the morning, Brussels time. The bright, cruel middle of the day in Lagos.

"You're not sleeping," Ifeoma said. Not a question.

Ifeoma keeps my infrastructure alive the way some people keep a plant alive: with a tenderness that mostly looks like harassment. She is the only member of my chosen family who has ever told me, to my face, that I love badly. She said it with kindness. It went in like a blade. Because it was true. And she had the receipts.

"I'm building something," I said.

"You're always building something." A silence. That particular Lagos silence — full of traffic, heat, a generator muttering somewhere far off. "Is it for her?"

I didn't answer. Not answering was the answer.

"Listen to me." Her voice dropped. Lost its teasing. "You called us in. All of us. You know what it means when you light up the network after disappearing that long. Basti's already restless, you know how he gets — cleaning his tools, telling stories about Marseille. Yuri thinks someone's listening to him, which means someone probably is. He's changed channels twice tonight already. And Noa—"

She stopped. Even Ifeoma treads carefully around Noa.

"Noa picked up on the first ring. You don't make Noa pick up on the first ring. Not unless the world is ending."

"The world isn't ending."

"Your world is." Gently. Gently. "It ended the night she disappeared. You've been falling ever since. And now you're trying to build a floor in mid-air. I love you. That's true. But you fall *all the way*, every time. And one day it's going to be off a building, not into a girl."

I looked at the thing I was making. The vault, assembling itself in the dark, line by line. Ready to hold every true thing I could give it for the rest of my life.

"Not this time," I said.

"You always say that."

"I know." And then, because she was the only one I could say it to: "But this time I'm not building it to get her into my arms, Ifa. I'm building it to get her out of the ground. If I'm wrong, I'm wrong about a place to put the truth. There is no version of that where the truth is a mistake."

Silence on the line. The generator muttered. A whole continent away, the smartest and most loyal woman I have ever failed by simply being myself decided, against all good sense, to believe me.

"Send me what you've got." She finally let it go. "I'll watch your walls while you work. You've got a draft in there somewhere. I can feel it from here."

I almost told her about the watcher. Almost said: yes, there's a draft, there's something already inside, already turned toward me.

Every rule I had ever learned about staying alive in the dark screamed at me to say it. The ones you learn so the dark doesn't swallow you. *A watcher you don't name is a watcher who wins.*

But I was tired. And afraid. And full of Clara. I told myself the watcher could wait. Wait until the vault was finished. Until there was a floor under our feet.

I have made a great many mistakes. I keep them in a vault of my own — the kind with no lock. The kind that lights up at three in the morning whether I want it to or not. That one — not telling Ifeoma what I had seen — went straight in.

---

I finished it as the sky over Brussels turned from black to the color of dirty steel.

There is a thing that happens when you finish something true. The room goes quiet in a way that isn't silence. It's the quiet of a held breath. A machine that has just understood its own purpose and is waiting, very politely, to be useful.

I felt it then. I had felt it before, a few times, in the long nights of this build. The feeling of not being entirely alone in the work. That something patient and precise was building *with* me. Finishing my sentences. Catching what I dropped when my eyes closed a second too long.

I never looked at it too closely. You don't look directly at the thing that helps you in the dark. You say thank you, very quietly, and keep your hands moving.

The vault was done. Empty. Perfect. A room with no doors, waiting for the first true thing.

I would give it Clara's last report first — the one that named the route. The route that crossed through the city I had sworn never to go back to. Then everything else. Every grain of her I still had. Every fact I could prove.

I would build her a tide-proof shore out of nothing but receipts. And when I found her — *when*, not if; the system had already taught me to say *when* — I would hold the only weapon that had ever frightened the men who lied for a living.

I reached in to place the first thing.

The vault was not empty.

There was already a file inside.

One file. Sealed the way the room seals things. Locked down to the last invisible grain. Untouchable. Ready to blaze with light the instant anyone tried to bend it. A true thing. A thing that fit exactly one way.

I hadn't put it there. I had built this room myself, with my own hands, in three days that had been one long sleepless held breath. I had put nothing inside it. The room was minutes old. No one in the world knew it existed. Not Ifeoma. Not the watcher in my walls. Not PHANTOM MERCY, with all its money and all its erasing.

But there was a file. And it had a name.

The name was mine.

Not my handle. Not the ghost that the shadow world whispers about in its hidden channels the way it whispers the names of the dead. *Mine*. The small private name that almost no living person knows. The one she used to say in the dark like a secret worth keeping.

And the handwriting on it — the way the letters leaned, the small flourish at the tail of the last one, the hand I would know at the bottom of the sea, at the end of the world, after every other true thing had been erased —

was hers.

Clara's handwriting.

And the file was waiting for me to open it.
