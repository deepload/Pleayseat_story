# Chapter 26: The Satellite War

The graph had put Clara at the center of everything, and the math had drawn the only conclusion math ever draws: the one who has seen the whole picture is the one they cannot afford to keep alive. I had sat with that all night, in the blue light of a screen that never blinks, with a cup of coffee going cold beside the keyboard the way it always does when the work eats the hours. It was the kind of fact that doesn't let a man sleep so much as stand guard over him, hand on his shoulder, leaning in to be sure he understands.

So I did the only thing I know how to do when a fact is too heavy to lift.

I went looking for an old one to set under it.

Because here is the truth about being the man I am, the man who broke into the world for twenty years before he started trying to defend it: I have a cellar full of nights. Every impossible room I ever talked my way out of, every fortress that looked like it had no seam until it did, every time someone smarter and richer and better-armed than me thought they'd won and then found out, too late, that the loser in the corner had been counting cards the whole time — all of it is down there, racked and labelled, gathering dust. In my world there's a name for men like me, men who live in the seams of the wired world and leave no shadow on it; the polite word is *hacker*, and the truer one doesn't translate. Most men forget their worst nights on purpose. I keep mine. They're the only education I ever finished.

And there was one down there I'd been avoiding, the way a man avoids the photograph of a person he can't think about yet. A night from the old life. A night I'd won when I had no business winning, against people who do not lose. I'd been carrying the lesson of it for years without ever looking straight at it, the way a man carries a stone in his pocket from a beach he can't go back to.

I went down and got it.

---

I have to set it down the way I tell my mother things, which is to say without the words that would let anyone check my work, because the words don't matter and never did. The lesson matters. The shape of the thing matters. So here is the shape.

Years ago — another life, a life I'm not proud of and won't pretend to be, back when my handle was whispered in the dark channels and the hidden rooms where my kind gather and nobody uses the name their mother gave them — I went to war with the sky.

That's how I think of it, even now. The Satellite War. There was a network back then, the kind of organization that doesn't have a name anybody would know because the whole point of it was to not have a name anybody would know, and they ran the world from above the world. Everything they did, they did through the things that hang in the dark over our heads, the silent machinery that watches and listens and routes the whole shouting planet without anybody on the ground ever looking up. They had taken something that wasn't theirs — that is the whole of it, and it's true, and it was the kind of theft that hurt people who couldn't afford to be hurt — and they had hidden it up there, in the cold and the distance, behind the most expensive locks money has ever built.

And a group of us, the kind of group that doesn't exist on paper, that meets only in rooms with no address and forums no one can find by looking, decided to take it back.

It was the worst odds I ever played. That part matters, for what comes after. We were nobody. We were a sysadmin who'd been fired for caring too much, a girl in Kyiv who heard things in the static the rest of us couldn't, a fixer or two, and me — me before I was anything, me when I was still just the boy who could talk a lock into thinking it was already open. And they were everything. They had the money. They had the sky. They had men who killed for a living and considered it overhead.

We lost, for weeks. We lost the way a man loses when the other side is simply *more.* Every door we found, they'd already thought of. Every clever thing we tried, they had a cleverer answer waiting, bored, like a chess master swatting a child. I remember sitting in a room in a city I won't name, three monitors and an ashtray and a connection routed through six countries so nobody could trace the breath in my lungs, the whole thing collapsing around me, and feeling for the first time the specific despair of being outclassed not by a person but by a *machine of people,* an organization so much larger than you that fighting it feels like punching the ocean.

And I remember the moment it turned. I will remember it on the day I die.

---

Yuri called me, the middle of one of those nights, on the kind of line that shouldn't exist and burns itself the second you hang up. Yuri — back then she was just a voice in the dark, a girl in Kyiv who'd washed out of something serious and landed in our orbit because the serious people couldn't keep up with her either. She is, to this day, the most paranoid person I have ever loved, and she is, to this day, usually right. When Yuri says the walls are listening, the walls are worth checking. When Yuri says to burn the laptop, you've already waited too long.

She said: *You keep trying to break their door. Stop. You're never going to break their door. They built their door before you were born and they've been improving it ever since. You could spend the rest of your stupid little life on their door.*

I said something self-pitying. I was good at that, in the old days.

And she said the sentence that rebuilt me. She said: *So don't go through the door. Go through the trust. They're not strong because their locks are good. They're strong because everyone above them trusts what's below them. The whole sky believes the ground. So make the sky believe something that isn't true, and the locks open themselves, and you walk in while they're still guarding a door nobody's using.*

It sounds simple now. It is, in fact, simple — the best ideas are, that's how you know them. The whole underground runs on tricks like that, traded in whispers, never written down. But I can still feel what it did to me in that room, because it was the same thing it did to me last night, sitting in the dark in Marseille with Clara running out of time on a high floor I couldn't reach.

It turned the whole war around.

We stopped trying to be stronger than them. We were never going to be stronger than them. Instead we became something they trusted. We found the one place in their enormous, perfect, paranoid machine where they had to take something on faith — where some part of the sky simply *believed* what some part of the ground told it, because checking it every time would have been too slow, too expensive, too human — and we lived in that place of faith like a ghost in a wall. We didn't break a single lock. We made the locks believe we belonged, and they opened for us, one after another, all the way up, all the way to the cold thing they'd hidden in the dark.

We took it back. We gave it to the people it had been stolen from. And the network that ran the world from above the world spent, I am told, a small fortune and a great deal of fury sweeping every log and tearing apart every record, hunting for the door we'd broken, and never found it, because we hadn't broken a door. We'd broken a *belief.*

That was the night I learned the only thing I really know. You don't beat the bigger thing by being bigger. You beat it by finding the one place it has to trust, and standing exactly there.

---

There's a part of that night I never tell. It belongs here, because it explains why my hands wouldn't stop shaking when I read what the machine handed me at the end.

We won the Satellite War, yes. But we didn't all walk out of it.

There was a man among us — I'll call him nothing, he'd want it that way, his real name went into the dark with him and stayed there — who was the one inside. The ghost in the wall. He was the hand on the inside of the machine that made our lie move, the trusted face the locks already believed. He was the whole reason it worked, and he knew, going in, exactly what that meant. The one inside is the one who can't run. The one inside is standing in the seam when the seam closes. The rest of us were out in the dark, working the lie from a safe distance, from basements and burner connections and rooms that didn't exist, and he was *in there,* in the heart of the thing, holding the door of belief open with his own body so the rest of us could walk through it.

They found him. Not the door — they never found the door. But they found *him,* eventually, the way the big animal always eventually finds the small hand in its own machinery. And by then we had what we came for and the war was already lost on their side and won on ours, but it didn't matter to him, did it, because the trap had already closed on the one who'd been standing in it the whole time.

I was twenty-something and I told myself it was the cost of the work. I told myself he'd known. I told myself a great many things, the way a man does when he's young and the thing he can't carry is too heavy to set down where anyone might see it. It took me years to understand the real shape of what I'd done — that the trick I'm so proud of, the one I polish in the cellar, the unbeatable beautiful trick of bending the sky's belief, has a body buried at the bottom of it. *Someone has to be inside.* And the someone inside pays for everyone outside.

I'd forgotten that, too, down in the cellar. I'd kept the lesson and lost the grave.

---

I came back up out of the cellar of old nights with the stone of it warm in my hand, and I laid it down under the heavy fact, and the heavy fact stopped crushing me and started, for the first time, to make a plan. Outside it was that hour the underground knows best, the dead flat middle of the night when honest people sleep and the seams of the world come alive, when the only light is the screen and the only sound is the low hum of the machines doing their patient work.

Because PHANTOM MERCY was the same animal. Of course it was. They're all the same animal under the skin, every organization too big to fight head-on, every fortress with too many guns. They were enormous and I was nobody. They had the money and the men and the protected names and the whole rotten weight of a thing that hides its crimes inside the one human activity the world refuses to suspect — charity, mercy, children being saved. I could no more break their door than I could break the sky. I'd already proven that, last night, dying a thousand bloodless deaths against every entrance to that building until the machine had to admit there was no clean way through the locks.

But I didn't need their locks. I'd never needed anyone's locks. I needed their *belief.*

And a thing this big, this confident, this used to no one daring — a thing like that is *riddled* with belief. It has to be. The convoys believe the paperwork. The paperwork believes the handlers. The handlers believe the names above them and the names above them believe the money is clean and the whole towering structure stands up straight on a single load-bearing assumption that nobody has ever had the nerve to test: that everyone inside it is who they say they are, and means what they say they mean.

So I wouldn't fight the building. I'd make the building believe something that wasn't true. Just like the sky. Find the one place where the whole machine had to take something on faith, the one seam where the watching eyes simply *trusted* — and stand exactly there, a ghost in the wall, and let the locks open themselves.

I felt the old certainty come up in me like a tide coming back to a beach it had forgotten. *There's always a way in.* Not through the doors. Through the trust. I'd known it last night and I'd been too tired and too frightened to recognize that I'd known it before, in a room in a city I won't name, with a girl's voice in my ear telling me to stop punching the ocean.

I asked the machine to do it. To do what we'd done to the sky. To find me the load-bearing belief in PHANTOM MERCY — the single point of faith the whole fortress stood on — and to show me how to stand there.

It went still the way it does. The whole patient weight of it bent to the question, the way it had bent to the building, to the doors, to the thousand ways a man could die. And I sat back and let myself, for one cheap second, feel something like hope. Because I'd won this exact war before. I knew the shape of the answer it was going to bring me. Find the trust, become the trust, walk through a sky full of open locks while they guarded a door nobody was using.

I'd done it once against people who do not lose.

I could do it again.

Basti came and stood in the doorway with two cups of the bitter coffee he makes when he's worried, which is the only time he makes it well. He didn't say anything. He just leaned on the frame and watched me wait for the machine, the screen-glow on both our faces turning us the color of drowned men, and I knew he was reading my face the way he always does, finding in it the thing I hadn't said out loud yet.

"You've got that look," he said finally. "The one from the old stories. The sky one."

"You remember the sky one."

"I remember you telling it at three weddings and a wake," he said. "I remember you leaving out the part where it cost something. You always leave that part out." He drank. "What's the plan, brother. Tell me there's a plan."

"There's a plan," I said. "It's the best plan I've ever had. It's the same plan that won the worst night of my life. We don't fight them. We don't break their doors. We find the one place the whole rotten thing has to trust somebody, and we stand right there, and we make it believe a thing that isn't true until it opens itself all the way up to the floor where she is." I heard my own voice gathering speed, gathering the old certainty, the tide coming back. "It works, Basti. I've *seen* it work. Against people a thousand times bigger than these animals."

He nodded slowly. He wanted to believe me. He's wanted to believe me through worse rooms than this one.

"So what's wrong," he said. "Your face. It's doing the thing again. The thing it did last night before everything broke."

I didn't answer, because the machine answered first.

---

The machine laid the answer at my feet the way it always did, patient and bloodless and certain, and I read it, and the hope went out of me like a candle a door has opened on.

Because the trick that had won the Satellite War — the beautiful, simple, unbeatable trick of making the locks believe a lie until they opened themselves all the way to the top — that trick had never, not once, worked from the outside alone.

I'd forgotten that. I'd let myself forget it, down in the cellar, polishing the lesson and skipping the cost. We hadn't beaten the sky from the ground. Nobody beats the sky from the ground. The whole thing had only worked because we'd had a ghost already living in the wall, a hand already on the inside of the machine, someone the locks already trusted, someone who could whisper our lie from a place the system would never think to doubt. Without that one person inside, the trust we needed to bend was a wall with no seam, a fortress with no traitor, a sky that believed only itself. The trick was nothing — *nothing* — without someone on the inside to make the belief move.

I stared at the answer until the words stopped being words.

There was exactly one person inside PHANTOM MERCY who the system would trust, who could whisper a lie from the heart of the machine and have the locks open themselves all the way up, who was already standing in the one place of faith the whole fortress stood on.

She'd put herself there. On purpose. To get inside. To reach the server room and hold the chain and prove the truth.

The trick that won the Satellite War only worked with someone on the inside.

And the only one inside was Clara.
