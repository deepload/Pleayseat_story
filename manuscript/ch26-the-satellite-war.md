# Chapter 26: The Satellite War

The diagram put Clara at the center of everything. And the numbers drew the only conclusion numbers ever draw. The one who has seen the whole picture is the one they cannot afford to leave breathing.

I had spent the night with that certainty. Alone in the blue glow of a screen. Coffee cold for hours. The hum of machines my only company. The kind of truth that doesn't let you sleep — that watches over you instead. Hand on your shoulder. Leaning close to make sure you understood.

So I did the only thing I know how to do when a fact is too heavy to carry.

I went looking for an older one to slide underneath it.

You need to understand something about the man I am. The man who forced his way into the world for twenty years before he decided to defend it. I have a cellar full of nights.

Every fortress I ever opened. Every man smarter than me who thought he'd won. I keep all of it. It's the only school I ever finished.

And there was one night down there I'd been avoiding. The way you avoid a photograph of someone you can't think about yet. A night from the old life. A night I won when I had no right to win it. Against people who do not lose. I'd been carrying the lesson of it for years without ever looking at it straight.

I went down and got it.

---

I have to tell it the way I tell my mother things. Without the words that let anyone check the work. The words don't matter. The lesson matters. The shape of the thing matters. Here is the shape.

Years ago — another life, a life I'm not proud of — I went to war with the sky.

That's how I think of it, even now. The Satellite War.

There was a network. The kind of organization that has no name you'd recognize. Because having no name was the whole point. No logo. No name. Not even a rumor that circulates in the dark forums late at night.

They ran the world from above the world. Everything moved through the things suspended in the dark over our heads. The silent machinery that watches, listens, routes the whole shouting planet. Without anyone on the ground ever looking up.

They had taken something that wasn't theirs. The how doesn't matter. A theft that hurt people who couldn't afford to be hurt. And they had hidden it up there. In the cold. In the distance. Behind the most expensive locks money had ever built.

And a group of us decided to take it back. The kind of group that doesn't exist on paper. That meets in internet back rooms behind so many dead drops that no one can trace the path in. Where nothing leaves a trail.

They were the worst odds I ever played. That part matters. It matters for what comes next.

We were nobody. A fired sysadmin who had cared too much about his job. A guy in Kyiv who heard things in the static that the rest of us couldn't. A fixer or two who appeared on no list, in no leak. And me. Me before I was anything. A handle on a screen. The kid who could convince a lock it was already open.

And they were everything. They had the money. They had the sky. They had men who killed for a living and considered it overhead.

We lost. For days. We lost the way you lose when the other side is simply *more.* Every door we found, they'd already thought of. Every move we made in the dark, they had sealed the crack before we reached it. Every trick, they had a sharper one waiting, looking bored. Like a chess master swatting a child away.

I can still see myself in a room. A city I won't name. Curtains drawn in the middle of the day. Three screens for light. Everything falling apart around me. Feeling for the first time that specific despair — being outclassed not by a person, but by a *machine of people.* An organization so large that fighting it is like punching the ocean.

And then I remember the moment it all turned. I will remember it on the day I die.

---

Yuri called me in the middle of one of those nights.

Back then he was just a voice on an encrypted line that shouldn't have existed. A handle with no face. A guy in Kyiv who'd failed at something serious and landed in our orbit because the serious people couldn't keep his pace.

He is, to this day, the most paranoid man I have ever loved. He changes phones the way other people change shirts. He'll tear into you if you type a real name in a chat window. And he's almost always right. When Yuri says the walls are listening, check the walls.

He said: "You keep trying to break their door. Stop. You're never going to break their door. They built their door before you were born and they've been improving it ever since. You could spend the rest of your stupid little life on their door."

I said something self-pitying. I was good at that, back then.

And then he said the sentence that rebuilt me. He said: "So don't go through the door. Go through the trust. They're not strong because their locks are good. They're strong because everything above them trusts everything below them. The whole sky believes the ground. So make the sky believe something false. The locks will open themselves. And you walk in while they're still guarding a door nobody's using."

It sounds simple now. It is simple — the best ideas always are. That's how you know them. The kind of move you whisper handle to handle for years after the person who invented it has vanished.

But I want to feel again what it did to me in that room. Because it did the exact same thing to me last night. Sitting in the dark in Marseille. A screen for my only candle. Clara running out of time, behind too many locks for me to reach her.

It turned the whole war around.

We stopped trying to be stronger than them. We were never going to be stronger. Instead we became something they trusted.

We found the one place in their enormous, paranoid machine where they had no choice but to take something on faith. Where one part of the sky simply *believed* what one part of the ground told it. Because checking every time would have been too slow, too expensive, too human. And we lived in that place of faith like a ghost in a wall.

We broke no locks. We made the locks believe we belonged. And they opened for us. One after another. All the way up. All the way to the cold thing they had hidden in the dark.

We took it back. We returned it to the people it had been stolen from.

And the network that ran the world from above the world spent, I was told, a small fortune and an enormous fury. Combing every log. Pulling apart every record. Hunting for the door we had broken. Never finding it. Because we had broken no door. We had broken a *belief.* A ghost leaves no trace. It leaves the locks convinced they did their job.

That was the night I learned the only thing I truly know. You don't beat the bigger thing by being bigger. You beat it by finding the one place it has to trust. And standing exactly there.

---

There is a part of that night I never tell. I need to tell it here, in the dark. Because it explains why my hands wouldn't stop shaking when I read what the machine handed me.

We won the Satellite War. But we didn't all walk out of it.

There was a man among us. I'll give him no name, not even a handle — he would have wanted it that way. He was the one inside. The ghost in the wall. The hand, inside the machine, that made our lie move forward. The trusted face the locks already believed.

He was the whole reason it worked. And he knew, going in, exactly what that meant. The one inside is the one who cannot run. He stands in the seam when the seam closes. No emergency switch. No exit door. No line to cut and disappear.

The rest of us were outside, in the dark. Running the lie from a safe distance. Three countries and ten relays away from any danger. He was *in there.* At the heart of the thing. Holding the door of belief open with his whole body so we could walk through it.

They found him. Not the door — they never found the door. But him, yes. Eventually. The way the big animal always eventually finds the small hand slipped into its machinery.

By then we had what we came for. The war was lost on their side, won on ours. But that meant nothing to him. The trap had already closed on the one who'd been standing inside it from the beginning.

I was in my mid-twenties. I told myself it was the cost of the work. I told myself he'd known. I told myself a great many things. The way you do when you're young and what you can't carry is too heavy to set down where anyone might see it.

It took me years to understand the real shape of what I had done. The trick I'm so proud of — the one I polish in the cellar, the beautiful, unbeatable trick of bending the sky's belief — has a body buried at the bottom of it. *Someone has to be inside.* And the someone inside pays for everyone outside.

I had forgotten that, too, down in the cellar. I'd kept the lesson and lost the grave.

---

I came back up from the cellar of old nights. I slid the old fact under the heavy one. And the heavy one stopped crushing me. For the first time, it started to look like a plan.

Because PHANTOM MERCY was the same animal. Of course it was. They're all the same animal under the skin. Every organization too large to fight head-on. Every fortress bristling with too many guns.

They were enormous and I was nobody. They had the money. The men. The protected names. And the whole rotten weight of a thing that hides its crimes inside the one human activity the world refuses to suspect. Charity. Mercy. Children being saved.

I could no more break their door than I could break the sky. I'd already proved it, the night before. Dying a thousand bloodless deaths against every entrance to that building. Until the machine admitted there was no clean way through the locks. No exploit. No crack. Nothing to force.

But I didn't need their locks. I needed their *belief.*

Same animal. Same trick. A thing this large, this certain of itself — it is *riddled* with belief. It has to be. The convoys trust the paperwork. The handlers trust the names above them. The names above trust that the money is clean. The whole structure stands upright on a single assumption no one has ever had the nerve to test. That everyone inside is who they claim to be.

So I wasn't going to fight the building. I was going to make the building believe something false. Find the one place where it had no choice but to take something on faith. And stand exactly there.

The old certainty rose in me. Like a tide returning to a forgotten beach. *There is always a way in.* Not through the doors. Through the trust.

I had known it last night. I had been too exhausted, too frightened, too saturated with cold coffee to recognize I had known it before. In a room with the curtains drawn. With a man's voice in my ear telling me to stop punching the ocean.

I asked the machine to do it. To do to the building what we had done to the sky. Find me the load-bearing belief in PHANTOM MERCY. The single point of faith the whole fortress stood on. Show me how to stand there.

It went still, the way it does. The cursor stopped blinking. All its patient weight bent toward the question. The way it had bent toward the building. Toward the doors. Toward the thousand ways a man can die.

I leaned back in the dark. The chair creaked. I gave myself one cheap second of something that felt like hope. Because I had already won this exact war. I knew the shape of the answer it was going to bring me. Find the trust. Become the trust. Walk through a sky full of open locks while they guard a door nobody's using.

I had done it once. Against people who do not lose.

I could do it again.

Basti appeared in the doorway holding two cups of the bitter coffee he makes when he's worried — the only time he makes it well. He didn't say anything. He leaned against the frame at the edge of the screen's glow and watched me wait for the machine. I knew he was reading my face the way he always does. Finding the thing I hadn't said out loud yet.

"You've got that look," he finally said. "The one from the old stories. The sky one."

"You remember the sky one."

"I remember you telling it at three weddings and a funeral." He drank. "I remember you always skipping the part where it cost something. You always skip that part." He paused. "What's the plan, brother. Tell me there's a plan."

"There's a plan," I said. "The best one I've ever had. The same plan that won the worst night of my life. We don't fight them. We don't break their doors. We find the one place the whole rotten thing has to trust somebody, and we stand right there, and we make it believe something false until it opens itself wide. All the way up to the floor where she is." I could hear my voice gaining speed. Gathering the old certainty. The tide coming back. "It works, Basti. I've *seen* it work. Against people a thousand times bigger than these animals."

He nodded slowly. He wanted to believe me. He'd wanted to believe me in worse rooms than this one. At hours when there was nothing left but a lit screen and two men who refused to admit they were afraid.

"So what's wrong," he said. "Your face. It's doing that thing again. The same thing it did last night just before everything fell apart."

I didn't answer. The machine answered first.

---

The machine laid the answer at my feet the way it always does. Patient. Bloodless. Certain. I read it in the cold light of the screen. And the hope went out of me like a candle when a door swings open.

Because the trick that had won the Satellite War had never once worked from the outside alone. Not a single time.

I had forgotten that. I'd let myself forget it, down in the cellar. Polishing the lesson, skipping the cost.

Nobody beats the sky from the ground. The whole thing had only worked because we already had a hand slipped inside the machine. Someone the locks already trusted. Someone who could whisper the lie from inside, from the one place the system would never think to doubt.

The trick was nothing — *nothing* — without someone inside to make the belief move.

I stared at the answer until the words stopped being words. Until they were nothing but pale light on my shaking hands.

There was exactly one person inside PHANTOM MERCY that the system would trust. One person who could whisper a lie from the heart of the machine and have the locks open themselves all the way to the top. One person already standing at the single point of faith on which the whole fortress rested.

She had placed herself there. On purpose. To get inside. To reach the server room, hold the chain, and prove the truth.

The trick that won the Satellite War only works with someone on the inside.

And the only one inside was Clara.
