# Chapter 18: The Case

The marker had moved overnight, and that was supposed to break me.

Whoever did it knew exactly what they were doing. They knew that a man who has spent six days narrowing the whole dark world down to one woman's heartbeat, when he is handed a location, will *run at it.* Run without thinking. Run with his heart out in front of him like a torch in a gas-filled room. They moved Clara's marker the way you move a piece of bread closer to the trap, and they set the trap for me, and they sat back to watch me sprint into it.

I didn't sprint.

I want to be honest about how hard that was. Everything in me — every cell, every old reflex, every nerve that had been screaming her name for six days — wanted to grab Basti and a car and go *now,* go *tonight,* go and tear that building apart with my hands until I found her. The image was already in my head, fully formed: kicking the door, the dark hallway, her face turning toward me out of the gloom. It was the most beautiful image I owned and it was bait, and I knew it was bait, and I still had to physically grip the edge of the desk to keep from standing up.

Because here is the thing the people who moved that marker forgot.

I used to *be* them.

I knew what a trap looks like from the inside. I'd built enough of them. And the one tell — the one thing that gives a trap away every time — is that it's too easy. Six days of fighting for every inch, every fragment, every ghost of a signal, and then suddenly, overnight, a clean bright pin dropped on a map like a gift. The dark doesn't give gifts. The dark *charges* for everything. So when it hands you something for free, you don't take it. You ask who's paying.

I sat back down.

And I did the thing that, of everything I did in those seven days, I am still proudest of, because it was the hardest, and because nobody will ever make a film about it, since it looks like a man sitting very still at a desk while the woman he loves runs out of time.

I started building the case.

---

Let me tell you what I mean by that, in plain words, because it's the whole spine of this book and I won't have you misunderstand it.

There were two ways to bring these people down.

There was *my* way — the old way, the way that lived in my hands and whispered to me at three in the morning. I could have torn into them. I could have burned their money to ash where it sat. I could have lit them up for every enemy they had, painted targets on their backs, salted their fields, ended them in the dark the way they ended people in the dark, and walked away clean and satisfied and damned. I had the skill. I had, by then, the reach. I will not pretend, even now, that I didn't sit with that option late at night and turn it over like a stone, feeling the cool underside of it, the relief of it. *They took her. They sell children. Burn them down and let God sort the ash.*

And then there was the *right* way. The slow way. The way Clara would have demanded, because Clara had spent her whole brave exacting life on it, because Clara — even now, even from inside, even bleeding evidence into the world drop by drop from wherever they were holding her — was *still doing it the right way.*

The right way was this: you don't end them. You *prove* them.

You build something so solid, so clean, so utterly unbreakable, that no lawyer on earth can unmake it. You gather every thread — the money, the convoys, the names, the dates, the lies — and you weave them into something a judge can hold in two hands and *believe.* You make the truth heavy enough that no amount of money or power can lift it off the table. And then you hand it to people whose job it is to bring the hammer down lawfully, in daylight, with their own names on it, so that when these people fall they fall *forever* — not into a grave where they become martyrs, but into a cage where they become *examples.*

The dirty way is fast and feels like justice and changes nothing. The right way is slow and feels like nothing and changes everything.

Clara taught me that. With her body, by then. With her silence. She had said it to me once, three years before, in a hotel bar that smelled of cheap whisky and other people's cigarettes, when I was still mostly the man who broke things and she was already the woman who proved them. *Anyone can destroy,* she'd said, turning her glass, not looking at me, the way she said the truest things. *It takes nothing. A child can knock down a tower. The hard thing — the only thing that ever actually saves anyone — is to build something so true it can't be knocked down. That's the work. Everything else is just noise pretending to be courage.* I hadn't understood her then. I'd thought she was being severe, the careful one lecturing the wild one. It took six days of losing her to understand she'd been handing me the only map that would ever lead me back.

So I built the case.

---

I won't tell you how the tool did it. I promised you that a long time ago and I'm not breaking the promise on the most important night.

But I'll tell you what it gave me, because that's the part that matters.

It gave me *proof that would hold.*

That's the closest honest words I have. Picture it like this. Imagine every lie these people had ever told was a thread, and the threads ran all over the world, through banks and charities and ports and convoy manifests and the bright clean lobbies of organisations that put their good works on billboards. And imagine you could take every one of those threads, and at the exact instant you touched it, the system would press a seal into it — a seal so particular, so impossible to fake, that years from now, in a courtroom, a stranger could pick up that thread and *know,* beyond any doubt, that no one had touched it since, that it was exactly as true the day it was found. Nothing added. Nothing removed. Nothing tampered with in the dark.

That's what it did. Quietly, all night, while I sat there hating the clock. Every piece of the truth I gathered, the moment I gathered it, became a piece that could not be argued with. Not *I think.* Not *we believe.* Not *trust me.* Just: *here is the truth, and here is the proof that it is the truth, and the proof cannot be broken.*

In a world where everyone lies, the only real weapon left is the one that can prove what's true. I'd known that as a slogan for years. That night I felt it in my chest like a second heartbeat. The whole reason these people had thrived was that nobody could ever *prove* it — they hid behind charity, behind borders, behind men whose word was supposed to be unquestionable. And I was about to take that away from them. Not with a bomb. With a sealed, silent, unbreakable yes.

I gathered all night.

The money first — the river of it, dirty water poured through clean pipes, donor coins flowing in as mercy and flowing out as cargo. Two thousand eight hundred and forty-seven separate movements, each one looking, on its own, like a kindness. Stacked together, sealed together, they looked like exactly what they were. A confession.

Then the routes — the aid convoys that were never carrying only aid, the manifests with their extra columns, the count of forty-seven, six points where the cargo changed hands in the dark.

Then the names. Twelve of them. Twelve people who had stood in front of cameras and accepted awards and held the hands of children for photographs, and who, when you sealed the threads and laid them side by side, were not philanthropists at all but a single organism with twelve faces and one appetite.

By dawn I had something I'd never had before in my entire crooked, brilliant, wasted life.

I had a thing that could not be denied.

---

Ifeoma called me at first light from Lagos, which meant it was the middle of her night, which meant she hadn't slept either.

Ifeoma is the conscience of everything I do. She runs the bones of it from a flat in Lagos with a generator that coughs and a daughter asleep in the next room and a faith in doing things properly that has saved my life more often than my own cleverness ever has. When I want to do something stupid, Ifeoma is the voice that asks, gently, whether I've considered not.

"You've been building all night," she said. Not a question. She could see the work breathing in the dark, the way you can hear a house is occupied.

"I have."

"Good." A pause. The generator coughed behind her. "And you're building the *clean* thing? The thing for the courts? Not the other thing?"

"The clean thing," I said.

"Say it again so I believe you."

"The clean thing, Ife. The case. Evidence. The right way."

I heard her let out a breath she'd probably been holding for six days. "You don't know," she said quietly, "how afraid I've been. Of which one of you would show up at the end. I've watched you all week and I kept thinking — he's tired enough now, he's frightened enough now, he loves her enough now, that he might just *burn it.* Might decide the rules are for people who haven't lost anybody. And I wouldn't even blame you. That's what scares me. That I'd understand."

"I thought about it," I admitted, because you don't lie to Ifeoma; it's a waste of breath.

"I know you did."

"I'm not going to."

"Why not?" she asked. Not challenging. Wanting it. Needing to hear me say the true thing out loud so it would be real for both of us.

"Because if I burn them," I said, "they become a story about a vigilante and his dead girlfriend, and the world sighs and forgets, and the next ones learn to hide better. But if I *prove* them — if I do it the slow, stupid, beautiful way she'd do it — then they become a *record.* They become the thing the next monster has to lie awake worrying about. She didn't bleed for revenge, Ife. She bled for *evidence.* If I take revenge instead, I waste everything she's spending herself for. I'd be telling her, with the whole rest of my life, that I didn't understand a single thing she was."

Ifeoma was quiet for a long time. The generator coughed. Somewhere in Lagos a rooster, absurdly, announced a morning.

"Okay," she said at last, and her voice had something wet in it. "Okay. *That's* the man I infrastructure for." She laughed, a small broken laugh. "Don't tell Basti I cried. He'll never let either of us live."

"Never," I said.

"Bring her home," Ifeoma said, "the right way," and hung up, because that's what my family does, they hand you the love and the law in the same breath and then they run before you can thank them.

---

But here is the trouble with the right way, and nobody warns you about it.

The right way needs other people.

The dirty way, I could do alone. That was always the seduction of it — it asked nothing of anyone but me. The clean way is different. The clean way means that when you've built your unbreakable thing, your sealed and silent perfect truth, you can't *use* it yourself. You have no badge. You have no warrant. You have, in the eyes of every law in every country, no right to so much as exist. All you have is the proof. And proof is useless until you put it into the hand of someone who is *allowed* to bring the hammer down.

Which meant that after a night of building the most beautiful thing I'd ever made, I now had to find a human being inside the machinery of the law — a prosecutor, someone with the authority and the spine to take my sealed truth, look at it, believe it, and act on it before the train left and the marker moved again and Clara ran out of the time she was spending so bravely.

One person. The right person. A prosecutor with reach across the borders these people hid behind, with the standing to move fast, with hands clean enough that nobody could claim the case was poisoned at the root.

Marchetti gave me the name. Commandant Thierry Marchetti, Clara's old handler, the weary hard man who needed my reach and gave me his authority and whom I trusted exactly as far as the next reversal. He gave me a name and a number and the closest thing to warmth I'd ever heard from him.

"This one," Marchetti said, "is the one. Untouchable. Twenty years putting away men who thought they were too big to fall. He's the only prosecutor in the region with the standing and the stomach to take something this size and run with it before the politics can smother it. If your case is as clean as you say, he is the door it walks through. There is no other door."

"You trust him?"

A silence on the line that I should have paid more attention to. "I trust no one," Marchetti said. "But him I trust more than most."

I sat with the name a moment. The whole plan, the slow beautiful right-way plan, the redemption I'd promised Ifeoma and the dead and the woman bleeding evidence into the dark — all of it now balanced on this single human being. One man, who could end this lawfully. One door.

I wanted to be sure. Of course I wanted to be sure. So before I put the most important truth of my life into a stranger's hands, I did the one thing the system was best at, the thing it had done with the money and the routes and the twelve faces. I asked it, quietly, to seal *this* man too. To gather the threads of him. To press its impossible seal into his life and show me, beyond any doubt, who he really was.

It gathered all night. By morning it laid the answer at my feet the way it always did — patient, bloodless, certain.

The one prosecutor who could end this was already on PHANTOM MERCY's payroll.

He had just found the wire.
