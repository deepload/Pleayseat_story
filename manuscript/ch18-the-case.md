# Chapter 18: The Case

The marker had moved overnight, and that was supposed to break me.

Whoever did it knew exactly what they were doing. They knew that a man who has spent six days narrowing the whole dark world down to one woman's heartbeat, when he is handed a location, will *run at it.* Run without thinking. Run with his heart out in front of him like a torch in a gas-filled room. They moved Clara's marker the way a hand moves a piece of bread closer to the trap, and they set the trap for me, and they sat back to watch me sprint into it.

I didn't sprint.

I need to be honest with myself about how hard that was. The flat was dark except for the screens, three of them throwing their cold blue light up onto my face, and a fourth small window in the corner where a channel I never close scrolled the quiet chatter of people who live where I live — handles I've known for years, ghosts I'll never meet, the brotherhood of the seams murmuring through the small hours. Everything in me — every cell, every old reflex, every nerve that had been screaming her name for six days — wanted to grab Basti and a car and go *now,* go *tonight,* go and tear that building apart with my hands until I found her. The image was already in my head, fully formed: kicking the door, the dark hallway, her face turning toward me out of the gloom. It was the most beautiful image I owned and it was bait, and I knew it was bait, and I still had to physically grip the edge of the desk to keep from standing up.

Because here is the thing the people who moved that marker forgot.

I used to *be* them.

I knew what a trap looks like from the inside. I'd built enough of them, baited enough hooks, sat in enough darkened rooms watching some clever soul walk straight into the thing I'd left out for him like a coin on the pavement. And the one tell — the one thing that gives a trap away every time — is that it's too easy. Six days of fighting for every inch, every fragment, every ghost of a signal pulled out of the noise, and then suddenly, overnight, a clean bright pin dropped on a map like a gift. The dark doesn't give gifts. The dark *charges* for everything, in a currency a man doesn't always find out until later. So when it hands him something for free, he doesn't take it. He asks who's paying.

I sat back down.

And I did the thing that, of everything I did in those seven days, I am still proudest of, because it was the hardest, and because nobody will ever make a film about it, since it looks like a man sitting very still at a desk while the woman he loves runs out of time.

I started building the case.

---

Here is what I mean by that, in plain words, because it is the whole spine of the thing, and I cannot afford to misunderstand it myself.

There were two ways to bring these people down.

There was *my* way — the old way, the way that lived in my hands and whispered to me at three in the morning, in the language I'd grown up speaking in chat rooms that no longer exist, among people who signed their work with handles instead of names. I could have torn into them. I could have burned their money to ash where it sat. I could have lit them up for every enemy they had, dumped their secrets into the open where the whole hungry underground could feast on them, painted targets on their backs, salted their fields, ended them in the dark the way they ended people in the dark, and walked away clean and satisfied and damned. I had the skill. I had, by then, the reach — every favour owed me across every time zone, every door I knew how to walk through sideways. I will not pretend, even now, that I didn't sit with that option late at night, the coffee gone cold beside me for the third time, and turn it over like a stone, feeling the cool underside of it, the relief of it. *They took her. They sell children. Burn them down and let God sort the ash.*

And then there was the *right* way. The slow way. The way Clara would have demanded, because Clara had spent her whole brave exacting life on it, because Clara — even now, even from inside, even bleeding evidence into the world drop by drop from wherever they were holding her — was *still doing it the right way.*

The right way was this: I would not end them. I would *prove* them.

I would build something so solid, so clean, so utterly unbreakable, that no lawyer on earth could unmake it. I would gather every thread — the money, the convoys, the names, the dates, the lies — and weave them into something a judge could hold in two hands and *believe.* I would make the truth heavy enough that no amount of money or power could lift it off the table. And then I would hand it to people whose job it is to bring the hammer down lawfully, in daylight, with their own names on it, so that when these people fell they fell *forever* — not into a grave where they become martyrs, but into a cage where they become *examples.*

The dirty way is fast and feels like justice and changes nothing. The right way is slow and feels like nothing and changes everything.

Clara taught me that. With her body, by then. With her silence. She had said it to me once, three years before, in a hotel bar that smelled of cheap whisky and other people's cigarettes, when I was still mostly the man who broke things and she was already the woman who proved them. *Anyone can destroy,* she'd said, turning her glass, not looking at me, the way she said the truest things. *It takes nothing. A child can knock down a tower. The hard thing — the only thing that ever actually saves anyone — is to build something so true it can't be knocked down. That's the work. Everything else is just noise pretending to be courage.* I hadn't understood her then. I'd thought she was being severe, the careful one lecturing the wild one, the white-hat scolding the boy who still loved the thrill of the break-in. It took six days of losing her to understand she'd been handing me the only map that would ever lead me back.

So I built the case.

---

The how doesn't matter and never did. I had sworn a long time ago never to say it, and I wasn't going to break that with myself on the most important night of my life.

What mattered was what it gave me.

It gave me *proof that would hold.*

That is the closest to honest words I have for it. It was like this. Every lie these people had ever told was a thread, and the threads ran all over the world, through banks and charities and ports and convoy manifests and the bright clean lobbies of organisations that put their good works on billboards — and underneath all that, in the parts of the world the billboards never show, through the hidden channels and the back rooms of the network where money goes to forget where it came from. And every one of those threads, at the exact instant it was touched, took a seal pressed into it — a seal so particular, so impossible to fake, that years from now, in a courtroom, a stranger could pick up that thread and *know,* beyond any doubt, that no one had touched it since, that it was exactly as true the day it was found. Nothing added. Nothing removed. Nothing tampered with in the dark.

That's what it did. Quietly, all night, while I sat there hating the clock and the servers hummed their low patient hymn under the desk like something breathing. Every piece of the truth I gathered, the moment I gathered it, became a piece that could not be argued with. Not *I think.* Not *we believe.* Not *trust me.* Just: *here is the truth, and here is the proof that it is the truth, and the proof cannot be broken.*

In a world where everyone lies, the only real weapon left is the one that can prove what's true. I'd known that as a slogan for years, the kind of thing scrawled on a forum at four in the morning that a man nods at and forgets. That night I felt it in my chest like a second heartbeat. The whole reason these people had thrived was that nobody could ever *prove* it — they hid behind charity, behind borders, behind men whose word was supposed to be unquestionable, behind the simple darkness that swallows everything left untended. And I was about to take that away from them. Not with a bomb. Not with a leak. With a sealed, silent, unbreakable yes.

I gathered all night.

The money first — the river of it, dirty water poured through clean pipes, donor coins flowing in as mercy and flowing out as cargo, washed through the kind of hidden rails that were supposed to be untraceable, that men like the ones I used to run with built precisely so they could never be followed. Two thousand eight hundred and forty-seven separate movements, each one looking, on its own, like a kindness. Stacked together, sealed together, they looked like exactly what they were. A confession.

Then the routes — the aid convoys that were never carrying only aid, the manifests with their extra columns, the count of forty-seven children, six points where the cargo changed hands in the dark.

Then the names. Twelve of them. Twelve people who had stood in front of cameras and accepted awards and held the hands of children for photographs, and who, once the threads were sealed and laid side by side, were not philanthropists at all but a single organism with twelve faces and one appetite.

By dawn I had something I'd never had before in my entire crooked, brilliant, wasted life.

I had a thing that could not be denied.

---

Ifeoma called me at first light from Lagos, which meant it was the middle of her night, which meant she hadn't slept either.

Ifeoma is the conscience of everything I do. She runs the bones of it from a flat in Lagos with a generator that coughs and a daughter asleep in the next room and a faith in doing things properly that has saved my life more often than my own cleverness ever has. She keeps the machines I depend on alive and humming a continent away, and she has never once, in all the years, asked me for anything but that I stay the better version of myself. When I want to do something stupid, Ifeoma is the voice that asks, gently, whether I've considered not.

"You've been up all night," she said. Not a question. She could see the work breathing across the wire, the lights blinking somewhere in the dark of her own racks, the way an ear can hear that a house is occupied.

"I have."

"Good." A pause. The generator coughed behind her. "And you're building the *clean* thing? The thing for the courts? Not the other thing?"

"The clean thing," I said.

"Say it again so I believe you."

"The clean thing, Ife. The case. Evidence. The right way."

I heard her let out a breath she'd probably been holding for six days. "You don't know," she said quietly, "how afraid I've been. Of which one of you would show up at the end. I've watched you all week — watched the logs, watched the hours you keep, watched the *kind* of quiet you go — and I kept thinking, he's tired enough now, he's frightened enough now, he loves her enough now, that he might just *burn it.* Might go back to the old handle and the old ways and decide the rules are for people who haven't lost anybody. And I wouldn't even blame you. That's what scares me. That I'd understand."

"I thought about it," I admitted, because no one lies to Ifeoma; it's a waste of breath.

"I know you did."

"I'm not going to."

"Why not?" she asked. Not challenging. Wanting it. Needing to hear me say the true thing out loud so it would be real for both of us.

"Because if I burn them," I said, "they become a story about a vigilante and his dead girlfriend, and the world sighs and forgets, and the next ones learn to hide better — they always learn to hide better, that's the whole game down here. But if I *prove* them — if I do it the slow, stupid, beautiful way she'd do it — then they become a *record.* They become the thing the next monster has to lie awake worrying about. She didn't bleed for revenge, Ife. She bled for *evidence.* If I take revenge instead, I waste everything she's spending herself for. I'd be telling her, with the whole rest of my life, that I didn't understand a single thing she was."

Ifeoma was quiet for a long time. The generator coughed. Somewhere in Lagos a rooster, absurdly, announced a morning.

"Okay," she said at last, and her voice had something wet in it. "Okay. *That's* the man I keep the lights on for." She laughed, a small broken laugh. "Don't tell Basti I cried. He'll never let either of us live."

"Never," I said.

"Bring her home," Ifeoma said, "the right way," and hung up, because that's what my family does, they hand me the love and the law in the same breath and then they vanish off the line before I can thank them.

---

But there is a trouble with the right way that no one ever warns a man about, and I learned it that morning.

The right way needs other people.

The dirty way, I could do alone, in the dark, with a handle and a cold cup of coffee and no one to answer to. That was always the seduction of it — it asked nothing of anyone but me. The clean way is different. The clean way meant that when I had built my unbreakable thing, my sealed and silent perfect truth, I couldn't *use* it myself. I had no badge. I had no warrant. I had, in the eyes of every law in every country, no right to so much as exist — I was a name that isn't a name, a man who lives in the seams precisely so the daylight can't find him. All I had was the proof. And proof is useless until it goes into the hand of someone who is *allowed* to bring the hammer down.

Which meant that after a night of building the most beautiful thing I'd ever made, I now had to find a human being inside the machinery of the law — a prosecutor, someone with the authority and the spine to take my sealed truth, look at it, believe it, and act on it before the train left and the marker moved again and Clara ran out of the time she was spending so bravely.

One person. The right person. A prosecutor with reach across the borders these people hid behind, with the standing to move fast, with hands clean enough that nobody could claim the case was poisoned at the root.

Marchetti gave me the name. Commandant Thierry Marchetti, Clara's old handler, the weary hard man who needed my reach and gave me his authority and whom I trusted exactly as far as the next reversal. He came to me the way he always did, through a channel that wasn't supposed to exist, on a line that would be gone by sunrise. He gave me a name and a number and the closest thing to warmth I'd ever heard from him.

"This one," Marchetti said, "is the one. Untouchable. Twenty years putting away men who thought they were too big to fall. He's the only prosecutor in the region with the standing and the stomach to take something this size and run with it before the politics can smother it. If your case is as clean as you say, he is the door it walks through. There is no other door."

"You trust him?"

A silence on the line that I should have paid more attention to. "I trust no one," Marchetti said. "But him I trust more than most."

I sat with the name a moment. The screens waited. The servers hummed. The whole plan, the slow beautiful right-way plan, the redemption I'd promised Ifeoma and the dead and the woman bleeding evidence into the dark — all of it now balanced on this single human being. One man, who could end this lawfully. One door.

I wanted to be sure. Of course I wanted to be sure. Old habits run deep; in the seams a man never puts his weight on a thing he hasn't tested in the dark first. So before I put the most important truth of my life into a stranger's hands, I did the one thing the system was best at, the thing it had done with the money and the routes and the twelve faces. I asked it, quietly, to seal *this* man too. To gather the threads of him — wherever they ran, however far down into the hidden places. To press its impossible seal into his life and show me, beyond any doubt, who he really was.

It gathered all night. By morning it laid the answer at my feet the way it always did — patient, bloodless, certain.

The one prosecutor who could end this was already on PHANTOM MERCY's payroll.

He had just found the wire.
