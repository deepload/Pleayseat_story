# Chapter 36: The Ministry

Forty minutes.

I have heard people say that in a crisis time slows down. It's a lie, or at least it has never once been true for me. In a crisis time does the opposite. It runs. It runs the way a stolen car runs, with someone else's foot on the floor and no plan for the corner, and all you can do is hold the wheel and pray the road is straight a little longer than your luck.

Forty minutes, and a building full of men with guns, and a woman under it holding a chain together with her bare hands. And against all that — three of us in a rotten flat above a harbour. Basti with his tools. Yuri with his phone. Me with a machine in the corner that knew the truth and couldn't fire a shot.

Three of us was not enough. I had known it for an hour and refused to know it, the way you refuse to know a thing right up until the moment the refusing becomes the danger. Three of us could reach the building. Three of us could maybe even reach her. But three of us could not lock a door behind a network that reached into four countries and twelve governments and ended in a hand so protected that its owner had probably never in his life felt afraid. Three of us could save a body and lose a war.

I needed weight. Real weight. The kind that wears a uniform and carries a flag and can make a phone ring in the right office at the wrong hour of the night and have someone answer *yes, sir* instead of *who is this.*

I needed a state.

And the terrible joke of my life — the joke I had been telling for two weeks without laughing — was that I had one. Almost. I had a man who *was* a state, in the small and weary way one tired man can be, and he was already on the phone, and I could hear his voice coming thin and cold out of Yuri's hand like wind under a door.

"Give it to me," I said, and took the phone.

---

"Commandant."

"You said dawn." Marchetti's voice was the voice of a man who has been woken into the exact nightmare he warned everyone about and is too professional to say *I told you so.* "Three hours ago you said dawn. My people are spread across this city on the strength of that word. Half of them aren't in position until five."

"Dawn's dead," I said. "They moved it. The convoy rolls in—" I looked at the screen, at the cold falling number, and I made myself say it without the catch in it. "Forty minutes. Less now. The children go on it. Clara goes with the building."

Silence. Not the silence of a man who doesn't believe you. The worse one — the silence of a man who believes you completely and is doing the arithmetic and not liking the answer.

"You can't make it," he said finally. "Not lawful. Not in forty minutes. A lawful entry takes a magistrate, a warrant, a chain of authorisation that—"

"I'm not asking you for lawful," I said. "I'm asking you to be standing at the lawful door when I open it from the inside. I'm going in. I'm going in soft if I can and hard if I can't, but I'm going in, and somebody who can make arrests stick needs to be on the other side of the wall when I do, or this whole thing — her whole thing, everything she bled for — washes out in a courtroom in two years and nobody pays and the children get filed and forgotten."

I heard him breathe. I heard, behind him, the particular hush of a serious building at a serious hour — a man's voice somewhere distant, a door, the flat hum of machines that never sleep. He wasn't at home. He was somewhere with a flag on it. He'd gone in to the office to manage the night he'd been dreading, and that meant he was close to phones I couldn't dream of reaching, close to the weight I didn't have.

"You don't understand what you're asking," he said, and his voice had changed. It had gone careful. The careful of a man stepping onto ice he already knows is thin. "If I move tonight — if I put my hand on this tonight — I have to wake people. Above me. People who do not get woken for a Belgian ghost and a missing cryptographer and a building you found in a memory."

"Then wake them."

"I'm trying to tell you what that *costs,*" he said. "You have built something extraordinary. I have watched you do it for two weeks with my mouth open, half of it terrified, the other half wishing my whole service worked the way your one mad head works. But the thing you built has no flag. No country owns it. No minister can point to it and say *that is ours, we did that, we are clean.* And the men I'd have to wake tonight — they don't get out of bed for a thing nobody owns. They get out of bed for a thing they can hold up afterward."

"Clara isn't a thing you hold up afterward," I said, and my voice cracked on her name despite me, despite everything, the way it always did. "She's a woman in a basement. The children aren't a press release. They're forty-seven kids in the back of a truck that's about to drive."

"I know that." Quiet. Almost gentle. The first gentle thing he'd said to me in two weeks, and it frightened me more than the cold. "I have known that longer than you have, my friend. Clara was mine before she was yours. I sent her into the cold. I carry that. You think I don't want this? I want this the way you want air." A pause. "But wanting a thing and being *allowed* a thing are two different countries, and I have spent my whole life living on the border between them."

---

Here is something I have learned, late, at great cost, in rotten rooms in cities I shouldn't have been in.

A state is not a person. I knew that, the way you know a fact. But that night I *felt* it, and feeling a thing is a different and more expensive kind of knowing. A state does not love anyone. It cannot. It is too big to love and too old to hurry. It is a vast cold animal that moves only when it is fed, and it eats one thing and one thing only, and that thing is not your grief and not your forty minutes and not the children in the truck.

It eats *proof.*

I had spent the whole war learning that. Clara had taught it to me before I ever knew her name for it — that in a world where everyone lies, the only real weapon left is the power to prove. She had bled for it. She had let herself be carried down into the dark holding nothing but that — the truth, sealed, clean, sworn, the kind of truth that survives daylight and lawyers and time, the kind of truth that even a vast cold animal has to swallow because it cannot pretend it isn't real.

And I had it. That was the thing. I sat in that blue room with the harbour breathing through the glass and I realised that I, of all the broken and unflagged men in the world, was sitting on the one thing the animal would get out of bed for. Everything Clara had built. Everything she'd held the chain together to keep clean. The twelve names. The two thousand eight hundred and forty-seven movements of dirty money dressed as mercy. The six relay points. The whole shape of the beast, proven, sealed, unbreakable — sitting in the corner of a flat above a harbour, in the keeping of a man with no country and a clock running out.

It was the most valuable thing on earth, that night, in that city. And I understood, with a cold that started in my chest and ran down into my hands, that Marchetti understood it too. That every careful word he'd just spoken had been walking me, gently, weary, toward this.

"You want the proof," I said.

The line was very quiet.

"Say it straight, Commandant," I said. "We're past three in the morning. We don't have the minutes for the careful version. You want me to give you what she built."

"I want," he said slowly, "to be able to walk into a room full of frightened powerful men in the next twenty minutes and put something on the table that makes them act instead of cover themselves. That is the only language that moves them. Not her name. Not your love story. Not forty-seven children, God forgive us all, because children alone have never once in history made a ministry move fast. They move for *evidence.* For the thing that, once they've seen it, they cannot un-see, cannot deny they saw, cannot survive having ignored." A breath. "Give me that, and I can wake the right hand. I can make the call that puts real weight behind your door. I can turn three of you in a flat into a sanctioned operation with the whole arm of a state behind it before that convoy clears the gate."

"Then I'll bring it," I said. "I'll bring all of it. After. When she's out. When the children are off that truck. I'll walk into your ministry with the whole thing in my two hands and you can build a hundred prosecutions on it. But I go now. We sort the paperwork while I'm—"

"No."

One word. It dropped into the room like a stone into a well, and I waited for the sound of it hitting the bottom and the sound never came, because there was no bottom.

"No," he said again, softer, and I heard in it the whole weariness of his whole life, every compromise, every door he'd stood at on the wrong side of *allowed.* "You don't understand the animal yet. I do. It does not move on a promise. It has been promised things by better men than you and me for three hundred years and it has learned. It will not wake the right hand on the strength of *I'll bring it after.* It needs to hold the thing first. In its own cold fingers. Before it spends a single soldier, a single phone call, a single ounce of the protection you need to put a wall behind that door."

"You're telling me to hand it over *before.*"

"I'm telling you what it costs," he said. "I'm telling you the price. I didn't write it. I hate it more than you can possibly hate it tonight, standing where I'm standing, knowing what I know about who's protected at the top of that chain. But I'm the one who has to carry it across to you, and I won't lie to you, not you, not tonight. You wanted a state. This is what a state is. This is the toll on the bridge."

---

I looked up.

Basti had stopped packing. Yuri had lowered his hood, just slightly, just enough to watch my face the way he'd watched it an hour ago when the night first lurched. They couldn't hear the other end. They didn't need to. They could read the toll off me, the way Clara could always read a sentence off my body before I'd finished saying it.

And here is what went through me, in the long cold half-second before I could speak, and I'm going to tell you all of it, because I've stopped being able to afford the flattering edit.

The proof was *her.* Not her body — her body was under a building, breathing, holding a chain. But the proof was her *self.* It was the thing she had become, the thing she'd traded her freedom to make and keep clean. To hand it over before she was out — to spend her life's work, the sealed truth she was still holding together with her hands at this exact moment, before I'd put a single hand on her — was to do a thing I had no right to do. It was to gamble the one weapon she'd given her freedom to forge, on the word of a tired man and the appetite of a cold animal, while she was still in the dark with no way of knowing I'd done it.

And the dirty road was right there again. I could feel it. I always could. Don't hand it over. Lie. Promise the animal everything and give it nothing, go in hard and alone and let the proof stay mine, my leverage, my insurance, my warm familiar knife in the dark. The old me would have done it without a heartbeat's hesitation. The old me had ruined every good thing he ever touched by keeping the knife.

But the proof in daylight was the only thing that made any of this *count.* Save her body, lose the war, and in two years the protected hand would still be reaching across borders and there'd be another building, another basement, another forty-seven, and Clara — *Clara* — would never forgive me for buying her body with the truth she'd bled to keep clean. I knew that the way I knew her breathing in the dark. She would rather die holding the chain than be carried out over its corpse.

I thought of her face. The exacting, brave, self-destructive face that said *someone has to protect the children.* And I knew what she would tell me to do, and I hated her a little, that night, for being right.

"How fast," I said into the phone. My voice was steady. I will never understand why it was steady. "If I give it to you now. How fast can you put a wall behind my door."

I heard him let out a breath he'd been holding for the length of his whole career.

"The moment it's in my hands," Marchetti said, "I make three calls. Inside ten minutes I can have authorisation. Inside twenty I can have the weight — real weight, the arm of the state, sanctioned, lawful, unbreakable — standing where you need it. You'll have your wall. You'll have your courtroom. You'll have the thing she bled for, surviving daylight." A pause, and then the gentleness again, the worst of all his weapons. "But not on a promise. Not after. Now. First. You give me the proof, and *then* I give you the rescue."

The harbour breathed through the cracked glass. Somewhere north, in the dead part of the city, a truck was idling in a hospital yard with its lights off and its doors open and the cold engine warming. The number on the screen fell, and fell, and did not care about my soul, and did not care about her self, and did not care about the toll on the bridge.

I closed my eyes. I saw DEFCON, August, the desert heat, the first time I ever heard her laugh. I saw the dead drop opening on the date only the two of us knew. I saw her hands, holding the chain.

And I understood, finally, what the ally had actually offered me. Not help. A trade. The kind that costs you the one thing you swore to protect, on the gamble that protecting the other thing is worth it.

The state had come to save her.

Their help came with one condition: hand over Clara's proof first — before the rescue.
