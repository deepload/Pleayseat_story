# Chapter 36: The Ministry

Forty minutes.

They say time slows down in a crisis. It doesn't. Not for me. In a crisis, time runs. It runs like a stolen car with someone else's foot on the gas and no plan for the turn ahead. You hold the wheel. You pray the road stays straight a little longer than your luck does.

I'd spent my whole life believing I knew what a clock looked like when it bolted. That night, I felt it in the bones.

Forty minutes. A building full of men with guns. A woman underneath it, holding a chain together with her bare hands.

And against all that — the three of us. In a rotten flat above a harbour. Basti with his tools spread across the table in the sick yellow light of a screen. Yuri with his burner phone, bought for cash under names that had never existed. Me with a coffee that had been cold for hours. And in the corner, a machine that knew the truth and couldn't fire a single shot.

Three wasn't enough. I'd known it for an hour. I'd refused to know it.

We could reach the building. Maybe reach her. But the network stretched across four countries. Governments protected it. At the far end, a hand that had probably never once felt afraid. We could save a body. We couldn't lock that door.

I needed weight. Real weight. The kind that wears a uniform and carries a flag. The kind that can make a phone ring in the right office at the wrong hour of the night and get "yes, sir" instead of "who's calling."

My whole life, I'd lived on the other side of that weight. In the gaps. In the unmarked corners of the network, the forums with no signs, where people like me crossed paths in silence behind handles and never saw each other's real faces. You learn fast down there: the state is not your friend. It's the wall you run into.

That night, for the first time, I needed the wall.

I needed a state.

The joke of my life was that I had one. Almost. A man who *was* a state, in his tired and modest way. He was already on the line. I could hear his voice coming thin and cold out of Yuri's hand, like a draft under a door.

"Give it to me," I said.

I took the phone.

---

"Commandant."

"You said dawn."

Marchetti's voice was the voice of a man dragged awake into the exact nightmare he'd warned everyone about. Too professional to say *I told you so.*

"Three hours ago you said dawn. My people are deployed across this entire city on the strength of that word. Half of them won't be in position until five."

"Dawn's dead," I said. "They moved it. The convoy leaves in—"

I looked at the screen. The cold number dropping. I made myself say it without breaking.

"Forty minutes. Less now. The children are already loading. Clara goes with the building."

Silence. Not the silence of a man who doesn't believe you. The worse kind. The silence of a man who believes you completely, is doing the math, and doesn't like the answer.

"You won't make it," he said at last. "Not legally. Not in forty minutes. A lawful entry takes a magistrate, a warrant, a chain of authorisations that—"

"I'm not asking you for lawful," I said. "I'm asking you to be standing at the lawful door when I open it from the inside. I'm going in. Soft if I can, hard if I have to. But I'm going. And there needs to be someone on the other side who can make the arrests stick. Otherwise this whole thing — hers, everything she bled for — dissolves in a courtroom two years from now. Nobody pays. The children get filed. Then forgotten."

I heard him breathe. And behind him, the particular hush of a serious building at a serious hour. A man's voice somewhere distant. A door. The flat hum of machines that never sleep.

The same sound as my servers. The same warm metal and hot dust I knew better than any room I'd ever slept in. He wasn't home. He was somewhere with a flag on the wall. He'd gone back to the office to manage the night he'd been dreading. Close to phones I couldn't dream of reaching. Close to the weight I didn't have.

"You don't understand what you're asking," he said.

His voice had changed. Careful. The careful of a man stepping onto ice he already knows is too thin.

"If I move tonight, I have to wake people. Above me. People you don't wake for a Belgian ghost, a missing cryptographer, and a building you found in a memory."

"Then wake them."

"I'm trying to tell you what it *costs.*" A pause. "You've built something extraordinary. I've been watching you do it for seven days, half terrified, half wishing my entire service ran the way your one mad mind does. But what you built has no flag. No country owns it. And the men I'd have to wake — they don't get out of bed for something nobody owns. They get out of bed for what they can hold up afterward."

"Clara isn't something you hold up," I said.

Her name broke my voice. Despite me. The way it always did.

"She's a woman in a basement. The children aren't a press release. They're forty-seven kids in the back of a truck that's about to move."

"I know that."

Quiet. Almost soft. The first soft thing he'd said to me in seven days. It frightened me more than the cold.

"I've known it longer than you have, my friend. Clara was mine before she was yours. I'm the one who sent her into the cold. I carry that. You think I don't want this? I want this the way you want air."

A pause.

"But wanting a thing and being *allowed* a thing are two different countries. I've spent my whole life on the border between them."

---

There's something I learned late, at great cost, in rotten rooms in cities I had no business being in.

A state is not a person. I knew that. The way you know a fact. That night, I *felt* it. And feeling something is a different and much more expensive kind of knowing.

A state doesn't love anyone. It can't. It moves for one thing and one thing only. Not a man's grief. Not his forty minutes. Not the children in the truck.

*Proof.*

I'd spent the whole war learning that. Clara had taught me before I even had a word for it. In a world where everyone lies, the only weapon left is the power to prove. She had bled for it. She'd let herself be taken down into the dark holding nothing else. The truth. Sealed. Clean. Sworn. The kind that survives daylight and lawyers and time. The kind even the state has to swallow, because it can't pretend it doesn't exist.

And I had it.

That was the thing. Sitting in that blue room with the harbour breathing through the glass, I understood it. Me, of all the broken and flagless men on earth, was sitting on the one thing that would make the animal get out of bed.

Everything Clara had built. Everything she'd held together, chain by chain, to keep it clean. The twelve names. The two thousand eight hundred and forty-seven movements of dirty money dressed as charity. The six relay points. The whole shape of the beast, proven, sealed, sitting in the corner of a flat above a harbour. Under the watch of a man with no country, and a countdown running out.

The most valuable thing on earth that night, in that city. The kind of thing that, on the black markets where secrets trade like weapons — and where I would have sold it without blinking, once — was worth more than the life of the man carrying it.

And I understood, with a cold that started in my chest and ran all the way down into my hands, that Marchetti understood it too. That every careful word had been walking me, gently, toward this exact moment.

"You want the proof," I said.

The line went very quiet.

"Say it straight, Commandant. We're past three in the morning. We don't have time for the careful version. You want me to hand over what she built."

"What I want," he said slowly, "is to walk into a room full of frightened, powerful men in the next twenty minutes and put something on the table that forces them to act instead of covering themselves. That's the only language that moves them. Not her name. Not your love story. Not forty-seven children — God forgive us, because children alone have never once in history made a ministry move fast. Not once. They move for *proof.* For the thing that, once seen, can't be un-seen. Can't be denied. Can't be survived if ignored."

A breath.

"Give me that, and I wake the right hand. I make the call that puts real weight behind your door. I turn three men in a flat into a sanctioned operation with the full arm of a state behind it — before that convoy clears the gate."

"Then I'll bring it to you," I said. "All of it. After. When she's out. When the children are off that truck. I walk into your ministry with everything in my hands and you build a hundred prosecutions on it. But I go now. We sort the paperwork while I'm—"

"No."

One word. It dropped into the room like a stone into a well. I waited for the sound of it hitting bottom. It never came. Because there was no bottom.

"No," he said again. Softer.

I heard his whole life in it. Every compromise. Every door he'd stood at on the wrong side of *allowed.*

"You don't understand the animal yet. I do. It doesn't move on a promise. Better men than us have been promising it things for three hundred years. It's learned. It will not wake the right hand on 'I'll bring it after.' It needs to hold the thing first. In its own cold hands. Before it spends a single soldier, a single phone call, a single ounce of the protection you need."

"You're telling me to hand it over *before.*"

"I'm telling you what it costs. I'm telling you the price. I didn't write it. I hate it more than you can possibly hate it tonight, standing where I'm standing, knowing what I know about who's protected at the top of that chain. But I'm the one who has to carry this price across to you. And I won't lie to you. Not you. Not tonight. You wanted a state. This is what a state is. This is the toll on the bridge."

---

I looked up.

Basti had stopped moving. His hands were still on his tools. Yuri had lowered his hood. Just barely. Just enough to watch my face — the way he'd watched it an hour ago when the night first lurched — reading me for what the words wouldn't say.

They couldn't hear the other end of the line. They didn't need to. They could read the toll off my face. The way Clara could always read a sentence off my body before I finished saying it.

And here is what went through me, in the long cold half-second before I could speak. All of it. Nothing flattering cut out, because I couldn't afford the flattering version anymore.

The proof was *her.* Not her body — her body was under a building right now, breathing, holding a chain. The proof was her *self.* What she'd become. What she'd traded her freedom to make and keep clean. To hand it over before she was out — to spend the work of her entire life before I'd put a single hand on her — was something I had no right to do. It was gambling the one weapon she'd forged with her freedom on the word of a tired man and the appetite of a cold machine. While she was still in the dark, with no way of knowing I'd done it.

The old road was right there. I could feel it. I could always feel it. The voice of the old world, of the nights without flags. Whispering in the language I'd grown up in: *don't hand it over. Lie. Promise the animal everything and give it nothing. Go in hard and alone. Keep the proof. Your leverage. Your insurance. Your knife, warm and familiar in the dark.*

The old me would have done it without a second thought. The old me had wrecked every good thing he ever touched by keeping the knife.

But the proof in daylight was the only thing that made any of this *count.* Save her body, lose the war — and two years from now the protected hand would still be reaching across borders. Another building. Another basement. Forty-seven more. And Clara — *Clara* — would never forgive me for buying her body with the truth she'd bled to keep clean.

I knew that the way I knew her breathing in the dark. She would rather die holding the chain than be carried out over its corpse.

I thought of her face. The demanding, brave, borderline-suicidal face that said: *someone has to protect the children.* And I knew what she would tell me to do. And I hated her a little, that night, for being right.

"How long," I said into the phone.

My voice was steady. I will never understand why it was steady.

"If I give it to you now. How long to put a wall behind my door."

I heard him release a breath he'd been holding for the length of his entire career.

"The moment it's in my hands," Marchetti said, "I make three calls. Ten minutes for authorisation. Twenty minutes for the weight — the real weight, the arm of the state, sanctioned, lawful, unbreakable — posted where you need it. You'll have your wall. You'll have your courtroom. You'll have the thing she bled for, surviving daylight."

A pause. And then the softness again. The worst weapon he had.

"But not on a promise. Not after. Now. First. You give me the proof, and *then* I give you the rescue."

The harbour breathed through the cracked glass. Somewhere near the old port, in the dead part of the city, a truck idled in the courtyard of an abandoned hospital. Lights off. Doors open. Cold engine warming up.

The number on the screen fell. And kept falling. It didn't care about my soul. It didn't care about hers. It didn't care about the toll on the bridge.

I closed my eyes.

I saw DEFCON. That vast gathering where thousands of people like us take over a casino hotel in Las Vegas for a week every August. The desert heat outside. The frozen air-conditioning inside. Crooked badges. False names. Stages where you blow up the world's machines on giant screens for an audience that lives in the dark the other fifty-one weeks of the year.

I saw a crooked badge. August. The first time I heard her laugh in a hall packed with people who, like me, spent the rest of the year alone. I saw the dead drop opening on the date that only the two of us knew existed. I saw her hands, holding the chain.

And I understood, finally, what the ally had actually offered me. Not help. A trade. The kind that costs you the one thing you swore to protect, on the bet that protecting the other thing is worth it.

The state had come to save her.

But first it wanted Clara's proof in its hands.

I opened my mouth to answer. And that was when Basti's screen lit up red, and every number on it stopped.
