# Chapter 6: The Genome

There is a particular kind of cold that has nothing to do with weather.

I felt it the moment the screen told me what I already knew and didn't want to. The enemy's fingerprint — the small, secret way a person leaves themselves behind in everything they make, like the slant of a signature or the way someone always burns the toast — matched something I had built. Years ago. In a life I had buried under a different name, a different city, a different idea of who I was allowed to love.

I sat very still in the dark of the Brussels apartment. Rain on the glass. The radiator ticking like a slow heart. Three coffees deep into a night that had no bottom.

*You made this,* the screen seemed to say. *You taught them how.*

I wanted to throw the laptop across the room. I wanted to call Clara and tell her I was sorry, that whatever had taken her was partly mine, that the same hands now trying to save her had once, somewhere, helped sharpen the knife. But Clara was the one I couldn't call. Clara was the reason I was here, not sleeping, not eating, building a thing that could find a heartbeat in the silence of a whole country.

So instead I did the only thing that has ever quieted me. I worked.

---

Let me tell you what I am, because by now you've earned it.

I find family resemblances in evil.

That sounds like a riddle. It isn't. People think cruelty is anonymous — that the men who take children and hide behind charity convoys are faceless, interchangeable, a fog. They are not. Every one of them has habits. A way of moving money that's a little too clever. A favourite hour of the night. A rhythm. A tell. The same way you'd know your brother's footsteps on the stairs in a house full of strangers, the thing I built knows the footsteps of the people who hurt the world.

I taught it to look for what I call the family resemblance. Not faces. Not names. The *manner* of a thing. The way the same hand keeps showing up in crimes that, on paper, have nothing to do with each other. A robbery in Geneva. A vanished shipment in Marseille. A donation that goes in clean and comes out dirty in three countries. To anyone else: noise. Coincidence. Bad luck spread across a map.

To the system, it's a face in a crowd. The same face, over and over, wearing different coats.

Clara called it *the genome.* The last thing she ever said to me, in a message I have read so many times the words have worn smooth: *Find the genome. Save the children.* She meant: don't chase the men. Chase the resemblance. Find the thing they all have in common, buried under the lies, and you find the body the limbs belong to.

She always was smarter than me. That was half of why I loved her, and the other half I'm still not brave enough to say out loud.

---

By two in the morning I had stopped shaking and started hunting.

I fed it everything. Clara's research — the careful, furious notebook of a woman who had given up sleep to protect children she'd never meet. The vanished convoys. The donations that breathed in clean and out dirty. The little crimes nobody connected, scattered across years and borders like ash from one fire.

And I told it: *find me the resemblance. Find me the hand.*

I want to describe what that's like, watching it work, but I won't bore you with the machine. I'll tell you what it *felt* like instead, because that's the only honest way.

It felt like standing in a vast dark room full of strangers, and slowly, one by one, the lights coming up — and realising that half the people you thought were strangers are wearing the same ring.

The system pulled threads. A theft here. A lie there. A pattern in the way money moved on the third Tuesday of certain months. And it began to braid them. Not guesses — connections, each one with its own little seal of proof, the way Clara had taught me to demand, because she said evidence is the only thing in this world that can't be charmed or bought or seduced into changing its story. *Anyone can lie to you,* she'd said, the first night, in a bar in Las Vegas that smelled of spilled bourbon and possibility. *Proof can't. Build me something that only believes proof.*

So I did. I built her a thing that only believes proof. And now it was showing me proof I would have given a great deal not to see.

The resemblances clustered. Out of all that noise, one shape rose — clean and undeniable, like a face surfacing in water. One hand. One *maker*. Behind the convoys, behind the laundered mercy, behind the whole rotten apparatus that called itself charity, there was a single craftsman. An architect of the way it all hid.

And his work had my fingerprints under it.

---

I need you to understand who he is, because the resemblance wasn't an accident.

Years ago, in the life I buried, I worked with someone. I won't dress it up. We broke things together. We were young and gifted and convinced the rules were for people without our gifts. He was the best I ever saw — better than me at the one thing I was supposed to be best at. Where I was loud and reckless and in love with the applause, he was silent, precise, surgical. We called him the Surgeon, and he earned it. He could open anything and leave no scar. He could take a thing apart and the thing wouldn't know it had been touched until it was far too late.

We were close, in the way men are close when they share a secret that could ruin them both. I taught him some of what I knew. He taught me more than I'll admit. Then I walked away from that life — clumsily, guiltily, half because I'd fallen for someone who deserved a better man and half because I couldn't look at what we'd become. He stayed. He got quieter. He got worse.

I had not thought about him in years. I had made myself not think about him.

And now his hand was all over the thing that had taken Clara.

The family resemblance the system found in the heart of PHANTOM MERCY — the careful, scarless, surgical way the whole network hid itself from the world — it was *his.* I would know that hand in my sleep. I had helped shape it. Every clean cut, every invisible door, every trick of making a monstrous thing look like a kindness — I could see the lessons in it. Some of them were mine.

It is one thing to fight a stranger. It is another to fight your own reflection wearing the face of the man you'll have to kill to get her back.

I sat with that. The rain kept coming. Somewhere four hours south and a thousand miles away, Clara was in a room I couldn't see, and the man helping to hold her there was a man I had once called a kind of brother.

I picked up my phone. I put it down. I picked it up again.

There is a way I could have done this. The old way. The fast, dirty, satisfying way — to reach across the wire and burn him, hurt his world the way he'd hurt mine, take what I wanted by force and let the proof rot. Every cell in my body wanted it. He had touched the one real thing in my life and I wanted to make him feel it.

But Clara built me to believe proof. And I knew — God, I knew — that the second I did it the dirty way, I'd lose the only thing that could actually bring her home: the clean, undeniable, court-proof chain of who did what. Burn him, and I'd feel better for an hour and lose her forever. Prove him, and maybe, maybe, I'd get her back and put him in a cage and let the children come home to mothers who'd stopped sleeping.

I put the phone down a third time. I left it down.

It was the hardest thing I did that night, and nobody will ever give me a medal for it, and it nearly killed me.

*Do it right,* I told myself, in her voice, because I'd lost my own. *Do it right or don't do it at all.*

---

So I went back to the work, because the work doesn't ask you to be a good man. It only asks you to be a careful one.

Here is the thing about a family resemblance: once you've found one, the system can't stop seeing it. It's like learning a new word — suddenly it's everywhere, in every conversation, jumping out of pages you've read a hundred times. I had taught it the Surgeon's hand. Now it went back through everything, every thread, every connection, looking for that same hand again. Looking for the brother of the fingerprint.

And it found one.

A second resemblance. Faint, almost shy, hiding under the noise the way a held breath hides under a song. The same scarless habits. The same surgical care. Not the Surgeon — I knew his hand and this wasn't quite it — but *kin* to it. Someone trained the same way. Someone who'd learned at the same table. A second craftsman, woven into the network's hidden machinery, helping it stay invisible, helping it hide.

My pulse picked up. Two hands. Two makers behind PHANTOM MERCY. If I could name them both, I could prove the spine of the whole thing. I could give Clara's evidence a face and a court could give it a sentence and the children could give it a future.

I leaned in. *Show me,* I told it. *Show me the second one.*

I want to tell you I was calm. I was not calm. I was a man holding a thread that might lead to the room where they kept the woman he loved, and the thread was getting warmer in my hand.

The system pulled the second resemblance closer. Cross-checked it. Sealed it with proof, the way she'd taught me, so that what came back couldn't lie even if I wanted it to.

Two signatures, it told me. Two hands, both real, both woven through the dark of PHANTOM MERCY. The first was the Surgeon — my buried brother, my reflection, my fault.

And the second one —

The second one I knew.

Not from the old life. From *this* one. From the life I'd built afterward, the clean one, the one full of people I'd chosen instead of inherited. The footsteps on the stairs in a house full of strangers — I knew them. I knew them the way you know the voice of someone who has saved you, more than once, at three in the morning, when nobody else would pick up.

The second signature didn't belong to an enemy.

It belonged to my family.

Two signatures matched — and the second one was alive inside his own family of friends.
