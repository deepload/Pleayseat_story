# Chapter 6: The Genome

There is a cold that has nothing to do with the weather.

I felt it the second the screen confirmed what I already knew. What I didn't want to know.

The enemy's fingerprint. That tiny way everyone betrays themselves in what they build. Like the slant of a signature. Like a man who always burns his toast.

It matched something I had made. Years ago. In a life buried under a different handle, a different city, a different idea of who I was allowed to love.

I stopped moving.

The Brussels apartment was dark. The only light was the screen — that blue that bleaches your face and turns you into a ghost. Rain scratched at the window. The radiator clicked like a tired heart. Under the table, the servers hummed in their dry heat.

Three coffees deep into the night. The last one had gone cold. I drank it anyway.

*You built this,* the screen said. *You taught them how.*

I wanted to throw the laptop against the wall. Call Clara. Tell her I was sorry. That what had taken her was partly mine. That the hands trying to save her had once helped sharpen the knife.

But Clara was the one person I couldn't call. Clara was the reason for all of it. For these nights without sleep, without food, building something that could find a heartbeat in the silence of an entire country.

So I did the only thing that has ever quieted me.

I worked.

---

Let me say who I am. At least in the dark, at least quietly.

I live in the seams. People like me don't exist on the street. We exist in the margins. Forums that change their address every month. Hidden channels where we speak in handles, because a name is a leash.

Once a year, though, we gather for real. Thousands of strangers in a big casino hotel in the desert. Badges with fake names. Bright, restless eyes. We sit in overheated rooms and watch one of our own walk up on stage and show the world the flaw they found in a lock the world thought unbreakable.

We applaud courage there, not harm. We drink too much. We barely sleep. It is the only family where I have ever recognized myself.

We have our legends. Our debts. Our codes of honor that nobody wrote down. And one real craft connects us all, underneath the posturing and the handles: we recognize a hand.

I find family resemblances in evil.

That sounds like a riddle. It isn't.

People think cruelty is anonymous. That the men who take children and hide behind charity convoys are faceless. Interchangeable. A fog.

They are wrong. Every one of them has habits. A way of moving money that's a little too slick. A favorite hour of the night. A rhythm. A tell.

You know your brother's footsteps on the stairs in a house full of strangers. The thing I built knows the footsteps of people who do harm in the world.

I taught it to look for the family resemblance. Not faces. Not names. The *manner*. The way the same hand keeps showing up in crimes that, on paper, have nothing to do with each other.

A robbery in Geneva. A vanished shipment in Marseille. A donation that goes in clean and comes out dirty in three countries, laundered through those shadow markets where money loses its memory.

To anyone else: noise. Coincidence. Bad luck scattered across a map.

To the system: a face in the crowd. The same face, again and again, wearing different coats.

Clara called it *the genome.* The last thing she said to me. A message I've read so many times the words are worn smooth: *Find the genome. Save the children.*

She meant: don't chase the men. Chase the resemblance. Find what they all have in common, buried under the lies, and you find the body the limbs belong to.

She was always smarter than me. That was half the reason I loved her. The other half I still don't have the courage to say.

---

By two in the morning I had stopped shaking. I had started hunting.

I gave it everything. Clara's research — the furious, meticulous notebook of a woman who had given up sleep for children she would never meet. The vanished convoys. The donations that went in clean and came out dirty. The small crimes nobody connected, scattered across the years like ash from a single fire.

And everything I had gathered myself. From haunting the dark web corners where this network thought it left its traces out of reach. Old forgotten files. Fragments from leaks. Scraps that strangers had thrown into the void thinking no one was reading.

Someone is always reading.

And I told it: *find me the resemblance. Find me the hand.*

I could try to describe what it's like to watch it work. But the machine says nothing true. What's true is what it *felt* like. And that's the only thing I want to keep.

It felt like standing in a vast dark room full of strangers. And watching the lights come on, one by one, slowly. And realizing that half those strangers are wearing the same ring.

The system pulled threads. A theft here. A lie there. A pattern in the way money moved on the third Tuesday of certain months. And it started braiding them.

Not guesses. Connections. Each one sealed with its own proof, the way Clara had taught me. Because she said proof is the only thing in the world you cannot charm, cannot buy, cannot seduce into changing its story.

*Anyone can lie to you,* she had said, the first night, in a bar in Las Vegas that smelled of spilled bourbon and possibility. *Proof can't. Build me something that only believes proof.*

So I did. I built her something that only believes proof.

And now it was showing me proof I would have given anything not to see.

The resemblances gathered. Out of all that noise, a shape emerged. Sharp. Undeniable. Like a face rising to the surface of water.

One hand. One *maker*. Behind the convoys, behind the laundered charity, behind the whole rotten apparatus that called itself benevolence, there was a single craftsman. The architect of the way it all hid.

And his work had my fingerprints underneath it.

---

I have to say who he is. Because the resemblance was no accident.

Years ago, in the buried life, I worked with someone. I won't dress it up. We broke locks together. We opened what others believed was sealed. We were young, gifted, convinced the rules were made for people without the touch.

He was the best I ever saw. Better than me at the one thing I was supposed to be best at.

I was loud. Reckless. In love with the applause, with those convention nights where you walk onstage in front of thousands of strangers to show off your latest exploit until the room is on its feet.

He was silent. Precise. Surgical. He never wanted an audience.

We called him the Surgeon. He had earned it. He could open anything without leaving a scar. Take something apart and that thing would not know it had been touched. Until it was always too late.

We were close. The way two men are close when they share a secret that could ruin them both. A strange brotherhood. Tighter than blood, because one word from either of you can send the other to a cage for twenty years.

I taught him part of what I knew. He taught me more than I'll ever admit.

Then I left that life. Clumsily. With guilt. Half because I had fallen for a woman who deserved a better man. Half because I couldn't stand looking at what we had become.

I cut the last wire myself. Erased the handle. Burned the bridges. Disappeared.

He stayed. He got quieter. He got worse.

I hadn't thought about him in years. I had forced myself not to.

And now his hand was everywhere on the thing that had taken Clara.

The family resemblance at the heart of PHANTOM MERCY — that meticulous, scarless, surgical way the entire network concealed itself from the world — it was *his.*

I would know that hand in my sleep. I had helped shape it. Every clean cut. Every invisible door. Every sleight of hand that made a monster look like a kindness. I could see the lessons inside. Some of them were mine.

It is one thing to fight a stranger. It is another to fight your own reflection on the face of the man you will have to destroy to get her back.

I stayed with that. The rain still fell. Somewhere, nearly a thousand kilometres to the south, Clara was in a room I couldn't see. And the man helping to keep her there was a man I had once called something like a brother.

I picked up my phone. Put it down. Picked it up again.

There was another way. The old way. The fast, dirty, satisfying way. Reach across the wire and burn him. Tear through his world the way he had torn through mine. Take what I wanted by force and let the proof rot.

Find his door, any door, and walk back in the way I used to. Kicking it in. Like I owned the place.

Every cell in my body wanted it. He had touched the only real thing in my life. I wanted him to feel that.

But Clara had built me to believe the proof. And I knew — God, I knew. The second I went dirty, I would lose the only thing capable of truly bringing her home. The clean, undeniable, court-ready chain of who had done what.

Burn him, and I'd feel better for an hour. And I'd lose her forever.

Prove him, and maybe. Maybe I'd get her back. Put him in a cage. Let the children come home to mothers who had stopped sleeping.

I put the phone down a third time. Left it there.

That was the hardest thing I did that night. Nobody will give me a medal. And it almost killed me.

*Do it right,* I told myself, in her voice, because I had lost my own. *Do it right or don't do it at all.*

---

So I went back to the work. Because the work doesn't ask a man to be good. It only asks him to be careful.

Here is the thing about a family resemblance. Once you've found one, the system can't stop seeing it. It's like learning a new word. Suddenly it's everywhere. In every conversation. On pages you've read a hundred times.

I had taught it the Surgeon's hand. Now it went back through everything. Every thread. Every connection. Searching for that same hand again. For the sibling of the fingerprint.

And it found one.

A second resemblance. Faint. Almost shy. Hidden under the noise like a held breath under a song. The same scarless habits. The same surgical care.

Not the Surgeon. I knew his hand, and this wasn't quite it. But a *relative*. Someone trained the same way. Someone who had learned at the same table, in the same blue light, at the same hours when the rest of the world sleeps. A second maker, woven into the network's hidden machinery. Helping it stay invisible.

My pulse jumped. Two hands. Two makers behind PHANTOM MERCY. If I could name them both, I could prove the spine of the whole operation. Give Clara's evidence a face. A court could give it a sentence. The children, a future.

I leaned toward the screen. Close enough that the blue burned my eyes.

*Show me,* I told it. *Show me the second one.*

I wanted to stay calm. I wasn't calm. I was a man holding a thread. A thread that might lead to the room where they were keeping the woman I loved. And the thread was getting warmer in my hand.

The system pulled the second resemblance closer. Cross-checked it. Sealed it with proof, the way she had taught me. So that what came back could not lie, even if I wanted it to.

Two signatures, it told me. Two hands. Both real. Both woven through the dark of PHANTOM MERCY.

The first was the Surgeon. My buried brother. My reflection. My fault.

And the second —

The second I knew.

Not from the old life. From *this* one. From the life I had built afterward. The clean one. The one filled with people I had chosen instead of inherited. My family of handles and voices in the dark. My operators scattered across three continents. The ones who still pick up when no one else does.

The footstep on the stairs in a house full of strangers — I recognized it. The way you recognize the voice of someone who has saved you. More than once. At three in the morning, when the rest of the world was offline.

The second signature didn't belong to an enemy.

It belonged to my family.

Two signatures matched — and the second one was alive, right at the heart of the family I had chosen for myself.
