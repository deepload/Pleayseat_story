# Chapter 5: The Mirror

I did not sleep. No one sleeps with a sealed file in his vault that he swears he never made, addressed to him, in handwriting he would know in the dark, on his deathbed, at the bottom of the sea.

I sat with it for a long time. I didn't open it. It sounds insane — I knew even then how insane it sounded — but I understood exactly what it meant. Clara had reached forward through time and put her hand on my shoulder. She had touched the one place in the whole world I thought was sealed — past every wall I'd ever built, past locks no one alive should have been able to pick — and she had left her fingerprints there to tell me one thing: *I knew you'd come this far.*

A man can carry that. A man can also be crushed by it. I felt both, at once, in the dark, the only light in the room the cold blue glow of the screens, with cold coffee turning to skin in the cup.

I didn't open it because I was afraid of what it would ask of me. And I was right to be afraid.

So instead I did the thing I always do when I'm afraid. I went looking for the man on the other side.

---

By dawn I had stopped being a person who missed someone and become a person who hunted. They're not the same animal. The first one cries in the shower. The second one doesn't blink for nine hours, forgets to eat, and goes down into the dark places of the network where people like me live — the hidden channels, the forums with no names, the rooms below the rooms — without ever once feeling the cold of it. Both of them were me, that week in Brussels, and they took turns at the wheel.

I made tea I didn't drink. I opened the windows to the grey Belgian sky because the room had started to smell like a man losing it — that stale electric smell of too many sleepless nights and a machine that never gets to rest. Down in the street a baker was raising his shutter, and the ordinary scrape of metal nearly broke me — the idea that somewhere people were still buttering bread while Clara was somewhere I couldn't see.

Behind me the servers hummed their low patient hum, the only steady heartbeat in the place. Then I sat down and asked the thing I had built to do the one thing it was best at.

I asked it to draw me a face.

---

It is the closest thing I have ever known to magic, and even now I can only feel it, never explain it.

You start with nothing — that was the truth of it. A network that hides children behind charity, that moves them inside the trucks that bring rice and blankets to the broken places of the world. *Phantom Mercy.* Clara's name for it. She had a gift for naming the thing that killed you so you couldn't look away from it.

Nothing was what I had. No name. No face. Out in the open world a man like this leaves no shadow at all — but down in the seams, in the darknet, on the channels where the trade whispers to itself when it thinks no one is awake, everything leaves a mark. Just damage — the shape of where they've been, like the dent a body leaves in a bed.

And then you let the system listen. Not to words. To the *way* things are done. Because this was the secret that took me twenty years and one broken heart to learn: every person who builds in the dark leaves a signature, the way a painter leaves brushstrokes, the way a grandmother's handwriting is hers and no one else's. You can change your handle a thousand times. You can burn every name you ever wore and put on a new one. You cannot change your hands.

So I fed it everything Clara had left me, and everything the dark would give up — the leaks, the dumps, the scraps that surface on forums that don't officially exist — and I let it sit in the silence and find the pattern in the brushstrokes. By the time the baker downstairs had sold his first baguette, the whole formless cloud of *Phantom Mercy* had begun to resolve. Like a photograph in a tray of developer, in those old films, where the grey water suddenly remembers it was a face.

A man. One man, at the heart of the dark machine. Not the boss — I knew that even then. There's always someone fatter and safer above the clever one. But the clever one. The builder. The hands.

I started to see him.

---

I have to be honest with myself about what I felt, because the whole rest of this is built on it.

I felt admiration.

God forgive me. I felt the warm, sick, professional admiration of one craftsman looking at another's work. He was *good.* The way the network breathed — patient, layered, beautiful in the way a trap is beautiful — was the work of someone who loved the craft the way I loved it. Who'd stayed up the way I'd stayed up, screen-blind at four in the morning. Who probably forgot to eat, too. Who lived, like me, in the seams of the world, where the only company is the hum of the machine and the glow of the screen on your face.

There is a loneliness in being very good at something almost no one understands. You spend your life in rooms nobody else can enter, building cathedrals nobody will ever see, and you start to hunger — really *hunger* — for one other person who could stand in the cathedral with you and say: *yes. I see what you did there. That was hard. You made it look easy.*

I had felt that hunger my whole life. And looking at the work of the man inside *Phantom Mercy*, I felt the obscene, terrible pull of recognition. Like calling to like. A handshake across a black river.

It frightened me more than the network did.

Because if he was that good — and he was — then he had not stumbled into hurting children. He had *engineered* it. With care. With pride. The same care I'd once put into the wrong things, in the years I never speak of, when I was younger and angrier and would break a thing just to prove I could, just to make some woman's eyes go wide. There was a name I wore back then, a handle half the scene still flinches at, that I'd buried with everything else.

Clara never knew that man. Or — she did. She knew exactly who I'd been. She'd run my whole history — every handle, every leak with my fingerprints on it, every door I'd ever quietly opened — before she ever let me buy her a drink, in a hotel bar in a desert city, three years and a lifetime ago. She knew what my hands had done. And she sat across from me anyway and said, *"You build now,"* like it was a fact and not a hope. Like she'd decided it for me.

That's the thing about being loved by someone who knows your worst. It isn't comfort. It's a sentence. You have to live up to the version of you they refused to stop seeing.

---

I gave him a name, because the system couldn't give me his real one and I couldn't keep calling him *the clever one* without going mad. In our world you don't get faces; you get handles, and the handle becomes the man. So I named him the way the scene names everyone — by the one thing he could not hide.

I called him the Surgeon.

It came to me unbidden, watching the way he cut. He didn't smash. He didn't take a sledgehammer to a wall the way amateurs do, the way the loud kids on the forums do, the way I had as a stupid kid. He opened things along their seams. He found the one stitch that, pulled, made the whole garment fall open, and he pulled it, clean, and stepped through, and left everything looking untouched behind him. No mess. No blood on the floor. No alarm tripped, no trace left, no kill switch ever reached for. Just a door that had quietly decided to be open.

That's a particular kind of person. That patience. That contempt for noise. I knew it because I'd spent years trying to become it and never quite had the stillness.

The Surgeon had the stillness.

And there was something else, something it took me a while to put words to, because it sat just under the skin of the work like a temperature. Cruelty. Not the hot kind — not the man who hurts you because he's angry, who you can almost forgive because at least he *feels* something. The cold kind. The kind that hurts you the way a tide goes out: without noticing you were there. Everything the Surgeon built treated the children the way a butcher treats meat — as logistics, as weight, as a number that has to get from one place to another without spoiling. There was no hate in it. That was the horror. Hate would have been human. This was *housekeeping.*

I have done bad things. I will not soften that for myself, because a confession with the worst parts left out is just bragging. I have hurt people for money and for pride and to make a girl's eyes go wide in a bar. I have rooted machines I had no business touching and walked away with things that weren't mine. But I never once stopped seeing them as people. The shame I carry is the shame of a man who knew, every time, exactly what he was doing to someone real. The Surgeon carried no shame at all, because to him there was no one there to be sorry to. That's the line. That's the whole line, between the man I'd been and the man across the river. Not the skill. The skill was identical. The line was who you let yourself stop seeing.

I sat back from the screen and my heart was going like I'd run up six flights, and I said out loud, to an empty apartment in a city that didn't know my real name:

"There you are."

And somewhere — I'm sure of it now, though I had no proof then — somewhere on the other side of the black river, in his own room, in front of his own cold tea, his own screens burning a hole in his own dark, the Surgeon felt me looking and went very, very still.

---

I called Yuri.

Yuri is in Kyiv. Yuri is the most paranoid human being I have ever loved, which is saying something, because I collect them. He answered on the first ring, on the encrypted line we keep that touches no network anyone can name, which meant he hadn't slept either, which meant the war was loud again that night and he was sitting in the dark listening to drones the way other people listen to rain.

"You look like death," he said. No video. He never does video. He just knows.

"I found a man," I said.

A pause. The particular Yuri pause, where he's deciding how worried to be. "Inside the thing that took her?"

"Inside it. The one who built it." I rubbed my face. "Yuri, he's — he's like me."

"Everyone you hunt is like you," Yuri said. "That's why you catch them. You're not a detective, Delta. You're a confession."

I laughed, because it was true and because if I didn't laugh I'd do something worse. Yuri's the only one who calls me what I am to my face. The others are gentler. Ifeoma prays for me down in Lagos, between keeping my whole infrastructure breathing with hands that never shake. Basti pours me a drink and tells me a filthy joke. Noa — Noa just goes quiet on the line and lets the silence say *I know, I know, I know.* But Yuri names it.

"Send me what you've got," he said. "I'll look for him in the noise. If he's that good, he's careful, and careful men leave a different kind of trace. They don't leave mistakes — they leave the *absence* of mistakes, and absence has a shape too. A hole in the dark where a man scrubbed himself out." A drone went over, somewhere in his window. He didn't flinch. "Delta. Listen to me."

"I'm listening."

"You sound like you want to admire him."

I didn't answer.

"Don't," Yuri said softly. "Admire him later, in court, in a transcript, when he is in a cage and the children are home. Admire him from the *outside.* The second you climb into his head to appreciate the artistry, you stop seeing the children. That's how he wins. Men like that — men like *you,* my friend, the old you, the one who used to sign his work — they make you fall in love with the cleverness so you forget the cruelty. Clara's whole work was about that. Don't make her wrong by proving it on yourself."

I closed my eyes. In a hotel bar three years ago Clara had said almost the same thing, with a glass of bad red wine in her hand and the desert dark outside, telling me about her work, telling me about *everything.* *"You can map any monster if you understand what they protect. But the moment you start protecting it too, you've joined them, and you'll never feel the door close behind you."*

"Yeah," I said to Yuri. "Yeah. I hear you."

"Eat something," he said, and hung up, because Yuri's love sounds like orders.

I sat with the dead line a moment. That's a thing about my family — the scattered, stolen, chosen family I've built across a dozen broken cities, the ones who live in the seams the way I do, who went dark to the rest of the world years ago and only ever surface for each other — they hang up the second the love has been delivered, like a parcel, like they're embarrassed to have signed for it. Ifeoma in Lagos does it. Basti in Marseille does it. Even Noa, who answers when no one else would, who has every reason in the world to let me ring forever and doesn't — even Noa hangs up first, always, so I won't hear whatever's in her voice if she stays on a second longer.

I'd dragged all of them, over the years, into rooms they shouldn't have been in. That was the thing eating me under everything else, that whole grey week. To find Clara I was going to have to light them up — all of them, one by one, the whole quiet web of handles and burner lines and people who don't exist — and ask them to walk toward the dark with me. And the man waiting in that dark, I was starting to understand, was not a stranger. He knew the trade I'd taught. He'd come up through the same forums, the same legends, maybe the same teachers. Which meant, somewhere down the line, he might know the family I'd taught it to.

I pushed the thought away. There is always a thought a man pushes away the night before he finds out it was the true one.

---

I didn't eat. Obviously.

I went back to the face.

Because Yuri was right, and because I'm a stubborn idiot, I'd decided to do the one thing that would either save me or undo me. If the Surgeon had a signature — and he did, every craftsman does — then I could match it. Compare his hands against the great library of hands, the museum of brushstrokes the dark keeps without meaning to — every leak, every dump, every old job that ever bled out onto the forums and got archived by someone who never forgets. Find his cousins, his teachers, the people he'd learned from and the people he'd burned. Build him a family tree of cruelty. Find out where a man learns to open children like envelopes.

I let the system do the comparing. It's the thing it's truly best at — not finding, but *recognising.* Holding two pieces of work side by side and saying, in its quiet way, *these came from the same room.* The same hands. The same school.

I leaned back. I let it run. Outside, Brussels woke up properly — trams, a dog, an argument in two languages, a church bell counting a number I lost track of. The screens kept their watch, the cursor blinking, the machine working through the dark library line by line. I thought about Clara's handwriting in my vault. I thought about the file I still hadn't opened. I thought, the way you do at the end of a long bad night, of nothing at all, just the hum of the machine and the grey light and the ache behind my eyes that had stopped being tiredness and become a kind of weather I lived in now.

And then the system found his cousin.

---

I knew it before I read it. I knew it the way a man knows the phone is about to ring. The room changed temperature. The hum changed pitch — or I imagined it did; I'll never be sure; in memory it always changes pitch.

The Surgeon's signature — that clean, patient, beautiful, monstrous way of opening a thing along its seam — had a match.

One match. Strong. The strongest the system had ever found, it told me, in its bloodless way, as if it were proud, as if it had brought me a bird and laid it at my feet.

I almost smiled. *A teacher,* I thought. *A mentor. The school he came from.* This was the thread Clara wanted me to pull. This was the seam. I leaned in to read whose hands had taught the Surgeon his terrible, elegant trade — whose handle sat at the root of it — leaned in like a man leaning over a well to see his own —

I read it twice.

The first time, the words didn't go in. They sat on the surface of me like oil on water and refused to mean anything, because what they meant could not be true and so my mind, kindly, gave me a second to not understand.

The second time, they went in.

The hands the Surgeon had learned from. The signature his work was a child of. The seam-cutting, door-opening, beautiful-terrible craft at the black heart of *Phantom Mercy* — the system had found its cousin, its source, its origin, and laid it at my feet like a gift.

It was mine.

The Surgeon's fingerprint matched something I had made myself, years ago, in a life I had buried.
