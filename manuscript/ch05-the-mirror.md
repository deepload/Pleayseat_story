# Chapter 5: The Mirror

I didn't sleep. You don't sleep with a thing like that sitting in your vault.

My vault. The one place no one alive knows how to open. The place I keep the things that would kill me if they saw daylight. And now, inside it — a sealed file. Addressed to me. In handwriting I would know in the dark, on my deathbed, at the bottom of the sea.

I sat with it a long time. I didn't open it.

I know how that sounds. But let me say what it meant. Clara had reached forward through time and put her hand on my shoulder. She had touched the one place I believed was untouchable — me, who spends his life walking through other people's walls, who tells himself no door can hold him. She had pressed her fingerprints into that place and left them there to say one thing: *I knew you'd come this far.*

A man can carry that. A man can also be destroyed by it. I felt both at once. Sitting in the dark, face blue from the screens, coffee going cold and skinning over in the cup. Three monitors burning in a room with no other light. The only kind of light I've ever known how to live in.

I didn't open the file because I was afraid of what it would demand from me.

I was right to be afraid.

So I did what I always do when I'm afraid. I went looking for the man on the other side.

---

By dawn I had changed animals.

I was no longer the man who missed her. I was the man who hunts. Those aren't the same creature. The first one cries in the shower. The second one doesn't blink for hours, forgets to eat, loses track of where the screen ends and the world begins. Both of them were me that week in Brussels. In a rented apartment under a name that wasn't mine, paid for without a trace. They took turns at the wheel.

I made tea I didn't drink. I opened the windows to the grey Belgian sky because the room had started to smell like a man coming apart. Cold sweat and warm electronics. The smell of long nights spent watching machines that purr like sleeping animals.

Down on the street a baker raised his shutter. The scrape of metal nearly broke me. The thought that somewhere people were buttering bread. While Clara was in a place I couldn't see.

Then I sat down. And I asked the thing I had built to do what it does better than anything.

I asked it to draw me a face.

---

What it does is the closest thing to magic in my life. Something you feel more than explain.

You start from nothing. A network that hides children behind charity. That moves them in trucks carrying rice and blankets to the broken places of the world. *Phantom Mercy.* Clara's name for it. She had a gift for naming what killed you — once named, you couldn't look away. In my world, naming a beast is the beginning of owning it. We burn through handles like skin. But a beast's true name only gets given once.

Nothing was what I had. No name. No face. Not a handle dragging on any forum. Not a rumor on the channels where people like us speak in low voices. Nothing but damage. The shape of places they'd moved through. Like the hollow a body leaves in a bed.

So you let the system listen. Not to words. To the *way* things are done.

Because here is the secret. It took me twenty years, a thousand sleepless nights, and one broken heart to learn it. Every man who builds in the dark leaves a signature. The way a painter leaves brushstrokes. You can burn your handle. Take ten new ones. Disappear from one forum and reappear somewhere else. You can change your name a thousand times. You cannot change your hands.

I gave it everything Clara had left me. And everything the dark was willing to give up — the scraps you collect from the lawless corners of the net, old stolen data caches sleeping for years on forums no one dares to open anymore.

And I let it search for the pattern underneath the brushstrokes.

By the time the baker downstairs sold his first baguette, the shapeless cloud of *Phantom Mercy* had taken form. Like a photo in a developing tray. When the grey water suddenly remembers it was a face.

One man. A single man, at the heart of the dark machine. Not the boss — I already knew that. There is always someone fatter and better protected above the capable one. But the capable one. The builder. The hands.

I began to see him.

---

I want to be honest about what I felt. Everything else rests on it.

I felt admiration.

God forgive me. The warm, sick admiration of one craftsman studying another's work. He was *good.* The way the network breathed — patient, layered, beautiful the way a trap is beautiful — was the work of someone who loved the craft the way I love it. Who had stayed up the way I stayed up, in the glow of a screen, at the hour when the world sleeps and only the insomniacs of the dark are left. Who probably forgot to eat too, like me.

There is a loneliness in being very good at something almost no one understands. You spend your life in rooms no one else can enter. Behind airlocks and false doors. Building cathedrals no one will ever see. And you end up starving. *Starving* for one other person who could stand there beside you and say: *yes. I see what you did. It was hard. You made it look easy.*

I had felt that hunger my whole life. And looking at the work of the man at the heart of *Phantom Mercy*, I felt the obscene pull of recognition. Like calling to like. A handshake across a black river.

That frightened me more than the network itself.

Because if he was that good — and he was — he hadn't stumbled into what he did to children. He had *engineered* it. With care. With pride. The same care I had once put into the wrong things. Those years I don't speak about to anyone, when I was younger and angrier. When I forced open a door just to prove I could. Just to watch someone's eyes go wide.

That first-time rush. A door that was supposed to stay closed forever suddenly giving way under your fingers. You are suddenly master of the whole house, and no one knows it. That marks you. It takes years to understand that rush is a poison.

Clara never knew that man. Or rather — she did. She knew exactly who I had been. She had gone through my entire history before she ever let me offer her a drink. Every stolen archive where my name surfaced. Every legend. Every truth and every lie told about me in clandestine back rooms. In a hotel bar, in a desert city, two and a half years ago and a whole lifetime. She knew what my hands had done. And she sat across from me anyway. She said: *"You build now."* Like a fact. Not a hope. Like she had decided it on my behalf.

That is what it means to be loved by someone who knows your worst. It isn't comfort. It's a sentence. You have to live up to the version of yourself they refused to stop seeing.

---

I gave him a name. The system couldn't give me the real one, and I couldn't keep calling him *the capable one* without losing my mind. In my world you always have a name for the enemy before you have a face. That's what you hunt first. A handle. A shadow. A rumor in the hidden channels.

I called him the Surgeon.

It came on its own, watching the way he cut. He didn't smash. He didn't take a sledgehammer to a wall the way amateurs do, the way the loud kids make noise just to exist. The way I had, as a stupid kid. He opened things along their seams. He found the one point that, pulled, made the whole garment fall apart. The clean gesture. Precise. He pulled it, slipped through, and left everything intact behind him.

No disorder. No blood on the floor. No fingerprint, no tripped alarm. Nothing blinking in the log where the machine records who comes and goes. Just a door that had quietly decided to be open.

That is a very particular kind of man. That patience. That contempt for noise. I knew it. I had spent years trying to become it, without ever quite possessing that stillness.

The Surgeon possessed that stillness.

And there was something else. It took me a moment to name it. It lived just under the skin of the work, like a temperature. Cruelty. Not the hot kind — not the man who hurts because he is angry, the one you can almost forgive because at least he *feels* something. The cold kind. The kind that hurts the way a tide goes out. Without even noticing you were there.

Everything the Surgeon built treated the children the way a butcher treats meat. Logistics. Weight. A number that must travel from one point to another without damage. A line in a spreadsheet. Cargo moved between relay points. There was no hatred in it. That was the horror. Hatred would have been human. This was *housekeeping.*

I have done bad things. Let that be clear. This is a confession, and a confession with the worst parts removed is just vanity. I have hurt people for money. For pride. But I never stopped seeing them as human beings. Never. My shame is the shame of a man who knew, every time, exactly what he was doing to someone real.

The Surgeon carried no shame. For him there was no one to apologize to. That is the line. That is the whole line, between the man I had been and the man on the other side of the river. Not talent. The talent was identical. The line was: who you allow yourself to stop seeing.

I leaned back from the screen. My heart was hammering like I'd climbed six flights of stairs. And I said out loud, to an empty apartment in a city that didn't know my real name:

"There you are."

And somewhere — I'm certain of it now, even though I had no proof then — somewhere on the other side of the black river, in his own room, in front of his own cold tea and his own burning screens, the Surgeon felt my gaze. And he went completely still.

---

I called Yuri.

Yuri is in Kyiv. He is the most paranoid man I have ever loved, and that is saying something, because I collect them. The trade demands it. In the world of those who live in the gaps, paranoia is not a disease. It is hygiene. It is what keeps you alive.

He picked up on the first ring, on the encrypted line that only the two of us know how to breathe life into. That meant he hadn't slept either. That the war was loud that night. That he was sitting in the dark listening to drones the way other people listen to rain.

"You look like a corpse," he said. No video. Never video with him. A rule you don't discuss. He just knows.

"I found a man.

— In the thing that took her?" A pause. The Yuri pause, where he decides how worried to be.

"Inside it. The one who built it." I rubbed my face. "Yuri, he's — he's like me.

— Everyone you hunt is like you. That's why you catch them. You're not a detective, Delta. You're a confession."

I laughed. Because it was true. And because if I didn't laugh I would do something worse. Yuri is the only one who says to my face what I am. The others are gentler. Ifeoma prays for me down in Lagos, between two servers she keeps alive with her bare hands. Basti pours me a drink in Marseille and tells me a crude joke. Noa goes quiet on the line and lets the silence say *I know, I know, I know.* Yuri names it.

"Send me what you have. I'll look for him in the noise. If he's that good he's careful. And careful men leave a different kind of trace. No mistakes. No slips, no missteps, nothing sticking out. But the *absence* of mistakes also has a shape. A hole too clean in the noise is already a signature." A drone passed somewhere behind his window. He didn't flinch. "Delta. Listen to me.

— I'm listening.

— You sound like you want to admire him."

I didn't answer.

"Don't." Quietly. "Admire him later. In court. When he's in a cage and the children are home. Admire him from the *outside.* The second you climb inside his head to taste the art, you stop seeing the children." A pause. "Men like him make you fall in love with the gesture. So you forget the cruelty. That's how he wins. Everything Clara worked on was about that. Don't prove her wrong by letting it happen to you."

I closed my eyes. In a hotel bar two and a half years ago, Clara had said almost the same thing. A glass of bad red wine in her hand, the desert dark outside the window. She was talking about her work. She was talking about *everything.* *"You can map any monster if you understand what they're protecting. But the moment you start protecting it too, you've joined them. And you'll never feel the door close behind you."*

"Yeah," I said to Yuri. "Yeah. I hear you.

— Eat something." And he hung up. Yuri's love sounds like orders.

I sat with the dead line for a moment. That's a thing about my family. The scattered, stolen, chosen family I have built across a dozen broken cities. A server keeper here. A repentant lockpick there. All people who, one day, chose to disappear and survive in the gaps of the world. They hang up the second the love has been delivered. Like a package. Like they're embarrassed to have signed for it.

Ifeoma in Lagos does it. Basti in Marseille does it. Even Noa — who answers when no one else would, who has every reason to let it ring forever and doesn't — even Noa hangs up first. Always. So I won't hear what's in her voice if she stays on one second longer.

Over the years I had dragged all of them into rooms they never should have entered. That was what was eating me underneath everything else, that whole grey week. To find Clara, I was going to have to light them up again. All of them. One by one. Reawaken the network I thought I had shut down for good. And ask them to walk toward the dark with me.

And the man waiting in that dark, I was beginning to understand, was not a stranger. He knew the trade I had taught. Which meant that somewhere at the end of the road, he might know the family I had taught it to.

I pushed the thought away. There is always a thought a man pushes away the night before he finds out it was the true one.

---

I didn't eat. Obviously.

I went back to the face.

Because Yuri was right. And because I'm a stubborn idiot, I had decided to do the one thing that would either save me or destroy me. If the Surgeon had a signature — and he did, every craftsman does — I could match it. Compare his hands against the great library of hands. The silent museum of brushstrokes the dark preserves without meaning to. Find his cousins. His teachers. The ones he had learned from and the ones he had burned. Build him a family tree of cruelty. Find out where a man learns to open children like envelopes.

I let the system compare. That is its true gift. Not finding. *Recognizing.* Holding two pieces of work side by side and saying, in its quiet way: *these came from the same room.* Same hands. Same school.

I leaned back. I let it run. Outside, Brussels woke up for real. Trams. A dog. An argument in two languages. A church bell counting a number I lost track of. I thought about Clara's handwriting in my vault. About the file I still hadn't opened. Then I thought about nothing. The hum of the machine. The burn behind my eyes, which had stopped being tiredness and was now just the air I breathed.

And then the system found his cousin.

---

I knew before I read it. The way you know the phone is about to ring. The room changed temperature. The hum changed pitch — or I imagined it did. I'll never be certain. In my memory it always changes pitch.

The Surgeon's signature — that clean, patient, beautiful, monstrous way of opening a thing along its seam — had a match.

One match. Strong. The strongest the system had ever found. It told me this in its bloodless way, as if it were proud. As if it had retrieved a bird and laid it at my feet.

I almost smiled. *A teacher,* I thought. *A mentor. The school he came from.* This was the thread Clara wanted me to pull. This was the seam. I leaned in to read whose hands had taught the Surgeon his terrible, elegant trade — leaned in the way a man leans over a well to see his own —

I read it twice.

The first time the words didn't go in. They sat on the surface of me like oil on water and refused to mean anything, because what they meant could not be true, and my mind, with a small mercy, gave me one second to not understand.

The second time, they went in.

The hands the Surgeon had learned from. The signature his work was a child of. The seam-splitting, door-opening, beautiful and terrible craft at the black heart of *Phantom Mercy* — the system had found its source, its origin, and laid it at my feet like a gift.

It was mine.

The Surgeon's fingerprint matched something I had built myself, years ago, in a life I had buried.
