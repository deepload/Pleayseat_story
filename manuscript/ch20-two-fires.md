# Chapter 20: Two Fires

The heartbeat moved faster than any human could run, and for nine seconds I had let myself believe she was free.

I want to be honest about those nine seconds, because they were the cruellest mercy of the whole week. For nine seconds the silence broke and her heartbeat came back and then it *ran* — across the map, across the dead north of that city, faster than legs, faster than fear, faster than anything with a pulse should move. And the animal part of me, the part that had spent six days starving, leapt up and said *she's out, she's running, she made it.* I was already half out of my chair. I was already reaching for my coat.

Then the cold part of me — the part Clara built, the part that had kept me alive every night that the warm part wanted to get me killed — caught the warm part by the collar and held it still and made it look again.

People don't move that fast. Hope does. A heartbeat doesn't sprint across a city in nine seconds. But a marker does, if a hand is carrying it. A signal does, if someone has picked it up and is walking it somewhere to make a man like me run after it.

It wasn't Clara running.

It was Clara's heartbeat being *carried.*

And the moment I understood that — the moment the warm part sat back down, sick and ashamed of its own joy — I understood the other thing too, the thing I'd been refusing to look at since the marker first lied to me. The Surgeon wasn't trying to lose me anymore. He'd stopped running. He was *steering* me. He had her pulse in his hand like a lure on a string, and he was dragging it across the water, watching to see which way the hungry thing would turn.

So I made myself do the unbearable thing.

I let it run.

I sat there and I watched the heartbeat I'd bled for go skidding off into the dark and I did not follow it. I let the lure swing. Because the only way to beat a man who knows exactly how you think is to do, just once, the thing he's certain you can't.

I let her go, to keep her.

---

Then the second fire started, and the night went from impossible to something worse.

It came through Ifeoma, the way the bad things always did, because Ifeoma watches the bones of everything and the bones are where the cracks show first. She came onto the wire from Lagos and her voice was wrong — not afraid, Ifeoma is never simply afraid, but *braced*, the way you brace when the floor's already tilting and you're deciding which thing to grab.

"We have a problem," she said. "A real one. Right now."

"I know. He moved her marker again, he's pulling me—"

"No. Listen to me." A pause, and in the pause I heard her decide to frighten me, which Ifeoma never does unless it's earned. "Somebody is taking the whole thing apart from the inside. Not the prosecutor. Not the building. *Us.* The room. The thing you built. Someone has their hands inside our own walls and they are pulling out the pins one at a time, and if they finish before we move, the rescue dies in its bed and nobody ever has to fire a shot."

The floor I'd been standing on for six days quietly informed me it was not, in fact, a floor.

Let me put this in human words, because it's the whole of the chapter and I won't fog it. Everything we'd built — the case, the map, the plan, the lawful hammer waiting to fall at the lawful hour — all of it stood on one quiet thing. The thing I'd made. The system. It was the spine of all of it: it held the proof that couldn't be argued with, it held the map of the dead hospital drawn so true I could walk it blind, it held the threads of two thousand eight hundred and forty-seven movements of dirty money sealed so tight no lawyer on earth could pry them loose. Pull the spine, and the body falls. And someone — somewhere out in the dark, with patient bloodless hands I'd have known anywhere — had gotten a grip on the spine and started, very gently, to break it.

It was the Surgeon. Of course it was the Surgeon. The marker running across the map wasn't the trap. The marker was the *distraction.* He'd lit a fire in the front of the house to send me running for water, and while I ran he'd slipped in the back to burn the thing that mattered.

I'd taught a whole generation of frightened companies this exact trick, in the life I'm trying to leave. *You don't beat a defender by being stronger. You beat him by giving him two emergencies and a single pair of hands.*

Now someone was doing it to me. With the woman I loved as the kindling.

---

"Talk to me," I said. "How bad."

"Bad in a smart way," Ifeoma said, and I could hear her already moving, already inside it, her fingers somewhere I couldn't see, fighting. "Not loud. If it was loud I'd just slam the doors. It's quiet. It's *clever.* He's not smashing anything — he's untying it. Thread by thread. And here's the part you're not going to like." She stopped. The generator coughed behind her, that homely cough, absurd against the size of what she was about to say. "I can't hold all of it. There's too much and there's one of me and the clock's already running. I can save the case, or I can save the rescue. I cannot do both. The hands aren't there. The *time* isn't there. You built one beautiful thing and he's set it on fire in two places at once and you have to tell me, right now, which fire we let burn."

There it was.

The cruelty of it was so pure, so surgical, that for a second I almost admired it the way you admire a knife. He hadn't beaten me. He'd done something worse. He'd handed me the one choice no man should have to make and stepped back to watch which half of myself I'd kill.

The case, or the rescue.

The proof, or the door.

The thing Clara had bled her whole brave life to build — the unbreakable record, the truth heavy enough that no power on earth could lift it, the thing that meant the children stayed home and the twelve faces went somewhere they could never reach a child again — or the thing that meant Clara herself walked out of that dead building alive tonight.

You understand the trap inside the trap. If I saved the rescue, I'd get her out and the case would crumble — and a year from now, in a courtroom, twelve men would stand up and brush the truth off their shoulders like lint, and walk into the sunlight, and start again, and Clara would look at me across whatever life we'd salvaged and know, without my ever saying it, that I'd thrown away the only thing she'd ever asked the world for. And if I saved the case, I'd have my unbreakable, beautiful, sealed yes — and no door for her to come home through.

He'd built me a choice where every answer was a betrayal. That was his signature. I'd seen it once before, years ago, in a thing he made, and it had made me sick then with how elegant it was, how *cold*, and I'd told myself the man who built that was someone I'd never have to meet.

I was meeting him now. Through the wall, in the dark, with his hands inside my house.

---

"Delta." Ifeoma's voice, gentle now, because she knew what she was asking. "I need an answer. Every second you spend deciding, he wins a thread. I'm not going to choose this for you. It's not mine to choose. But I need it *now.*"

I closed my eyes.

And here is the thing I have never told anyone, the thing I'm only telling you because this whole book is a confession and a confession that flinches is just a brag. In that second, with the woman I love being carried across a map like bait and the truth she'd died for coming apart in my friend's exhausted hands, the old man woke up in me. The one who breaks things. He woke up and he was *calm* and he was *certain* and he said, very reasonably, the most seductive sentence I have ever heard:

*You don't have to choose. Burn it all down.*

Stop saving. Stop building. Forget the case, forget the clean way, forget the courtroom a year from now. Go dirty. Go now. Take everything I'd sworn off and pour it onto these people like petrol and drop a match and walk into that dead hospital through the ash and carry her out over the bodies of the men who took her, and let the truth burn with everything else, because what is *truth* worth against her actual breathing body, against the actual warmth of her, against three years of wanting and six days of dying a little every hour. Who is *evidence* for. Evidence is for people who still have something left to lose.

It would have felt so good. That's the part nobody warns you about evil — that it doesn't feel like evil from the inside. It feels like *relief.*

And then I heard her voice. Not on the wire. In the memory, where she lives now, the hotel bar that smelled of cheap whisky, the glass she turned without looking at me. *Anyone can destroy. It takes nothing. A child can knock down a tower. The hard thing — the only thing that ever actually saves anyone — is to build something so true it can't be knocked down.*

She wasn't asking me to choose between her and the case.

She was asking me to be the kind of man who could find a third thing.

---

"Ifeoma," I said, and my voice came out steadier than I had any right to. "How long can you hold the rescue alone if you let the case go?"

"If I drop the case and put everything into the rescue?" A breath. "Long enough. Maybe. If nothing else breaks. But you'd be throwing away the—"

"I'm not throwing it away." I was already moving now, already inside it, the cold part of me running the room while the warm part screamed. "The case doesn't have to live in *our* house tonight. It just has to live somewhere he can't reach it before dawn. Somewhere with its own walls. Somewhere with a flag on it."

The line went very quiet.

"Marchetti," Ifeoma breathed.

"Marchetti." Because the prosecutor was bought, the one clean door was rotten, but Marchetti was something else — a hard weary man with a real flag and real walls and a vault of his own, the lawful kind, the kind I couldn't build because I'm a ghost with warrants in three countries. I couldn't keep the case safe. But I could *hand it to him*, sealed and whole, push the entire unbreakable truth out of my burning house and into his — and then turn, with both my hands finally free, and throw everything I had left into getting Clara out. Let the Surgeon burn an empty room. Let him pull the pins out of a house I'd already evacuated. The case would be gone — out of my reach, out of his reach, behind a flag.

It would cost me. I want to be clear about that. The moment I pushed the case into Marchetti's hands, I lost control of it. I was trusting the truth Clara bled for to a man I trusted *exactly as far as the next reversal* — a man who, three jurisdictions ago, I'd promised myself I'd never need. I was handing my redemption to a stranger and praying he was the man Clara thought he was.

But it was the third thing. It was the door out of a room with no doors. Save the rescue with my own hands. Save the case by giving it away. Refuse the choice the monster built for me.

"Do it," Ifeoma said, and there was something fierce in her now, something that wasn't fear at all. "Push it to Marchetti. I'll hold the rescue. *Go.*"

I went.

---

It is a strange thing, to perform a rescue and a salvage in the same ten minutes, with the same shaking hands, while the heartbeat of the woman you love drags itself across a map as bait. I won't tell you how it was done. I told you a long time ago I wouldn't, and I'm not breaking that on the night it cost the most. But I'll tell you what it *felt* like, because the feeling is the only true thing I have to give you.

It felt like standing in a burning house with a child in one arm and a letter in the other — a letter that proves who set the fire — and knowing you can only carry one of them through the door. And finding, at the last second, a window. Pushing the letter out the window into the hands of a man on the lawn you're not even sure you trust. And then turning, with the child, toward the flames.

I pushed the case out the window.

Marchetti caught it. I felt it land — felt the whole sealed weight of it leave my house and settle behind his flag, behind his walls, where the Surgeon's patient hands couldn't follow. The truth was safe. Out of my reach, but safe. The children would still go home. The twelve faces would still fall, in daylight, forever.

And I turned, with both hands free at last, and threw everything that was left of me into the rescue. Into the dead hospital. Into the way in I'd drawn so true I could walk it blind. Into getting her out before the heartbeat-bait ran off the edge of the world and took my heart with it.

For one clean minute, it worked. It *worked.* I had refused the monster's choice. I had found the third thing. The case lived; the rescue lived; I was holding both fires at arm's length and not letting either one have her.

I should have known there's no such thing as a choice with no cost. He'd built it too well for that. When you save an operation by sacrificing one piece of it, you don't get to choose, in the chaos, *which* piece pays.

Because to push the case out the window, to free my hands for her, I'd had to let one part of the house go dark. One thread. One small piece of the whole, dropped in the rush, abandoned to the fire so the rest could live. I didn't even feel it go. You never feel the one that matters until later.

It was the eyes on the south road. The piece that watched the way out. The one I'd let burn so the rest could live.

And that was exactly where Basti was standing.
